Indo-Islamic architecture PREMAINS

Overview

Indo-Islamic architecture emerged from the fusion of Central Asian–Persian building traditions with indigenous Indian craftsmanship after the establishment of the Delhi Sultanate (1206). It introduced the true arch, true dome, minaret and geometric ornamentation to India, evolving through distinct Sultanate, Provincial Sultanate and Mughal phases. MPSC asks about key monuments, their patrons, and stylistic features in both Prelims and Mains.

In-Depth

Defining Features of Indo-Islamic Style

Indo-Islamic architecture broke from earlier temple traditions in three core ways:

  1. Structural — true arch (keystone) and true dome replaced the corbelled arch and post-and-beam of Hindu-Buddhist structures.
  2. Decorative — geometric (arabesque), floral, and calligraphic ornamentation replaced figural sculpture (Islamic prohibition).
  3. Material — extensive use of lime mortar (compared to dry-masonry of temples) and reuse of Hindu–Jain temple material (spolia) in early Sultanate buildings.

Early Sultanate Phase (1206–1320) — Slave & Khilji

  • Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque, Delhi (1193) — oldest surviving mosque in India; built by Qutb-ud-din Aibak using material from 27 Hindu/Jain temples. Features the famous Iron Pillar (Gupta era, 4th c. CE).
  • Qutb Minar, Delhi — begun by Aibak, completed by Iltutmish; 5 storeys, 72.5 m; UNESCO World Heritage Site. Each storey uses alternating angular/round fluting.
  • Iltutmish's Tomb (1235) — first Islamic tomb in India; geometric star-pattern ceiling.
  • Alai Darwaza (1311) by Alauddin Khilji — first true dome in Indian architecture; red sandstone with white marble inlay; honeycomb squinch (pendentive) technique.

Tughlaq Phase (1320–1414)

Characterised by austerity: massive sloped walls (batter), rubble masonry, grey sandstone, absence of elaborate ornamentation.

  • Tughlaqabad Fort — Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq's fortified city; first garrison-capital.
  • Firuzabad — Firuz Shah Tughlaq's new city; Ashokan pillars transported here.
  • Khirki Mosque and Begumpur Mosque — major congregational mosques of the Tughlaq period.

Lodi and Sayyid Phase (1414–1526)

  • Octagonal tomb popularised: Lodi Gardens, Delhi contains tombs of Sayyid and Lodi rulers; octagonal plan with high drum allows better dome proportions.
  • Moth ki Masjid (Ibrahim Lodi era) — refined ornamentation.

Transition to Mughal Style

The Lodi→Mughal transition brought two key innovations: use of red sandstone as primary material (Sher Shah Suri's Qila-i-Kuhna Mosque, 1541) and introduction of double dome (outer shell for profile, inner for interior space). The Mughal period perfected these into marble inlay and charbagh gardens.

Maharashtra Connection

Bijapur (present-day Karnataka but formerly within the Deccan Sultanate sphere) and Bidar are architecturally significant for the Deccan style. Within Maharashtra, the Daulatabad Fort (Devgiri) shows Islamic additions to a Yadava base — Chand Minar (1445) at Daulatabad is a notable minaret. Aurangabad caves and Bibi Ka Maqbara (1660) — the "Taj of the Deccan" — are major examples of Mughal-Deccani synthesis within present Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • Oldest mosque in India: Quwwat-ul-Islam (1193) by Qutb-ud-din Aibak.
  • Qutb Minar height: 72.5 m, completed by Iltutmish.
  • First true dome in India: Alai Darwaza (1311), Alauddin Khilji.
  • Largest pre-Mughal dome: Gol Gumbaz, Bijapur (1656) — diameter 44 m; famous whispering gallery.
  • Tughlaq style hallmark: sloped/battered walls, grey stone, austerity.
  • Provincial style with jali screens: Gujarat Sultanate.
  • Double dome introduced by: Mughal period (first major use at Humayun's Tomb, 1570).
  • Chand Minar at Daulatabad (Maharashtra) built 1445.

Memory Aids

  • Sultanate arch progression: "Corbelled → Alai Darwaza (true arch, 1311) → Double dome (Humayun)".
  • Qutb Minar = Q = Qutb-ud-din started, Iltutmish finished.
  • Gol Gumbaz = "Gol = round, biggest dome, Bijapur, Adil Shahi".
  • Tughlaq = "Tough and grey" (austerity, grey stone).

Exam Traps

  • Qutb Minar was started by Aibak but completed by Iltutmish — questions often ask who "built" it; both names appear in options.
  • Alai Darwaza (1311) ≠ Alai Minar — Alauddin also began a much taller second minaret (Alai Minar) but it was never completed.
  • Gol Gumbaz is in Bijapur (Karnataka), not Aurangabad — don't confuse with Bibi Ka Maqbara.
  • The Iron Pillar in the Quwwat-ul-Islam complex is Gupta period, not Sultanate — it predates the mosque by ~800 years.

Questions

  1. [Expected] Describe the chief features of Indo-Islamic architecture and explain how it differed from earlier Indian architectural traditions. (~200 words)

  3. [Expected] What role did provincial Sultanates play in diversifying Indo-Islamic architectural styles?
