Buddhist art — Gandhara, Mathura, Amaravati schools PREMAINS

Overview

Buddhist art in India evolved through distinct phases and schools, reflecting the spread of Buddhism from the Mauryan period onward. The three major sculptural schools — Gandhara, Mathura, and Amaravati — represent different artistic traditions that shaped the depiction of the Buddha. Understanding the differences between these schools (Greek influence, indigenous tradition, limestone style) is an MPSC staple. This topic also covers the Mahayana vs Theravada art split (anthropomorphic Buddha vs aniconic symbols).

In-Depth

Early Buddhist Art: Aniconic Period (3rd–1st c. BCE)

  • Early Buddhist art did not depict the Buddha in human form (aniconic tradition).
  • Buddha represented by symbols: Bodhi tree (Enlightenment), Dhamma Chakra (wheel = First Sermon, Sarnath), Footprints (Paduka), Empty Throne, Elephant (dream of Maya at birth), Horse Kanthaka (departure).
  • Examples: Sanchi toranas, Bharhut railings.

Anthropomorphic Phase (1st c. CE onward)

With Kushana patronage (Gandhara and Mathura schools), the Buddha began to be depicted in human form.

Gupta Period Buddhist Art (4th–6th c. CE)

  • Synthesis: Gandhara and Mathura styles merged during Gupta period into a refined, idealized classical form.
  • Sarnath School (Gupta): finest expression — transparent garments, meditative posture, serene faces, refined usnisha; no heavy drapery.
  • Ajanta murals (Gupta and post-Gupta): narrative paintings; life of Buddha; Jataka stories; Cave 1 (Padmapani Bodhisattva) = peak of Gupta Buddhist art.

Nalanda School (Pala Period, 8th–12th c. CE)

  • Bronze casting: Pala dynasty (Bengal/Bihar) produced exquisite bronze Buddhist sculptures.
  • Nalanda University (Bihar): major Buddhist learning centre.
  • Influence spread to Southeast Asia (Cambodia, Thailand, Indonesia — Angkor Wat, Borobudur have Nalanda-style influence).

Maharashtra Connection

Maharashtra has the richest Buddhist art heritage in India:

  • Ajanta Caves (Aurangabad district): 30 rock-cut caves; paintings represent peak Buddhist mural art; discovered by John Smith 1819; UNESCO WHS 1983. Cave 1 has the famous Padmapani Bodhisattva — greatest example of Gupta Buddhist painting.
  • Ellora Caves (Aurangabad district): Cave 1–12 are Buddhist (Mahayana); UNESCO WHS 1983. Cave 5 = Maharwada (largest Buddhist cave); Cave 10 = Vishwakarma/Carpenter's Cave (finest Mahayana chaitya; Buddha in dharmachakra mudra above the stupa).
  • Karla and Bhaja Caves (Lonavala area, Pune district): among the oldest chaitya halls in India (~2nd–1st c. BCE); Karla has the largest single-nave chaitya hall.
  • Kanheri Caves (Mumbai, Sanjay Gandhi National Park): ~109 caves; Buddhist monastic complex; 1st c. BCE–11th c. CE.
  • Nashik Caves (Nashik): Buddhist rock-cut caves; early Hinayana.
  • Maharashtra was historically part of the Deccan Buddhist circuit — Dr. B.R. Ambedkar converted to Buddhism in Nagpur on October 14, 1956 (Dhamma Diksha); this gave Buddhism a modern revival centre in Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • Gandhara: Greco-Roman influence; grey schist; NW India (Pakistan); Kushana.
  • Mathura: indigenous; red sandstone; UP; Kushana.
  • Amaravati: white limestone; AP; Satavahana.
  • Aniconic phase: Buddha = symbols (bodhi tree, wheel, footprints).
  • Bhumisparsha mudra: most depicted; Enlightenment (earth-touching).
  • Ajanta: 30 caves; paintings; Gupta Buddhist art peak; UNESCO 1983.
  • Ambedkar conversion: Nagpur; October 14, 1956.

Exam Traps

  • Gandhara school ≠ Indian indigenous — it is Hellenistic (Greek-Roman); Mathura is the indigenous school.
  • Amaravati is in Andhra Pradesh (Guntur district), NOT in Amravati city (Maharashtra) — two different places.
  • Bhumisparsha mudra is the most commonly depicted Buddha image — "earth-touching = Enlightenment."
  • Aniconic period came FIRST (symbols); anthropomorphic Buddha came LATER (Kushana period).
