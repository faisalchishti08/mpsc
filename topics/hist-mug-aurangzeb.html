Aurangzeb — Deccan policy, decline PREMAINS

Overview

Aurangzeb (1618–1707, r. 1658–1707) was the sixth and last significant Mughal emperor, whose 49-year reign saw both the territorial zenith and the beginning of irreversible decline of the Mughal Empire. His orthodox Islamic policies reversed Akbar's syncretic approach; his 25-year Deccan campaign bankrupted the empire and never subdued the Marathas. Extremely high MPSC frequency — both Prelims (policies, jizya, Deccan) and Mains (Deccan policy, causes of decline).

In-Depth

Rise to Power (War of Succession 1657–58)

Shah Jahan fell ill in 1657; his four sons fought a brutal succession war:

  • Dara Shikoh (liberal, eldest, Shah Jahan's favourite) — defeated at Samugarh (1658) near Agra; captured and executed 1659.
  • Shah Shuja — governor of Bengal; fled to Arakan (Burma).
  • Murad Bakhsh — allied with Aurangzeb, then betrayed and imprisoned.
  • Aurangzeb won decisively; imprisoned Shah Jahan at Agra Fort (1658) where Shah Jahan died in 1666.

Deccan Policy — The Fatal Campaign

The Deccan (1681–1707) — 25+ years of war in the Deccan against the Marathas and Deccan Sultanates — defined and doomed Aurangzeb's reign:

Phase 1 (Bijapur and Golkonda, 1686–87): Aurangzeb annexed Bijapur (1686) and Golkonda (1687) — ending the two most powerful Deccan Sultanates.

Phase 2 (Maratha Guerrilla War): Aurangzeb captured Sambhaji (Shivaji's son) at Sangameswar (1689), tortured and executed him. But Maratha resistance continued under Rajaram and Tarabai with guerrilla tactics (ganimi kava).

  • Aurangzeb took Satara fort and other strongholds but could never pacify the Deccan.
  • Maratha chiefs under Tarabai (1700–07) raided deep into Mughal territory.
  • Aurangzeb died at Ahmednagar (1707) — having spent his last 25 years in the Deccan, never returning to Delhi.

Drain on Empire: Standing army in the Deccan, jagir crisis (too many mansabdars for available jagirs), drain of treasury, and alienation of Rajputs and Marathas destabilised the empire beyond repair.

Revolts and Opposition

  • Jat revolt (Mathura, 1669–81): led by Gokula, then Rajaram (not Maratha Rajaram); opposed jizya and temple demolitions.
  • Satnamis revolt (Haryana, 1672): Satnami peasant sect; crushed.
  • Rajput revolt: Marwar (Jodhpur) king Jaswant Singh died (1678); Aurangzeb annexed Marwar; Durga Das Rathore led sustained resistance.
  • Sikh conflict: Guru Tegh Bahadur arrested and executed (1675); Guru Gobind Singh founded the Khalsa (1699) in direct response.
  • Shivaji (Deccan): see hist-mar-shivaji; Aurangzeb never defeated the Marathas permanently.

Significance and Legacy

  • Aurangzeb brought the Mughal Empire to its greatest geographical extent (from Kabul to Tanjore).
  • But the very conquests were unsustainable; he never returned to the north.
  • His policies are debated: orthodox Muslim ruler vs complex administrator; he patronised temple grants in some cases and employed large numbers of Hindus in his administration (more Hindus as % of mansabdars than under Akbar, per some historians).

Maharashtra Connection

Directly and centrally: Aurangzeb fought Shivaji's successors for 25 years in Maharashtra. He died at Ahmednagar (Maharashtra, 1707). Key Maharashtra-specific events:

  • Execution of Sambhaji (1689) at Tulapur near Pune.
  • Captured Raigad fort (1689) — the Maratha capital.
  • Tarabai led Maratha resistance from Satara (1700–07) — the queen-regent who kept resistance alive against the mightiest Mughal army.
  • Shahu released by Mughals (1707) after Aurangzeb's death — led to Maratha resurgence under Peshwa Bajirao.

Exam Traps

  • Aurangzeb did NOT ban painting entirely — he disapproved of court paintings but artists continued working; many paintings survive from his era.
  • More Hindus in mansabdari under Aurangzeb than Akbar (percentage-wise; recent historiography) — temple demolitions coexisted with Hindu appointments.
  • Aurangzeb's tomb is at Khuldabad (Aurangabad District, Maharashtra) — a simple earth-covered grave, not a grand mausoleum (he disapproved of lavish tombs).
  • Sambhaji ≠ Rajaram — Sambhaji (executed 1689) and Rajaram (brother, ruled after) are separate; Tarabai was Rajaram's wife.
