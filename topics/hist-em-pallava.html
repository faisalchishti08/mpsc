Pallavas and Cholas — administration, art PREMAINS

Overview

The Pallava dynasty (c. 275–897 CE) ruled from Kanchipuram (Tamil Nadu) and created the foundational monuments of South Indian temple architecture — the Shore Temple at Mahabalipuram (Mamallapuram) and the rathas (monolithic temples) — while also being fierce rivals of the Chalukyas of Badami. Their administrative innovations influenced later dynasties. The Imperial Cholas (850–1279 CE), their successors, are studied here for their iconic administration, mandalam system, and naval reach. MPSC tests Pallava monuments, the Chalukya-Pallava rivalry, and Chola administration.

Pallava Architecture — Mamallapuram and Kanchipuram

The Pallavas created a distinctive phase in South Indian temple architecture, evolving from rock-cut to structural:

Phase 1 — Rock-cut caves (Mahendravarman I style):

  • Single-storey cave temples with flat roofs; square pillars with lion base (yali); no tower.
  • Sites: Mandagapattu, Mahabalipuram, Tiruchirappalli.

Phase 2 — Monolithic Rathas (Narasimhavarman I / Mamalla style):

  • Seven Pagodas / Pancha Rathas (Mahabalipuram): Five monolithic temples carved from single granite outcrops, named after the Pandavas. They were never completed (no sanctum, no deity installed) — experimental architectural prototypes.
  • Each ratha demonstrates a different architectural form: Dharmaraja Ratha (largest; multi-storied Dravidian tower), Bhima Ratha (barrel-vaulted roof — Buddhist chaitya influence), Draupadi Ratha (simplest; grass-hut form origin), Arjuna Ratha (the model for later Dravidian towers), Nakula-Sahadeva Ratha (apsidal/elephant-back roof).

Phase 3 — Structural temples (Rajasimha style):

  • Shore Temple, Mahabalipuram (c. 700–728 CE): Narasimhavarman II. First free-standing structural stone temple in south India with a proper shikhara (tower). Faces the sea. UNESCO World Heritage Site (1984).
  • Kailasanatha Temple, Kanchipuram (c. 685–705 CE): greatest Pallava structural temple; became the model for the Virupaksha Temple at Pattadakal (Chalukya).

Administration

  • Mandalam: the Pallava kingdom was divided into mandalams (provinces), sub-divided into kottams (divisions), then nadus (sub-divisions), then urs (villages).
  • Village assemblies: Pallava-era inscriptions (especially at Uttaramerur, under later overlords) describe village assemblies (sabha for Brahmin villages, ur for others) with elected committees (variyam) handling finance, tanks, gardens. A sophisticated local self-government system.
  • The Uttaramerur inscription (c. 919–920 CE, under Chola Parantaka I) famously records the eligibility rules for election to village committees — evidence of proto-democratic local governance.

Imperial Cholas (850–1279 CE)

The Cholas who defeated the last Pallava founded an empire that reached its peak under Rajaraja I (985–1014 CE) and Rajendra I (1014–1044 CE):

Administration:

  • Mandalam → Valnadu → Nadu → Kurram/Kottam → Ur — layered administrative hierarchy.
  • Village-level self-government: Sabha (Brahmin villages) and Ur assemblies continued; sub-committees (variyam) specialised (e.g., pon-variyam for gold/finance).
  • Land revenue: assessed through cadastral surveys; recorded on copper plates.

Military and Naval Power:

  • Rajendra I sent a naval expedition c. 1025 CE to Southeast Asia — captured the Srivijaya empire's ports (modern Malaysia/Sumatra) to protect trade routes. This is the longest naval expedition in ancient Indian history.
  • Also sent troops to the Ganga (northern campaign) — took the title Gangaikondacholan ("Chola who conquered the Ganga").
  • Founded new capital Gangaikondacholapuram (Tamil Nadu).

Art and Architecture:

  • Brihadesvara Temple, Thanjavur (Rajaraja I, c. 1010 CE): largest Dravidian temple; 63-m tower (vimana); entire temple plan in proportion; UNESCO WHS (1987, Chola Temples group).
  • Nataraja (Dancing Shiva) bronze sculpture reached its artistic peak under the Cholas.
  • Bronze casting (cire perdue / lost-wax): Chola bronzes of Nataraja, Parvati, Uma-Maheshvara are among the finest metal sculptures in the world.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. However, Pallava architectural traditions directly influenced the Western Chalukyas (who bordered Maharashtra) — the Kailasanatha Temple at Kanchipuram was the model for the Virupaksha Temple at Pattadakal (Karnataka, near Maharashtra). Chola maritime power intersected with Deccan trade networks through the eastern Deccan corridor. The village assembly tradition documented at Uttaramerur influenced scholarship on ancient Indian local governance, relevant to Maharashtra's panchayat history comparisons.

Key Facts

  • Pallava capital: Kanchipuram (Tamil Nadu).
  • Vatapikonda = "Conqueror of Vatapi (Badami)": title of Narasimhavarman I.
  • Shore Temple: Mahabalipuram; built c. 700–728 CE; UNESCO WHS 1984.
  • Pancha Rathas: five monolithic temples; named after Pandavas; never completed.
  • Kailasanatha Temple: Kanchipuram; model for Pattadakal's Virupaksha Temple.
  • Rajendra I: naval expedition to Srivijaya c. 1025 CE — longest ancient Indian naval campaign.
  • Brihadesvara Temple: Thanjavur; by Rajaraja I; c. 1010 CE; UNESCO WHS 1987.
  • Uttaramerur inscription: documents Chola-era village assembly (sabha) election rules.

Exam Traps

  • Shore Temple UNESCO year is 1984 — Brihadesvara (Thanjavur) is 1987; both are Tamil Nadu but different years.
  • Narasimhavarman I (Mamalla) built the Pancha Rathas at Mamallapuram — NOT Narasimhavarman II (Rajasimha) who built the Shore Temple. Two different Narasimhavarmans.
  • Uttaramerur inscription is a Chola-era document about village assemblies — not Pallava; though the Pallava administrative tradition preceded it.
  • Chola Nataraja bronze: this is a Chola achievement, not Pallava — don't attribute Chola bronzes to Pallavas.
