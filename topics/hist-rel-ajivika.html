Ajivikas and other sects PREMAINS

Overview

The Ajivikas were a major heterodox (Nastika) philosophical movement of ancient India (~6th–5th c. BCE) founded by Makkhali Gosala, a contemporary of both Mahavira and the Buddha. Along with the Ajivikas, other heterodox schools — Charvakas/Lokayatas (materialists), Ajnanis (sceptics/agnostics) — challenged Brahmanical orthodoxy. These "nastika" schools (those who reject Vedic authority) form an important but often overlooked part of the intellectual ferment that produced Buddhism and Jainism. MPSC asks on the defining doctrines of each school, especially Niyati (fatalism) of the Ajivikas.

In-Depth

The Ajivikas

Founder: Makkhali Gosala (~484–468 BCE?). He is mentioned in Buddhist (Majjhima Nikaya, Samaññaphala Sutta) and Jain texts (Bhagavati Sutra) as a rival of both Buddha and Mahavira. He even lived with Mahavira for a time before breaking away.

Core Doctrine — Niyati (Absolute Determinism):

  • The central teaching of Ajivikas is Niyati — complete and absolute fatalism/determinism.
  • All events in the universe are pre-determined; nothing can change one's fate — no amount of effort, asceticism, or good deeds can accelerate or delay liberation.
  • The soul (Jiva) will automatically pass through exactly 84,00,000 (8.4 million) births in a fixed cycle, after which it will attain liberation regardless of actions.
  • This is the most radical form of determinism in ancient philosophy — eliminating free will entirely.

Contrast with contemporaries:

  • Mahavira (Jainism): Liberation through effort, austerity, and adherence to five vows.
  • Buddha (Buddhism): Liberation through the Eightfold Path and personal effort (no fixed fate).
  • Ajivikas: Liberation fixed, automatic, inevitable — effort is irrelevant.

Survival: Ajivikas declined by ~14th c. CE but had significant influence for centuries. Ashoka and his grandson Dasharatha gave cave donations to Ajivikas (Barabar Caves, Bihar; Nagarjuni Caves) — showing royal patronage even after the Mauryan Buddhist conversion.

Charvakas / Lokayata (Materialism)

  • India's only thoroughgoing materialist and atheist school.
  • Attributed to Brihaspati (legendary founder) and Charvaka (a teacher — source of the name).
  • Core doctrine: Only the material world exists; no soul, no rebirth, no karma, no God.
  • "While life remains, let a man live happily; let him feed on ghee even though he runs in debt" — attributed Charvaka saying.
  • Only valid knowledge source: direct perception (Pratyaksha) — inference and verbal testimony rejected.
  • Perception is the only pramana (valid means of knowledge); inference can be wrong; Vedas are authored by frauds and cheats.
  • All religious rituals are invented by priests for their own benefit.
  • Ethics: Pursue pleasure (Kama) and avoid pain; the "highest good" is sensory enjoyment.

Historical value: No original Charvaka texts survive — we know their views only through critics (Buddhist, Jain, Hindu refutations). This suggests systematic suppression or neglect of preservation.

Ajnanis / Sceptics (Sanjaya Belatthiputta)

  • Sanjaya Belatthiputta (~5th c. BCE) led this school of radical scepticism/agnosticism.
  • Refused to affirm any metaphysical claim: "Is there an afterlife? I don't know. Is there not? I don't know. Both? I don't know. Neither? I don't know."
  • Called Eel-wrigglers (Amaravikkhepika) in Buddhist texts — they wriggle out of any definite position.
  • Many of the Buddha's early disciples (including Sariputta and Moggallana) came from Sanjaya's group.

The Six Teachers (Chha Titthiya)

Buddhist texts record six major teachers contemporary with the Buddha, representing different heterodox views:

  1. Purana Kassapa: Amoralism — actions have no moral consequences (neither good nor bad karma).
  2. Makkhali Gosala: Ajivikas — Niyati (absolute determinism).
  3. Ajita Kesakambali: Charvakas — materialism; death ends all; no rebirth.
  4. Pakudha Kaccayana: Eternalism — seven eternal, uncreatable elements (earth, water, fire, air, pleasure, pain, soul).
  5. Nigantha Nataputta (= Mahavira): Jainism — karma, liberation through austerity.
  6. Sanjaya Belatthiputta: Agnosticism/scepticism.

Maharashtra Connection

No direct Maharashtra-specific connection for Ajivikas or Charvakas. However, Maharashtra's rationalist and social reform tradition — Phule, Ambedkar, Gopal Ganesh Agarkar (rationalist journalist, 1856–1895) — echoes the Charvaka spirit of questioning supernatural authority and Brahmanical monopoly. Agarkar's journal Sudharak explicitly promoted rationalism and rejected caste and religion's role in public life.

Key Facts

  • Ajivika founder: Makkhali Gosala.
  • Ajivika core: Niyati (absolute fatalism); 8.4 million births pre-fixed.
  • Ashoka gave donations to Ajivikas: Barabar Caves (Bihar).
  • Charvakas: Materialists; only perception (Pratyaksha) valid; no soul, no karma, no God.
  • Sanjaya Belatthiputta: Agnostic/sceptic; "eel-wriggler."
  • Six teachers include Makkhali Gosala, Ajita Kesakambali, Sanjaya, and Mahavira (Nigantha Nataputta).

Exam Traps

  • Ajivika ≠ Atheist: They believed in the soul (Jiva) and rebirth but denied free will — a subtle distinction from Charvakas who deny soul entirely.
  • Charvakas accept only Pratyaksha (perception) as valid — they reject inference (Anumana) unlike almost all other Indian schools.
  • Sanjaya ≠ Jainism/Buddhism — he was a sceptic who refused to commit to any position; he is listed separately.
  • Ashoka's Barabar Caves donations were to Ajivikas, NOT Buddhists — an important trap since Ashoka is associated with Buddhism.
