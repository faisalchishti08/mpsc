Tughlaq dynasty — Muhammad bin Tughlaq, Firoz Shah PREMAINS

Overview

The Tughlaq dynasty (1320–1414 CE) was the third and longest dynasty of the Delhi Sultanate. It produced two extraordinary but controversial rulers: Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq (founder) and his son Muhammad bin Tughlaq (1325–1351 CE) — famous for ambitious but often disastrous experiments — and Firoz Shah Tughlaq (1351–1388 CE) who attempted welfare-state reforms. The Tughlaq era also saw the Deccan's separation (Bahmani kingdom, 1347 CE) and the catastrophic Timur's invasion (1398 CE) that ended effective Tughlaq power. Maharashtra's medieval history connects directly through the Deccan connection.

In-Depth

Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq (1320–1325 CE)

Ghiyasuddin was a general of the last Khilji sultan who seized power:

  • Reintroduced the Iqta system properly after Alauddin's direct-pay modifications.
  • Revived the Deccan campaigns; reconquered Bengal.
  • Died under a collapsing pavilion (1325 CE) — possibly engineered by his son Muhammad, though the "fratricide" charge is disputed.
  • Built Tughlaqabad — the third city of Delhi — as a fort-city.

Muhammad bin Tughlaq (1325–1351 CE) — The Paradox

Muhammad bin Tughlaq (born Jauna Khan) was intellectually brilliant but administratively erratic — described by historian Ibn Battuta (Moroccan traveller who served in his court) as simultaneously merciful and cruel, learned and impractical:

Five Major "Experiments" (all controversial):

  1. Transfer of capital from Delhi to Devagiri (renamed Daulatabad) (c. 1327 CE):
  • Rationale: Daulatabad is geographically central; better to administer the south; safer from Mongol raids from the north.
  • Reality: Forced migration of entire Delhi population (~1327 CE) over 1,500 km; mass suffering, deaths; Delhi depopulated. Within years, abandoned — he shifted the capital back. Ibn Battuta found Delhi "empty" on arrival.
  1. Token currency experiment (1329–1332 CE):
  • Issued copper/brass coins at the value of gold and silver coins (token currency — like modern paper money, but without institutional backing).
  • Rationale: shortage of gold/silver; expand money supply.
  • Reality: No anti-forgery system; public flooded the market with forged brass coins; state treasury collapsed. Policy abandoned; government redeemed tokens for silver at enormous loss.
  1. Khurasan/Persia/Transoxiana campaign (c. 1329):
  • Raised an army of 370,000 to conquer Khurasan (Persia/Central Asia) — a wildly ambitious plan.
  • Army destroyed by famine and disease in the mountains; plan abandoned.
  1. Qarachil (Himalayan) campaign (1337–1338 CE):
  • Sent a large army into the Himalayan foothills (Qarachil, near Kangra/Kumaon).
  • Army almost entirely wiped out in mountain terrain.
  1. Taxation increase in the Doab (Gangetic plain) (c. 1326–1334 CE):
  • Raised revenue to fund campaigns; combined with a severe famine — caused mass peasant revolts; region depopulated.

Positive aspects: Muhammad was well-read (philosophy, logic, medicine), wrote Persian poetry, was personally courageous, and genuinely attempted revenue measurement (direct assessment rather than intermediaries). Ibn Battuta described him as the most generous of rulers and the most terrible in punishment.

Ibn Battuta (Rihla, c. 1354): Moroccan scholar-traveller who served as a qadi (judge) in Muhammad bin Tughlaq's Delhi court (1334–1342 CE); later sent as ambassador to China. His Rihla (travelogue) is the richest first-hand account of 14th-century India.

Firoz Shah Tughlaq (1351–1388 CE)

Firoz Shah reversed his predecessor's excesses and focused on welfare and consolidation:

  • Welfare works: dug 150 wells, 100 caravanserais, 30 reservoirs (bawlis/hauz), 5 canals (including the 200 km Western Yamuna Canal from Yamuna to Hissar — still in use) — unprecedented public works programme.
  • Tax policy: abolished all taxes not sanctioned by Islamic law (Sharia); reduced jizya (but enforced it on Brahmins who had previously been exempt under some previous sultans).
  • Hospitals (shifa-khanas) and a court of justice (diwan-i-khairat) for widows and orphans.
  • Patronised learning: built Firozabad (the 5th city of Delhi), repaired the Qutb Minar, transported Ashokan pillars from Topra and Meerut to Delhi (he could not read the inscriptions — Brahmi was not deciphered until 1837).
  • Negative: Reintroduced hereditary iqtas (son inheriting father's iqta — reduced central control); enforced religious orthodoxy strictly; increased jizya on Brahmins; persecuted Muslim "heretics."

Timur's Invasion (1398–1399 CE)

Timur (Tamerlane) invaded India in 1398–99 CE:

  • Crossed the Indus, sacked Delhi (December 1398) — massacred thousands of the city's inhabitants.
  • The Tughlaq Sultan Mahmud Tughlaq fled; Delhi was left in ruins.
  • Timur returned to Samarkand with enormous plunder and thousands of skilled artisans (used to build Samarkand's monuments).
  • Delhi did not fully recover for decades; the Tughlaq dynasty became a shadow.

Maharashtra Connection

High relevance for Maharashtra. Muhammad bin Tughlaq's capital transfer to Daulatabad (Devagiri) is in Aurangabad district, Maharashtra. This capital transfer — though a failure — brought Delhi's administrative machinery, Islamic scholars, Sufi saints, and Persian-speaking elites to Maharashtra's heartland for several years. The Bahmani Sultanate (1347 CE) — which directly controlled Maharashtra — arose as a revolt of Tughlaq governors in the Deccan, making the Tughlaq era the direct precursor to independent Muslim rule in Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • Muhammad bin Tughlaq: capital to Daulatabad (Devagiri, Maharashtra) c. 1327 CE.
  • Token currency: copper/brass coins at gold/silver value — failed catastrophically.
  • Ibn Battuta: served Muhammad bin Tughlaq; wrote Rihla; Moroccan traveller.
  • Firoz Shah: dug 5 canals; Western Yamuna Canal (200 km) still functional.
  • Firoz Shah: moved Ashokan pillars from Topra and Meerut to Delhi.
  • Timur invaded: 1398–99 CE; sacked Delhi; ended effective Tughlaq power.
  • Bahmani revolt: 1347 CE — Tughlaq governors in Deccan revolted; established Bahmani kingdom.

Exam Traps

  • Muhammad bin Tughlaq's token currency was NOT paper money — it was copper/brass coins assigned the value of precious metals; failed because of forgery (no anti-counterfeiting mechanism).
  • The Bahmani kingdom arose as a revolt against the Tughlaqs (1347), not the Khiljis.
  • Firoz Shah moved Ashokan pillars — he could not read the inscriptions; they were not deciphered until 1837 (James Prinsep).
  • The Western Yamuna Canal (dug by Firoz Shah) is sometimes called Firoz Shah's Canal — still partly operational; do not confuse with the Eastern Yamuna Canal (British era).
