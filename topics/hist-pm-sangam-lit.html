Sangam literature and society PREMAINS

Overview

Sangam literature is the body of classical Tamil poetry compiled by the three legendary literary academies (Sangams) held at Madurai, spanning roughly the period c. 300 BCE – 300 CE. It comprises 2,381 poems in the major anthologies, written by over 473 named poets (including women) — India's earliest secular literature covering love (akam) and war/heroism (puram). MPSC asks this topic for specific texts, their classification, notable poets, and the society they reflect.

In-Depth

The Three Sangams (Legend)

Tamil tradition holds that three literary academies were held:

  1. First Sangam (Mutamutakkaniyar presided): at Madurai — gods and legendary sages; texts lost.
  2. Second Sangam (Agastya presided): at Kapatapuram (now submerged) — partly survived.
  3. Third Sangam (Nakkirar presided): at Madurai — produced the extant Sangam corpus.

The three-Sangam tradition is partly legendary, but the literary corpus is historically real and dates mainly to the 3rd–4th centuries CE in its compilation, with individual poems as old as 3rd century BCE.

Classification: Pathinenmelkanakku (18 Major Works)

The Sangam corpus is divided into:

  • Pattu-paattu (Ten Idylls / Ten Songs) — longer poems, each 100–800+ lines.
  • Ettuthokai (Eight Anthologies) — collections of shorter akam and puram poems.
  • Pathinenkilkanakku (Eighteen Minor Works) — post-Sangam, didactic; includes Tirukkural.

Key Texts in Detail

Tirukkural — composed by Thiruvalluvar (date disputed: 1st century BCE to 5th century CE; most scholars: c. 2nd–4th century CE). Divided into 3 books:

  • Aram (Dharma) — 380 couplets on ethics.
  • Porul (Artha) — 700 couplets on polity and statecraft.
  • Inbam (Kama) — 250 couplets on love.

Total: 133 chapters × 10 couplets = 1330 kural (couplets). Universally respected across Tamil tradition; called Muththamizh (three-Tamil) and the "Tamil Vedas."

Cilappatikaram ("The Tale of an Anklet") — composed by Ilango Adigal (a Jain or Buddhist prince, brother of Chera king Senguttuvan). It is the story of Kovalan and Kannagi — Kovalan abandons Kannagi for a courtesan Madhavi; later accused of stealing the queen of Madurai's anklet; killed; Kannagi tears off her breast and burns Madurai. A story of love, fidelity, and divine justice. It covers all three kingdoms' capitals (Kaveripattinam/Puhar → Madurai → Vanji) and is an invaluable social history source.

Manimekalai — sequel to Cilappatikaram by Sattanar; traces Kovalan's daughter Manimekalai who becomes a Buddhist nun. Rich in Buddhist philosophy.

Notable Poets

  • Avvaiyar: Woman poet; famous for moral poetry; appears in multiple eras (Sangam and later medieval Tamil tradition — likely multiple poets with this name).
  • Nakkirar: Presided over the Third Sangam; known for commentary on Iraiyanar Akapporul.
  • Kapilar: Brahmin poet; wrote both akam and puram; close friend of chieftain Pari; composed laments on Pari's death.
  • Tolkappiyar: Author of Tolkappiyam — the earliest Tamil grammar text (c. 3rd century BCE), covering phonology, grammar, and porulatikaram (poetics). It is older than most Sangam poetry.

Tolkappiyam — The Grammar Text

  • Author: Tolkappiyar (a student of the sage Agastya, per tradition).
  • 3 books (iyal): Ezhuttu (phonology), Col (grammar), Porul (poetics/semantics).
  • The Porul chapter systematically defines the tinai system and the conventions of akam and puram poetry — the foundational text for Sangam literary theory.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. Tamil Sangam literature is entirely South Indian in origin and content. However, the Sangam epics (Cilappatikaram, Manimekalai) reflect Buddhist and Jain influences that were also strong in Maharashtra's Satavahana period — indicating a shared intellectual climate. Tolkappiyam's treatment of grammar is sometimes compared to Panini's Sanskrit grammar, both of which represent India's early linguistic tradition.

Key Facts

  • Tirukkural: by Thiruvalluvar; 1330 couplets; 3 books (Aram, Porul, Inbam).
  • Cilappatikaram: by Ilango Adigal; story of Kovalan and Kannagi.
  • Manimekalai: sequel to Cilappatikaram; by Sattanar; Buddhist theme.
  • Tolkappiyam: earliest Tamil grammar; by Tolkappiyar.
  • Third Sangam presided by: Nakkirar.
  • Eight anthologies (Ettuthokai) + Ten idylls (Pattu-paattu) = core Sangam corpus.
  • Sangam period: c. 300 BCE – 300 CE.
  • Purananuru: key historical source for Sangam polity (400 puram poems).

Memory Aids

  • "Three Sangams: Two lost, One survives" — only the Third Sangam's texts are extant.
  • "Tirukkural = 1330" — 133 chapters × 10 = 1330; "Thiru = 133."
  • Cilappatikaram = "Anklet tale = Kannagi burns Madurai."
  • Tolkappiyar = "Tolka = old + Piyar = person = oldest Tamil grammar."
  • Three books of Tirukkural: "A-P-I" (Aram, Porul, Inbam) — Ethics, Polity, Love.

Exam Traps

  • Tirukkural is categorised under Pathinenkilkanakku (18 Minor/Later Works), NOT the Eight Anthologies — it is post-Sangam in compilation (though Thiruvalluvar's date is debated).
  • Cilappatikaram's author Ilango Adigal is traditionally a Jain prince (some say Buddhist) — not a Brahmin poet.
  • Avvaiyar appears in both Sangam and later medieval Tamil literature — there may be multiple poets with this name spanning centuries.
  • Tolkappiyam is older than most Sangam poetry compilations — it is a grammar/poetics text, not itself a Sangam anthology.
