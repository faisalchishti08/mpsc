Vijayanagara empire — Krishnadevaraya PREMAINS

Overview

The Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646) was the last great Hindu empire of south India, founded by Harihara I and Bukka Raya of the Sangama dynasty at Hampi on the Tungabhadra river. It served as a bulwark against Deccan Sultanate expansion southward and reached its zenith under Krishnadevaraya (1509–1529) of the Tuluva dynasty. MPSC asks about Krishnadevaraya, administration, Hampi's architecture, and the Battle of Talikota.

In-Depth

Foundation and Dynasties

Vijayanagara was established in 1336 — traditionally by two brothers, Harihara (Hakka) and Bukka Raya, said to have been officers under the Hoysala/Kampili kingdoms who converted to Islam under Muhammad bin Tughlaq and reconverted to Hinduism under the guidance of saint Vidyaranya. The capital was at Hampi (Vijayanagara = "City of Victory"). Four dynasties ruled:

Krishnadevaraya (1509–1529) — The Pinnacle

Krishnadevaraya is the greatest ruler of Vijayanagara and one of the most celebrated monarchs of medieval India:

  • Military conquests: defeated Gajapati king of Orissa in 1512 (captured Udayagiri and Kondavidu); crushed Bidar Sultanate (1512); defeated Adil Shahi of Bijapur (1520) and captured Raichur Doab.
  • Administrative skills: efficient revenue collection; promoted agriculture and irrigation; maintained a large standing army supplemented by Portuguese firearms (bought from Portuguese at Goa).
  • Literary achievements: he was himself a scholar and poet in Telugu; authored Amuktamalyada (Telugu) and Madalasacharitra (Sanskrit). His court had the Ashtadiggajas (Eight Elephants — eight great Telugu poets including Allasani Peddana, the Andhra Kavita Pitamaha, and Tenali Ramakrishna).
  • Architecture: built Vitthala Temple complex at Hampi (including the famous Stone Chariot); commissioned Hazara Rama Temple; erected Ugra Narasimha monolith.
  • He maintained cordial relations with Portugal (received ambassador Domingo Paes) — Paes' account is a valuable historical source.

Administration

  • Nayankara system (similar to Mughal Mansabdari): provincial governors (Nayakas) were assigned territory (amaram) in exchange for military service and tribute. This decentralised power and was a major cause of collapse post-Talikota.
  • Amatya Pravar (chief minister) and a council of ministers assisted the king.
  • Revenue: primarily land tax (shistha); trade taxes from coastal ports (Calicut, Cochin, Bhatkal) boosted treasury.
  • Trade with Arabia, Persia, China; exports of cotton, spices, iron, copper.

Hampi Architecture

Vijayanagara developed a distinctive Dravida-style architecture with some Islamic influences:

  • Vitthala Temple: unfinished but most ornate; stone chariot (Garuda shrine); musical pillars.
  • Virupaksha Temple: oldest active temple; main gopuram.
  • Hazara Rama Temple: bas-relief narrative panels (Ramayana story).
  • Lotus Mahal and Elephant Stables: blended Indo-Islamic style (arched stables).
  • Tungabhadra dam: irrigation infrastructure.
  • Hampi is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (1986).

Battle of Talikota (1565) and Fall

Aliya Rama Raya, regent for a weak Vijayanagara king, fought the combined forces of four Deccan Sultanates (Bijapur, Ahmednagar, Bidar, Golkonda) at Talikota (near Bijapur) in January 1565. The Vijayanagara army was defeated when two Muslim commanders in its own army (Gilani brothers) switched sides. Rama Raya was captured and beheaded. The confederate forces sacked Hampi, reducing it to ruins. The empire survived in diminished form under the Aravidu dynasty till 1646.

Maharashtra Connection

The Bahmani Sultanate (northern neighbour and rival) and its successor Deccan Sultanates constantly clashed with Vijayanagara over the Raichur Doab. The Ahmednagar Sultanate (in present Maharashtra) was one of the four confederate powers that defeated Vijayanagara at Talikota (1565). Krishnadevaraya's victory at Raichur Doab had direct ramifications for the Deccan political map. The conflict shaped the political landscape that Shivaji Maharaj later inherited.

Key Facts

  • Founded: 1336, by Harihara I and Bukka Raya.
  • Capital: Hampi (Vijayanagara), on the Tungabhadra river.
  • Greatest ruler: Krishnadevaraya (1509–1529), Tuluva dynasty.
  • Krishnadevaraya's Telugu work: Amuktamalyada.
  • Eight poets in court: Ashtadiggajas (chief: Allasani Peddana).
  • Nayankara system: provincial governors in exchange for military service.
  • Battle of Talikota: 1565 — four Deccan Sultanates defeated Vijayanagara.
  • Hampi: UNESCO World Heritage Site (1986).
  • Portuguese ambassador to Krishnadevaraya: Domingo Paes.

Memory Aids

  • Four dynasties: "Sangama → Saluva → Tuluva → Aravidu" (SSTA).
  • Krishnadevaraya's court: Ashtadiggajas = 8 poets (ashta = eight).
  • Talikota = "T-1565" (four Sultanates ended the glory in 1565).
  • Stone Chariot: Vitthala Temple, Hampi — on the ₹50 note (historical connection).

Exam Traps

  • Harihara and Bukka founded Vijayanagara, NOT Krishnadevaraya — Krishnadevaraya made it famous but didn't found it.
  • Talikota (1565) did NOT immediately end the empire — Aravidu dynasty continued till 1646; Talikota ended Vijayanagara's supremacy, not its existence.
  • Nayankara ≠ Mansabdari — similar concept (service for revenue) but different in operation; don't use Mughal terms for Vijayanagara.
  • Domingo Paes (Portuguese) visited Krishnadevaraya, not Deva Raya II — timeline matters.
