Dr. B.R. Ambedkar — Mahad, Kalaram, Constitution PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

Dr. Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar (1891–1956) is Maharashtra's — and India's — greatest Dalit leader, architect of the Indian Constitution, and a towering intellectual. His journey from an untouchable childhood in Ambavade (Ratnagiri) to chairing the Drafting Committee of the Constitution is India's greatest story of social mobility through education. For MPSC, Ambedkar's satyagrahas (Mahad 1927, Kalaram temple 1930), writings, Constitution drafting, and conversion to Buddhism (1956) are core high-frequency topics.

Major Satyagrahas

Mahad Satyagraha (1927)

  • Location: Mahad, Raigad district (on the banks of the Chowdar Tank / Chawdar Talab).
  • Date: 20 March 1927 — led thousands of Dalits to drink water from the Chowdar Tank (public tank) from which they were excluded.
  • Significance: First major assertion of civil rights for untouchables in Maharashtra; symbolically equivalent to Gandhiji's Dandi March but for social rights.
  • Second Mahad session (25 December 1927): Ambedkar burned a copy of Manusmriti — the Hindu lawbook that codified caste discrimination. This is one of the most dramatic acts of social protest in Indian history.
  • 25 December is observed as Manusmriti Dahan Din by Dalit organisations.

Kalaram Temple Satyagraha (1930)

  • Location: Kalaram Temple, Nashik — a prominent Ram temple from which Dalits (Mahars) were excluded.
  • Date: 2 March 1930 — Ambedkar led a procession of thousands to enter the Kalaram Temple; upper-caste groups blocked entry; protest continued for five years until 1935.
  • Significance: Even after years, the temple remained barred for Dalits — this drove Ambedkar's declaration: "I was born a Hindu, but will not die a Hindu."

Constitution Drafting

  • Ambedkar was appointed Law Minister in the first Union Cabinet (1947) by Nehru.
  • Elected to the Drafting Committee of the Constituent Assembly; elected Chairman of the Drafting Committee — effectively the chief architect of the Constitution.
  • Key contributions to the Constitution:
  • Article 17: Abolition of untouchability.
  • Article 15: Prohibition of discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth.
  • Article 16: Equality of opportunity in public employment; reservations for SC/ST.
  • Article 46: Directive Principle — promote educational and economic interests of weaker sections.
  • Articles 330, 332, 335: Reservations for SC/ST in Parliament, State Legislatures, services.
  • Opposed Uniform Civil Code deferral; later resigned (1951) over Hindu Code Bill's non-passage.

Writings

  • "The Annihilation of Caste" (1936): Speech written for the Jat-Pat Todak Mandal; never delivered (organisers cancelled); self-published; the most devastating intellectual attack on the caste system; Gandhi and Ambedkar had a famous public debate over it.
  • "Who Were the Shudras?" (1946): Argued that Shudras were originally Aryans who lost status.
  • "Pakistan or the Partition of India" (1945): Analysed communal question.
  • "The Buddha and His Dhamma" (1956): Completed just before his death; the Buddhist text for the neo-Buddhist movement.
  • "Castes in India: Their Mechanism, Genesis and Development" (1916): His Columbia paper — first systematic sociological study of Indian caste.

Conversion to Buddhism (14 October 1956)

  • Date: 14 October 1956, Nagpur — at Deekshabhoomi.
  • Ambedkar led ~5–6 lakh Dalits in converting to Buddhism in a mass ceremony at Deekshabhoomi.
  • This is called the Dhammachakra Pravartan Din (Day of Conversion/Dhamma Revolution).
  • He chose Buddhism because it rejected caste and the concept of the soul (Atman) while promoting equality, reason, and ethics.
  • Died: 6 December 1956 (just 47 days after conversion) — "Mahaparinirvan Din" observed on 6 December.
  • Deekshabhoomi (Nagpur): The stupa at Deekshabhoomi is the largest hollow stupa in Asia; millions gather on 14 October each year; a pilgrimage site.
  • Chaitya Bhoomi (Mumbai): His memorial at Dadar, Mumbai; he died at his Delhi home, cremated in Mumbai; millions gather on 6 December.

Maharashtra Connection

Ambedkar's life map is Maharashtra's map: born in Ratnagiri ancestral roots, educated in Bombay (Elphinstone), rose to prominence through Mahad (Raigad) and Nashik (Kalaram), died in Delhi, and cremated in Mumbai. Deekshabhoomi (Nagpur) and Chaitya Bhoomi (Mumbai) are Maharashtra's most important Ambedkari pilgrimage sites. 14 April (Ambedkar Jayanti) is a national and Maharashtra public holiday; 6 December (Mahaparinirvan Din) draws millions to Chaitya Bhoomi.

Exam Traps

  • Ambedkar was born at Mhow (MP) — NOT at Ambavade (Ratnagiri), which is his ancestral village. Both MH and MP can appear as options.
  • Mahad Satyagraha water event = 20 March 1927; Manusmriti burning = 25 December 1927 — same year, two events.
  • He resigned as Law Minister in 1951 (over Hindu Code Bill) — NOT in 1947.
