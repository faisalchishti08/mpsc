Rajput kingdoms and tripartite struggle PREMAINS

Overview

The Rajput kingdoms (c. 700–1200 CE) dominated north and northwestern India after the decline of Harsha's empire, forming the political landscape that confronted the early Turko-Afghan invasions. The period is marked by the tripartite struggle for Kanauj among the Gurjara-Pratiharas, Palas, and Rashtrakutas, and the catastrophic defeat of the Rajput confederacy at the Second Battle of Tarain (1192 CE) by Muhammad Ghori. MPSC tests the origins controversy, key dynasties, and the tripartite struggle.

In-Depth

Rajput Origins — The Controversy

The origin of Rajputs is debated:

  1. Foreign origin theory (V.A. Smith): Rajputs descended from foreign invaders (Huns, Gurjaras, Sakas) who were Hinduised and given Kshatriya status.
  2. Indigenous origin: descended from existing Kshatriya clans.
  3. Agnikula (Fire-origin) legend: Four major Rajput clans — Pratiharas, Paramaras, Chahamanas (Chauhans), and Chaulukyas (Solankis) — were born from a sacred fire (agni-kunda) at Mount Abu — recorded in the Prithviraj Raso of Chand Bardai.

The Agnikula legend is not historically accurate but serves to legitimise these clans as a distinct martial group. Modern historians view Rajputs as a varna (status group) that crystallised in the 7th–9th centuries CE from diverse backgrounds.

Tripartite Struggle for Kanauj (c. 750–1000 CE)

Kanauj was the symbolic capital of north India — whoever held it claimed supremacy. Three powers fought over it in shifting coalitions:

  • Gurjara-Pratiharas (Rajasthan/UP): Nagabhata I (repelled first Arab attacks in Sindh c. 730 CE), Bhoja I/Mihira Bhoja (c. 836–882 CE, greatest Pratihara — Arab envoy Al-Masudi called his cavalry the finest in India).
  • Palas (Bengal-Bihar): Gopala (founder), Dharmapala (greatest — held Kanauj briefly; patron of Vikramashila monastery), Devapala.
  • Rashtrakutas (Deccan): Dhruva, Govinda III — repeatedly raided north India but never permanently held Kanauj.

None achieved lasting dominance; the struggle exhausted all three, leaving north India fragmented before Turkish invasions.

Rajput Political Culture

  • Feudal structure: the Rajput king granted land (iqta-like bhumi) to subordinate chiefs who owed military service. This created a layered, decentralised military hierarchy.
  • Samanta system: intermediate lords (sāmanta) who acknowledged suzerainty but had autonomous control.
  • Clan loyalty: kula (clan) and gotra pride was paramount; inter-clan warfare was frequent.
  • Women and jauhar: Jauhar (mass self-immolation by women when a fort was about to fall) was a Rajput practice recorded from the 12th century — associated with Chitor fort particularly.

Khajuraho Temples (Chandela Rajputs)

The Chandela dynasty built the Khajuraho temple complex (MP) c. 950–1050 CE:

  • ~85 temples originally; 25 survive; UNESCO World Heritage Site (1986).
  • Famous for erotic sculptures (mithuna panels) on the outer walls — various explanations offered: tantric symbolism, worldly-to-spiritual progression, apotropaic (warding off evil).
  • Main temples: Kandariya Mahadeva (largest, dedicated to Shiva), Lakshmana Temple (Vishnu), Devi Jagadambi (Parvati).

Prithviraj III (Chauhan) and the Battle of Tarain

Prithviraj Chauhan (r. 1178–1192 CE) of Ajmer-Delhi — the last great independent Rajput king:

  • First Battle of Tarain (1191 CE): Prithviraj defeated Muhammad Ghori — the only major defeat inflicted on Ghori.
  • Second Battle of Tarain (1192 CE): Ghori returned with a reformed cavalry strategy; Prithviraj's coalition was decisively defeated. Prithviraj captured and killed (accounts vary — Rajput tradition says he shot Ghori blind before dying; historically unlikely).
  • The defeat of Prithviraj opened the Gangetic plain to Turkish conquest and marked the beginning of the Delhi Sultanate.
  • Prithviraj Raso by Chand Bardai — a bardic poem (partly legendary) about Prithviraj; important for Rajput cultural history.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. The Paramara king Bhoja II (Raja Bhoja of Dhara) — one of the most learned Indian kings — ruled Malwa (adjacent to Maharashtra's northern border). His Dhara (Dhar, MP) is near Maharashtra. Bhoja patronised Sanskrit learning extensively — wrote works on grammar (Saraswatikanthabharana), poetics, yoga, medicine. He reportedly built a Saraswati temple whose remains are in Dhar. Some Rajput clans eventually settled in parts of Maharashtra (Khandesh region).

Key Facts

  • Greatest Pratihara: Bhoja I (Mihira Bhoja) (c. 836–882 CE).
  • Arab visitor Al-Masudi praised Pratihara cavalry.
  • Khajuraho: built by Chandelas; UNESCO WHS 1986; largest temple: Kandariya Mahadeva.
  • First Battle of Tarain: 1191 CE — Prithviraj defeated Ghori.
  • Second Battle of Tarain: 1192 CE — Ghori defeated Prithviraj; opening of Sultanate era.
  • Prithviraj Raso: by Chand Bardai — bardic account of Prithviraj.
  • Agnikula clans: Pratiharas, Paramaras, Chahamanas, Chaulukyas.

Exam Traps

  • First Battle of Tarain (1191): Prithviraj WON; Second Battle of Tarain (1192): Prithviraj LOST — don't reverse.
  • Khajuraho UNESCO year is 1986 — different from Ellora/Ajanta (1983) and Pattadakal (1987).
  • Agnikula legend = four clans; Prithviraj Chauhan (Chahamana) is one of them.
  • Palas were NOT a Rajput dynasty — they were from Bengal and followed Buddhism (paramabhaugata / Mahayana); often confused with Rajputs in tripartite struggle.
