Literary sources — religious & secular texts PREMAINS

Overview

Literary sources for ancient and medieval Indian history include religious texts (Vedas, Buddhist Tripitaka, Jain Agamas), secular literature (Arthashastra, Puranas, chronicles), and biographical and semi-fictional works. They complement archaeological sources by providing names, dates, narratives, and ideas — but carry the bias of their authors and traditions. MPSC tests knowledge of which text was written by whom, in which period, and what historical information it contains.

In-Depth

Religious Literature

Vedic corpus:

  • 4 Vedas (Rigveda, Samaveda, Yajurveda, Atharvaveda): Oldest literary compositions (~1500–900 BCE). Rigveda (~10 mandalas) is oldest — contains hymns, references to the Sabha, Samiti, early polity, river geography (Sapta Sindhu). Historical value limited but provides insight into early Aryan society.
  • Brahmanas: Prose texts explaining Vedic rituals; contain legend/mythology.
  • Upanishads (~108 in number): Philosophical speculations on Brahman and Atman; Brihadaranyaka and Chandogya are earliest.
  • Epics: Mahabharata (~1 lakh verses; attributed to Vyasa) and Ramayana (24,000 verses; Valmiki) — contain historical kernels (Kurukshetra war, Dwaraka) embedded in mythology; useful for understanding Iron Age society and values.
  • Puranas (18 Mahapuranas): Contain king-lists (vamsanucharita) — Vishnu Purana, Vayu Purana have valuable dynastic data on Mauryas, Guptas, Satavahanas.

Buddhist Literature (Pali):

  • Tripitaka (Vinaya Pitaka, Sutta Pitaka, Abhidhamma Pitaka): Contains the Dhammapada; Vinaya Pitaka describes monastic rules; Jatakas (birth stories) reflect everyday life, trade, urban conditions (~6th–4th c. BCE).
  • Milindapanha: Dialogue between Menander (Indo-Greek king, 2nd c. BCE) and monk Nagasena — evidence for Indo-Greek history.
  • Dipavamsa and Mahavamsa (Sri Lankan chronicles, ~4th–5th c. CE): Important for Mauryan history (Ashoka sending Mahinda to Sri Lanka), though written long after events.
  • Divyavadana: Sanskrit Buddhist text; mentions Ashoka and his religious activities.

Jain Literature:

  • Agamas (Angas): Earliest Jain texts; contain historical info on Mahavira, 24 Tirthankaras.
  • Parishishtaparvan by Hemachandra (12th c.): Biography of Chandragupta Maurya's connection with Jainism.

Secular Literature

  • Arthashastra (Kautilya/Chanakya, ~3rd c. BCE; rediscovered 1905 by R. Shamasastry): Most important secular source for Mauryan administration. Covers governance, espionage, foreign policy (Saptanga theory), taxation, revenue, and law.
  • Mudrarakshasa (Vishakhadatta, ~9th c. CE): Sanskrit play about Chandragupta Maurya overthrowing the Nandas — historical fiction with political detail.
  • Harshacharita (Banabhatta): Sanskrit prose biography of Harsha (~630 CE); valuable but panegyric.
  • Rajatarangini (Kalhana, 1148–50 CE): First true chronological history in Sanskrit; history of Kashmir from mythological origins to 12th c.; critical and rational in approach compared to earlier literary sources.
  • Prithvirajraso (Chand Bardai, 12th–13th c.): Epic poem on Prithviraj Chahamana; later interpolations make it unreliable for detailed facts.

Limitations of Literary Sources

  1. Authorial bias: Court chronicles flatter patrons; religious texts promote theological views.
  2. Time lag: Many texts compiled centuries after events described (Puranas, Sri Lankan chronicles).
  3. Myth and fact intermingled: Epics embed historical kernels in mythology; difficult to separate.
  4. Selective coverage: Focus on kings and elites; women, peasants, artisans largely absent.
  5. Oral transmission distortion: Long oral periods before writing introduced errors.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Satavahana inscriptions + Prakrit literature: The Satavahana dynasty (Deccan, ~2nd c. BCE–3rd c. CE) is documented partly through texts like the Gathasaptashati (Hala's Prakrit verses) and partly through coins and inscriptions. Nashik cave inscriptions (Pandu Lena) are a mix of both literary and epigraphic sources.
  • Lilavati (Bhaskara II, 12th c., from Bidar/Deccan): Mathematical text — evidence of Maharashtra/Deccan's intellectual heritage.

Key Facts

  • Arthashastra rediscovered in 1905 by R. Shamasastry (Mysore Oriental Library).
  • Rajatarangini = first chronological history in Sanskrit (Kalhana, 1148–50 CE).
  • Milindapanha: Evidence for Indo-Greek king Menander (known as Milinda in Pali).
  • Jatakas: Buddhist stories reflecting ~6th–4th c. BCE society; 547 in number.
  • Kitab-ul-Hind (Al-Biruni, 1030): Empirical Arabic study of India; product of Mahmud of Ghazni's raids.
  • Ain-i-Akbari (Abul Fazl, 1598): Part of Akbarnama trilogy; key source for Mughal economy/administration.

Memory Aids

  • "Arthashastra = Kautilya, Harshacharita = Bana, Rajatarangini = Kalhana" — three secular literary giants.
  • Rajatarangini: "Raja-tara-ngini = kings-flow-river → chronicle of Kashmir kings, flowing like a river."
  • Milindapanha: "Milinda = Menander — Greek king who asked Buddhist questions."
  • Arthashastra found in 1905 by Shamasastry — two '5's to remember (1905, Sham-ASTRY).

Exam Traps

  • Arthashastra is by Kautilya/Chanakya (~3rd c. BCE), NOT by Vishakhadatta (he wrote Mudrarakshasa, a play about the same period).
  • Rajatarangini is a chronicle of Kashmir, not all of India — scope is often misread.
  • Indica (Megasthenes) is a foreign account, not an Indian literary source; covered under foreign accounts.
  • Jatakas are Buddhist and contain mainly story/folklore mixed with historical data, NOT doctrinal texts (Sutta Pitaka is doctrinal).
