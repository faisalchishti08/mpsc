Harshavardhana and Pushyabhuti dynasty PREMAINS

Overview

Harshavardhana (606–647 CE) of the Pushyabhuti dynasty was the last great emperor of northern India before the medieval period. He ruled from Kanauj and for a brief period reunited much of north India, but his empire collapsed after his death, marking the start of fragmented regional kingdoms. MPSC tests Harsha's administration, his literary output, his failure in the Deccan, and the accounts of Xuanzang (Hiuen Tsang) — the most important Chinese pilgrim in Indian history.

In-Depth

Pushyabhuti Dynasty and Rise of Harsha

The Pushyabhutis ruled Thanesar (Sthanvishvara, Haryana) as feudatories. Harsha's father Prabhakaravardhana was the first powerful Pushyabhuti king. His sister Rajyashri was married to Maukhari king Grihavarman (Kanauj), who was killed by Deva Gupta (Malwa) and Shashanka (Gauda/Bengal). Harsha's elder brother Rajyavardhana died fighting Shashanka. Harsha became king at ~16 years of age (606 CE) and waged war to rescue Rajyashri.

He transferred the capital from Thanesar to Kanauj — geographically central, symbolically the seat of paramount power in north India.

Military Campaigns

Harsha quickly built a large empire through:

  • Defeated/subordinated the rulers of Punjab, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Bengal, Odisha.
  • Attempted to cross the Narmada southward but was stopped by Pulakesi II (Chalukya of Vatapi/Badami) c. 618–619 CE — the only military setback of his career. The Aihole Inscription of Pulakesi II (composed by his poet Ravikirti) boasts of stopping Harsha's advance.
  • Alliance with Bhaskaravarman (king of Assam/Kamarupa) against Shashanka (Bengal).

Administration

Harsha's administration is well-documented through:

  1. Bana's Harshacharita — court biography in ornate Sanskrit prose; covers his rise and early campaigns.
  2. Xuanzang's Si-yu-ki (Records of the Western Regions) — Chinese pilgrim's first-hand account.

Key features:

  • Personal monarchy: Harsha was extremely active — toured constantly, heard petitions, inspected military and welfare works. Xuanzang records his tireless energy.
  • Revenue: Land revenue was divided — 1/4 for state expenses, 1/4 for scholars and learned men, 1/4 for infrastructure (rest-houses, roads), 1/4 for religious endowments. This is strikingly generous allocation to non-state purposes.
  • Military: Large force — Xuanzang mentions 60,000 elephants and 100,000 cavalry.
  • Welfare: Charitable hospitals (dharmashalas) at road junctions; free food and medicine.

Kanauj Assembly (643 CE)

Harsha organised the Kanauj Assembly in honour of Xuanzang — a grand philosophical debate lasting 18 days. Representatives of all major religious sects attended. This was a public legitimation of Mahayana Buddhism, which Harsha patronised alongside Shaivism and Vaishnavism.

Prayag Quinquennial Assembly

Every five years, Harsha held a mass assembly at Prayag (Allahabad confluence) — distributing all his accumulated treasury to the poor, Brahmins, Buddhists, and beggars. Xuanzang attended the 6th such assembly (~643 CE) and describes Harsha giving away even his personal jewels and robes. His sister Rajyashri gave him a plain garment to cover himself at the end.

Harsha as Author

Harsha himself was a playwright:

  • Ratnavali — romantic play.
  • Priyadarshika — romantic play.
  • Nagananda — Buddhist play about the Bodhisattva Jimutavahana sacrificing himself to save serpents; considered his masterpiece.

Xuanzang's Account

Xuanzang (Hiuen Tsang) visited India 629–645 CE — primarily to study Buddhist texts at Nalanda. His Si-yu-ki is the single richest foreign account of 7th-century India:

  • Describes Nalanda as a great university with thousands of monks, free boarding and education.
  • Describes Hindu and Buddhist religious practices; temple wealth.
  • Notes the decline of Buddhism in the Gangetic plain (shifting from its 3rd-century BCE heartland).
  • Notes the prosperity of the Gupta–Harsha continuum.

His disciple Yijing (I-Tsing) also visited India (671–695 CE) and documented Buddhist monasteries.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. Harsha's failure at the Narmada is directly related to Maharashtra — Pulakesi II (Chalukya of Vatapi/Badami) stopped him, and Chalukya power was centred in Karnataka-Maharashtra border region (Vatapi = Badami, Karnataka; but their campaigns extensively covered the Deccan including Maharashtra). The Aihole inscription (Karnataka) commemorating this victory is adjacent to Maharashtra history. Post-Harsha fragmentation created the conditions for the Rashtrakuta dynasty (Maharashtra) to rise in the 8th century.

Key Facts

  • Harsha's dynasty: Pushyabhuti.
  • Capital: Kanauj (shifted from Thanesar).
  • Reign: 606–647 CE (died without heir → empire collapsed).
  • Stopped by: Pulakesi II (Chalukya) at Narmada, c. 618–619 CE.
  • Aihole inscription: by Ravikirti (Pulakesi II's court poet) — boasts about stopping Harsha.
  • Xuanzang visited India: 629–645 CE; wrote Si-yu-ki.
  • Prayag Assembly: every 5 years; Harsha gave away entire treasury.
  • Harsha's plays: Ratnavali, Priyadarshika, Nagananda (Buddhist masterpiece).
  • Court biography: Bana's Harshacharita.

Memory Aids

  • "Pushya = Thanesar → Kanauj" — dynasty name and capital shift.
  • "Harsha stopped at Narmada by Pulakesi" — his one defeat.
  • "Xuanzang = 629–645 CE = Nalanda" — Chinese pilgrim of Harsha's era.
  • Prayag Assembly = 5-year interval; "Harsha goes broke every 5 years giving away wealth."
  • Three plays: "R-P-N" — Ratnavali, Priyadarshika, Nagananda.

Exam Traps

  • Xuanzang visited Harsha's India (7th century CE) — do NOT confuse with Faxian who visited Chandragupta II (5th century CE); two different pilgrims.
  • Harsha was a playwright himself — do NOT confuse his literary works with Bana (Harshacharita is Bana's biography, not Harsha's own work).
  • Harsha was not primarily a Buddhist — he was a Shaivite who later became a patron of Mahayana Buddhism; he patronised all faiths.
  • The Aihole inscription is of Pulakesi II (Chalukya), not of Harsha — it is a Chalukya victory record.
