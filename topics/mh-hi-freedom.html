Overview

Maharashtra was at the forefront of India's freedom struggle — from Tilak's Bal-Pal-Lal extremism to Vinayak Damodar Savarkar's revolutionary movement, the Chapekar brothers' assassinations, Quit India Movement centres in Pune and Bombay, and Subhas Chandra Bose's connections. MPSC tests Maharashtra's contribution to specific freedom struggle events, organisations, and leaders.

Chapekar Brothers (1897) — Maharashtra's First Political Assassins

  • Damodar Hari Chapekar and Balkrishna Chapekar assassinated Walter Charles Rand (Plague Commissioner) and another British officer Lt. Ayerst on 22 June 1897 in Pune.
  • Context: Rand had authorised coercive plague search operations (entering homes forcibly, separating families) during the bubonic plague of 1896–97 in Pune — causing massive resentment.
  • Tilak had published a Shivaji Jayanti article with the line "when God was about to do, who could prevent it?" (interpreted as endorsing tyrannicide) — used to prosecute him.
  • Damodar Chapekar was hanged in April 1898; Balkrishna in 1899; a third brother Vasudeo was also executed.
  • Their act is considered India's first political assassination in the modern freedom struggle.

Vinayak Damodar Savarkar (1883–1966)

  • Born: 28 May 1883, Bhagur, Nashik district.
  • Founded Abhinav Bharat Society (1904, Nashik) — revolutionary organisation for armed uprising.
  • Wrote "The Indian War of Independence — 1857" (1907, London) — argued 1857 was a planned nationalist uprising, not a "mutiny"; banned by British in India; published in Holland.
  • Arranged supply of firearms from London (including the Nasik Conspiracy Case).
  • Arrested 1910, transported to Cellular Jail (Kala Pani), Andaman Nicobar Islands — spent ~13 years; wrote on prison walls with a nail; committed to memory thousands of lines of verse.
  • Coined the term "Hindutva" in his 1923 book; post-prison, became a Hindu Mahasabha leader.

Quit India Movement — Satara Prati Sarkar (1942–1945)

  • Gowalia Tank (now August Kranti Maidan), Mumbai: Site where Gandhi delivered the "Do or Die" speech on 8 August 1942 — launching Quit India Movement.
  • Satara Prati Sarkar (Parallel Government): After the movement was suppressed, underground leaders in Satara district (Y.B. Chavan, Nana Patil, Krishnarao Bapat) established a parallel government (Prati Sarkar) — collected taxes, ran courts, organised schools; operational 1943–1946; one of the most organised underground governments in India.
  • Nana Patil (Kranti Singh): Leader of the Satara Prati Sarkar; Maharashtra's most famous freedom fighter outside the mainstream; simple, mass leader.

Senapati Bapat (1880–1967)

  • Pandurang Mahadev "Senapati" Bapat — known as "Senapati" (Army General) for his revolutionary activities.
  • Led the Mulshi Satyagraha (1921–24) against the Mulshi Dam construction (Tata Power) that was displacing tribal and peasant communities in Pune district — one of India's earliest environmental-tribal rights protests.

Maharashtra Connection

August Kranti Maidan (Gowalia Tank, Mumbai) is where Gandhi launched Quit India — a national landmark in Maharashtra. Satara's Prati Sarkar is Maharashtra's unique contribution to underground freedom struggle organisation — Y.B. Chavan (later Maharashtra's first CM) was shaped by this movement. Savarkar's Abhinav Bharat (Nashik) and Chapekar brothers (Pune, 1897) establish Maharashtra as the seedbed of revolutionary nationalism.
