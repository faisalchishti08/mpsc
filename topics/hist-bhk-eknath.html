Eknath PREMAINSMH

Overview

Sant Eknath (1533–1599 CE) is the third pillar of the Warkari tradition in Maharashtra, bridging the 13th-century era of Dnyaneshwar and Namdev with the 17th-century flowering of Tukaram and Ramdas. His greatest achievement was restoring the corrupted text of the Dnyaneshwari and producing the Eknathi Bhagavat — a Marathi commentary on the 11th chapter of the Bhagavat Purana. He was a householder saint (grihastha), demonstrating that devotion is compatible with domestic life. He is especially known for his inclusivity — eating with untouchables, and challenging caste-based discrimination in devotion. MPSC tests his works, his social reform, and his role in the Warkari chain.

In-Depth

Life

  • Born: 1533 CE, Paithan (ancient Pratishthana), Aurangabad district, Maharashtra — on the banks of the Godavari.
  • Guru: Janardhan Swami (of Devgad/Daulatabad) — a devotee of Dattatreya.
  • Caste: Deshastha Brahmin — but he transcended caste in practice.
  • Family: Married; maintained family life (grihastha) while being a great saint — key distinction from renunciant saints.
  • Death: 1599 CE, Paithan; Jalasamadhi (samadhi in water — merged into the Godavari at Paithan).

Restoration of Dnyaneshwari

  • By the 16th century (300 years after Dnyaneshwar), the text of the Dnyaneshwari had been corrupted by scribal errors, interpolations, and regional variations.
  • Eknath critically edited the text using multiple manuscripts from across Maharashtra and produced a corrected standard version — without which the Dnyaneshwari may have been lost to corruption.
  • This act alone makes Eknath indispensable to Marathi literary history.

Bharud — Eknath's Innovation

  • Bharud = allegorical compositions where the saint adopts the persona of a common person (vendor, washerman, bangle-seller, Muslim fakir, courtesan) to convey devotional and moral messages.
  • Accessible to common people; no Sanskrit; folk language.
  • Example: Eknath adopted the persona of a Muslim fakir and described God-devotion in Islamic idiom — demonstrating his synthesis of Hindu and Muslim spiritual values.
  • Bharud was a distinctly Maharashtrian contribution to Indian literature and folk performance.

Social Reform: Eating with Untouchables

  • Famous story: Eknath invited Mahar (untouchable) community members to a meal at his home in Paithan — a radical act in 16th-century Maharashtra.
  • He argued that Vitthal (God) dwells equally in all beings, including those considered untouchable.
  • He also reportedly performed puja (worship) after eating with them, defying the notion that such contact causes ritual pollution.
  • Such acts anticipate the later social reform movements of Maharashtra (Phule, Shahu, Ambedkar).

Role in Warkari Tradition

  • Compiled and organised the abhangas of previous Warkari saints (Dnyaneshwar, Namdev) into a standard corpus.
  • Trained disciples who continued the tradition; created the organisational structure of Wari more formally.
  • Eknath Sashti (death anniversary, observed as festival); palkhi (palanquin) from Paithan to Pandharpur during Wari.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Born and died at Paithan, Aurangabad (now Chhatrapati Sambhajinagar): ancient capital of Satavahanas; Eknath revitalised its spiritual significance.
  • Godavari river: Jalasamadhi (water samadhi) in the Godavari at Paithan.
  • Restored Dnyaneshwari: without Eknath's editorial work, the primary text of Marathi literature may have been lost.
  • Bharud tradition: uniquely Marathi form; Eknath its primary creator.
  • Eknath Palkhi: from Paithan (Aurangabad) to Pandharpur during Ashadhi Wari; third major Warkari palkhi after Dnyaneshwar and Tukaram.
  • Eknath's challenge to caste (eating with Mahars) is seen as precursor to Maharashtra's social reform tradition.

Exam Traps

  • Eknathi Bhagavat is on Chapter 11 of Bhagavat Purana, NOT the entire Bhagavat Purana.
  • Dnyaneshwari restoration is Eknath's work (16th c.) — not Tukaram or Ramdas.
  • Eknath was a Brahmin but challenged caste in practice; not to be confused with lower-caste saints.
  • Bharud = Eknath's innovation — though later saints also composed Bharuds; it's primarily his contribution.
