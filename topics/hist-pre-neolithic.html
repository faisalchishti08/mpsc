Neolithic age — agriculture, settlements PREMAINS

Overview

The Neolithic Age (New Stone Age, ~7000–1500 BCE in various Indian regions) marks the first food-producing revolution: humans shifted from hunting-gathering to agriculture and animal husbandry, enabling permanent settlements, pottery, and increasingly complex social organisation. Defined by polished stone tools, the Neolithic is foundational to understanding how Indian civilisation evolved from nomadic prehistory toward the Harappan urban phase. MPSC asks on specific sites (Mehrgarh, Burzahom, Chirand, Piklihal), crop evidence, and the significance of the Neolithic transition.

Ash Mounds of South India

A distinctive feature of southern Indian Neolithic is ash mounds — large mounds of burnt cattle dung found at sites like Piklihal, Utnur, Kupgal, Brahmagiri. Interpretations:

  1. Seasonal burning of large cattle-pen enclosures.
  2. Ritual sites or cattle festivals.
  3. Accumulation over centuries of burning cattle dung (fuel + ritual combined).

Ash mounds suggest cattle herding was central to South Indian Neolithic economy and ritual life.

Neolithic Pottery

  • First appearance of pottery in South Asian Neolithic.
  • Burzahom: Coarse grey-black burnished ware.
  • Southern Neolithic: Coarse handmade ware, later finer wheel-thrown types.
  • Pottery enabled storage of grain and food — critical for sedentary lifestyle.

Significance of the Neolithic Revolution (in Indian Context)

  1. Settled life: Villages formed — earliest known villages predate the Harappan civilisation.
  2. Surplus production: Grain surplus → specialisation of labour, trade, social stratification.
  3. Population growth: Stable food supply supported larger populations.
  4. Technological base: Pottery, polished tools, and early metallurgy (in transition to Chalcolithic) evolved from Neolithic base.
  5. Pre-Harappan foundation: Mehrgarh directly evolved into Harappan civilisation over millennia.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Maharashtra's Neolithic is represented through the southern Neolithic tradition extending into Vidarbha, and through the subsequent Chalcolithic cultures (Jorwe, Inamgaon) which developed from Neolithic roots.
  • Research on the Neolithic-Chalcolithic transition in Maharashtra is conducted by Deccan College (Pune) — a globally recognised centre for South Asian prehistoric studies.
  • The crop assemblage of southern Neolithic (millets, ragi) continues to be culturally significant in Maharashtra's Vidarbha and Marathwada food culture.

Key Facts

  • Oldest Neolithic site in South Asia: Mehrgarh (~7000 BCE, Pakistan/Balochistan).
  • Earliest cotton cultivation: Mehrgarh.
  • Burzahom (Kashmir): pit dwellings; dog buried with master; grey pottery.
  • Ash mounds: southern Neolithic sites — Piklihal, Utnur, Kupgal (Karnataka/Telangana).
  • Chirand (Bihar): early rice cultivation; antler bone tools.
  • Polished stone tools = Neolithic (contrast: chipped = Paleolithic, microlithic = Mesolithic).
  • Key crops: Wheat/barley (NW), rice (east/Ganga), millets/ragi (south).

Memory Aids

  • "MBCP": Mehrgarh, Burzahom, Chirand, Piklihal — 4 key Neolithic sites.

Exam Traps

  • Mehrgarh is in present-day Pakistan (Balochistan), NOT India — this catches many aspirants.
  • Polished stone tools = Neolithic; do NOT say polished = Paleolithic.
  • Burzahom's pit dwellings are dug into the ground (semi-subterranean) — unique to Kashmir Neolithic; don't confuse with regular mud houses.
  • Ash mounds are in South India (Karnataka, Telangana) — not in North India.
