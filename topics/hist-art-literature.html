Sanskrit, Prakrit, Tamil and regional literature PREMAINS

Overview

Indian literary tradition spans Vedic Sanskrit (1500 BCE), Epic and Puranic literature, Pali/Prakrit Buddhist-Jain texts, Sangam Tamil (1st–3rd c. CE), and the emergence of regional bhakti literature (7th–17th c.) in Marathi, Hindi, Bengali, Kannada, Telugu and others. MPSC asks about key authors, their languages, works, and the significance of the bhakti literary movement — with special emphasis on Marathi literature.

In-Depth

Vedic and Epic Sanskrit Literature

Vedas (1500–600 BCE): Four Vedas — Rigveda (hymns), Samaveda (melodies), Yajurveda (ritual), Atharvaveda (spells/medicine). Rigveda is the oldest text in any Indo-European language.

Upanishads (800–200 BCE): Philosophical texts appended to Vedas; 108 in number; principal 12–13 (Brihadaranyaka, Chandogya, Kena, Katha, etc.); foundation of Vedanta philosophy. Tat tvam asi ("Thou art that") and Aham Brahmasmi ("I am Brahman") — core philosophical statements.

Epics:

  • Mahabharata: attributed to Vyasa; ~100,000 verses (longest poem in the world); includes Bhagavad Gita (Chapter 6, 18 chapters of philosophical dialogue between Krishna and Arjuna).
  • Ramayana: attributed to Valmiki; ~24,000 verses; 7 kandas; the Adi Kavya (first poem).

Classical Sanskrit Literature (Gupta period, 4th–6th c. CE):

  • Kalidasa: Abhijnanasakuntalam (drama), Meghaduta, Raghuvamsha, Kumarasambhava — considered greatest Sanskrit poet.
  • Banabhatta (Harsha's court): Harshacharita (biography of Harsha), Kadambari (Sanskrit novel).
  • Vishakhadatta: Mudrarakshasa (political drama).
  • Shudraka: Mricchakatika (social drama).

Pali and Prakrit Literature (Buddhist and Jain)

Pali (Buddhist canon):

  • Tripitaka (Pali Canon): Vinaya Pitaka (discipline), Sutta Pitaka (discourses), Abhidhamma Pitaka (philosophy).
  • Jataka tales: 547 stories of the Buddha's past lives; rich source of social history.
  • Dipavamsa, Mahavamsa: Sri Lankan chronicles; key sources for Ashokan history.

Prakrit (Jain canon):

  • Agamas (Jain scriptures): 12 Angas; in Ardhamagadhi Prakrit.
  • Kahakosu and other Jain texts in Shauraseni Prakrit.

Sangam Tamil Literature (1st–3rd c. CE)

The Sangam (academy/assembly) produced the earliest significant body of secular Tamil literature under the patronage of Pandya kings:

  • Three Sangams (tradition): at Madurai; the third produced surviving texts (~500 CE).
  • Surviving texts: Ettuthokai (8 anthologies) and Pattuppattu (10 idylls); themes — love (akam) and war/heroism (puram).
  • Thirukkural (by Thiruvalluvar): ~500 CE; 1,330 couplets; covers Aram (virtue), Porul (polity), Inbam (love); considered the greatest work in Tamil and translatable to 80+ languages.
  • Silappatikaram (by Ilango Adigal): epic; story of Kannagi; considered the first Tamil epic; strong social/judicial themes.
  • Manimekalai: Buddhist Tamil epic (sequel to Silappatikaram).

Marathi Literature — Special Focus

Medieval (Warkari tradition):

  • Dnyaneshwari (1290): Marathi commentary on Bhagavad Gita by Dnyaneshwar (13 years old); established Marathi as a literary language.
  • Amrutanubhav: philosophical work by Dnyaneshwar.
  • Eknathi Bhagavat: retelling of the 11th Skandha of Bhagavata Purana by Eknath.
  • Dasbodh: 20 chapters of spiritual wisdom by Samartha Ramdas (Shivaji's guru); deals with practical ethics, statecraft, devotion.
  • Thousands of abhangas by Tukaram, Namdev — the backbone of Warkari singing tradition (kirtan).

Modern (19th–20th c.):

  • Balshastri Jambhekar (1812–1846): first Marathi journalist; started Darpan (1832) — the first Marathi newspaper.
  • Jyotirao Phule (1827–1890): Gulamgiri (1873) — exposed caste oppression; Shetkaryacha Asud; radical social reformer.
  • Sane Guruji (Pandurang Sadashiv Sane, 1899–1950): Shyamchi Aai — most beloved Marathi novel.
  • Vishnu Sakharam Khandekar: Jnanpith Award winner (1974) for Yayati — first Marathi Jnanpith.

Maharashtra Connection

Directly central:

  • Marathi literature from Dnyaneshwar (1290) to the modern novel is among India's richest regional traditions.
  • Jnanpith Awards to Marathi: V.S. Khandekar (1974), V.V. Shirwadkar "Kusumagraj" (1987), Vinda Karandikar (2006), Bhalchandra Nemade (2014).
  • Dnyaneshwar, Eknath, Tukaram, Ramdas — the four pillars of Marathi spiritual literature; their works still recited in Warkari tradition.
  • Darpan (1832) — first Marathi newspaper; pioneered Marathi prose journalism.

  • Thiruvalluvar = Thirukkural = 1,330 = Tamil wisdom.
  • Marathi milestones: "Dnyaneshwari 1290 → Darpan 1832 → Jnanpith 1974."
  • Hindi bhakti: "K-S-T-M" = Kabir, Surdas, Tulsidas, Mirabai.

Exam Traps

  • Dnyaneshwari (1290) was composed when Dnyaneshwar was ~13 years old — this fact sometimes appears as a question.
  • Thirukkural has 1,330 couplets — not 108 or 500 as sometimes stated in wrong options.
  • Silappatikaram (Ilango Adigal) ≠ Manimekalai (Sattanar) — two different epics by different authors.
  • Dasbodh was by Samartha Ramdas (Shivaji's guru), not by Tukaram or Dnyaneshwar.
