Ashtapradhan council PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

The Ashtapradhan (literally "eight ministers") was the council of eight ministers established by Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj to administer the Maratha kingdom. Unlike the Mughal wazir system with one all-powerful prime minister, the Ashtapradhan distributed power across specialists — an innovative arrangement blending Hindu political thought with practical statecraft. MPSC tests the eight designations, their functions, and how this system differed from the later hereditary Peshwa model.

Key Features of the Ashtapradhan

  • Non-hereditary (under Shivaji): All eight posts were appointed by the king, NOT hereditary. Ministers served at the king's pleasure.
  • Not a cabinet: They did NOT collectively deliberate — each minister reported directly to Shivaji; there was no collective cabinet responsibility.
  • Senapati and Peshwa most important: Military and administrative heads were the two most powerful. The Senapati held the field command; the Peshwa managed civil governance.
  • First Peshwa: Moropant Trimbak Pingle — appointed by Shivaji; a Brahmin; he was a minister (not dynastic).
  • First Senapati: Prataprao Gujar — won the Battle of Salher (1672); later died gallantly at Nesari (1674) against Bahlol Khan.

Post-Shivaji Change: Rise of Hereditary Peshwa

  • After Shivaji's death (1680) and the turbulent reigns of Sambhaji (1680–1689) and Rajaram (1689–1700), the Maratha state weakened.
  • Under Shahu Maharaj (Shivaji's grandson; 1707–1749), effective power passed to the Peshwa — specifically to the Bhat family of Chitpavan Brahmins: Balaji Vishwanath Bhat (first hereditary Peshwa, 1713), followed by Bajirao I, Balaji Bajirao (Nanasaheb), Madhavrao, and finally Baji Rao II.
  • The Peshwa became hereditary and all-powerful; the other Ashtapradhan ministers became subordinate — the "Ashtapradhan" effectively became a Peshwai system.

Maharashtra Connection

The Ashtapradhan system has been discussed extensively in the context of India's early modern administrative history. Ambedkar criticised the Peshwai (the later Brahmin-dominant system) for caste oppression of Mahars and lower castes — the Mahad Satyagraha (1927) and the burning of Manusmriti were direct protests against the social order perpetuated under Peshwai. This links Shivaji's administration to Maharashtra's social reform movement.

  • Post-1713: Balaji Vishwanath Bhat = first hereditary Peshwa (under Shahu Maharaj)
  • Peshwai: Power shifted from Chhatrapati to Peshwa (Chitpavan Brahmin family)
  • Council of 8: P-A-S-M-S-S-N-P (Peshwa, Amatya, Sachiv, Mantri, Senapati, Sumant, Nyayadhish, Panditrao)

Memory Aids

  • P-A-S-M × 2: Peshwa-Amatya-Sachiv-Mantri (civil) + Senapati-Sumant-Nyayadhish-Panditrao (military/foreign/justice/religion)
  • "PASS-MSNP" → P-A-S-S (finance, sec, mantri) → SSNP (army, foreign, justice, pandit)
  • First Peshwa: Moropant Pingle (M-P) — minister under Shivaji; NOT same as Peshwai Bhats

Exam Traps

  • Peshwa under Shivaji = appointed minister (Moropant Pingle); Hereditary Peshwa starts with Balaji Vishwanath (1713) — two completely different things.
  • Senapati (army chief) ≠ Peshwa (PM) — do NOT swap roles.
  • Ashtapradhan did NOT function as a "cabinet" in the modern sense — NO collective decision-making.
