Gupta empire — Samudragupta, Chandragupta II PREMAINS

Overview

The Gupta Empire (c. 319–543 CE) was founded by Chandragupta I and reached its peak under Samudragupta and Chandragupta II (Vikramaditya), controlling most of the Indian subcontinent. The Guptas mark the beginning of India's "Classical Age" — a period of political unification, Sanskrit literary renaissance, and scientific achievement. MPSC tests the rise and key rulers' military campaigns, inscriptions, and administrative features as both Prelims fact questions and Mains analytical themes.

In-Depth

Origins and Early Guptas

The Guptas originated from the Magadha (Bihar) region. Their early history is obscure:

  • Sri Gupta (c. 240–280 CE): founder of the Gupta line; probably a local chieftain; no coins of his own found.
  • Ghatotkacha (c. 280–319 CE): son of Sri Gupta; still a subordinate/chieftain.
  • Chandragupta I (c. 319–335 CE): first Gupta to use the title Maharajadhiraja (Great King of Kings); considered the real founder of the empire. He married Kumaradevi, a Lichchhavi princess (important clan from Nepal/Bihar), and this alliance is commemorated on his gold coins showing him and Kumaradevi — earliest Gupta coin type. The Gupta Era begins 319 CE with his coronation.

Samudragupta (c. 335–375 CE) — "Napoleon of India"

Samudragupta is the greatest military expansionist of the Gupta dynasty. The primary source is the Allahabad Pillar Inscription (Prayag Prashasti) composed by his court poet Harishena in ornate Sanskrit (a prasasti — eulogy). It describes his campaigns:

Northern campaigns (Aryavarta):

  • Defeated and annexed 9 northern kings: Rudradeva, Matila, Nagadatta, Chandravarman, Ganapati Naga, Nandin, Balavarman, Nagasena, Achyuta.

Southern campaigns (Dakshinapatha — Atavirajyas):

  • 12 southern rulers were "captured and then released" — the dharmavijayi policy of capture-release-tribute.
  • Rulers included: Mahendra of Kosala, Vyaghraraja of Mahakantara, Mantaraja of Kaurale, Mahendragiri of Pishtapura, Svamidatta of Kottura, Damana of Erandapalla, Vishnugopa of Kanchi (Pallava), Nilaraja of Avamukta, Hastivarman of Vengi, Ugrasena of Palakka, Kubera of Devarashtra, Dhananjaya of Kushtalapura.
  • This "southern campaign" model — capture-and-release — became the Gupta template for extending influence without direct annexation.

Forest kingdoms (Atavirajyas): tribes in central India made subordinate.

Frontier states: kings of Assam, Nepal, and island kingdoms (Sri Lanka's king Meghavarna sent gifts) acknowledged his suzerainty.

Samudragupta was also a poet and musician — his epithet kaviraj (king of poets) appears in the Allahabad inscription. He played the vina (depicted on his coins).

Chandragupta II (Vikramaditya) — c. 375–415 CE

Chandragupta II expanded the empire westward by defeating the Western Satraps (Shaka rulers) of Ujjain and Gujarat. Key events:

  • Defeat of Rudrasimha III (last Western Satrap) c. 395–415 CE — ended Shaka control of Saurashtra/Gujarat/Malwa; Chandragupta took the title Vikramaditya.
  • Established a second capital at Ujjain after the Shaka conquest — strategic control of western trade routes.
  • His court at Pataliputra/Ujjain hosted the Navaratna (Nine Gems) — including Kalidasa (poet-dramatist), Aryabhata (astronomer-mathematician), Varahamihira (astronomer), Amarasimha (lexicographer), Dhanvantari (physician), Vararuchi (grammarian), Vetala Bhatta (magician), Ghatakarpara, Shanku.
  • The Udayagiri Caves (MP) inscription and the Mehrauli Iron Pillar (Delhi, now in Qutb complex) — Sanskrit inscription mentioning a king "Chandra" who defeated the Vahlikas (Bactria-region enemies) and crossed the seven mouths of the Sindhu (Indus) — most scholars identify this as Chandragupta II.
  • Chinese pilgrim Faxian (Fa Hien) visited India during Chandragupta II's reign (c. 399–414 CE) and described a prosperous, peaceful kingdom with no corporal punishment.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. However, Samudragupta's southern campaigns passed through the Deccan corridor. His campaign list includes Mahendragiri (in Odisha-Maharashtra-Andhra border region) and Devarashtra (possibly near the Vidarba/Berar area of Maharashtra). After the Gupta decline, the Vakataka dynasty (Maharashtra) became the dominant power — and Chandragupta II's daughter Prabhavati Gupta was married to Vakataka king Rudrasena II, creating a Gupta-Vakataka alliance critical for Deccan history and the Ajanta caves.

Key Facts

  • Gupta Era begins: 319 CE (Chandragupta I's coronation).
  • Allahabad Pillar Inscription (Prayag Prashasti): composed by Harishena; describes Samudragupta's campaigns.
  • Samudragupta's title: kaviraj (king of poets); played the vina.
  • Chandragupta II's title: Vikramaditya; defeated last Western Satrap Rudrasimha III.
  • Faxian visited India: c. 399–414 CE (Chandragupta II's reign).
  • Mehrauli Iron Pillar: 99%+ pure iron; "Chandra" inscription — likely Chandragupta II.
  • Navaratna: nine gems at Chandragupta II's/Vikramaditya's court; included Kalidasa, Aryabhata.
  • Nalanda monastery: founded by Kumaragupta I.

Memory Aids

  • "SGC = Sri, Ghatot, Chandra I" — three early Guptas before the imperial era.
  • "SCC = Samudragupta, Chandra II, Kumaragupta" — peak Gupta rulers.
  • Allahabad Pillar = Samudragupta (not Ashoka's pillar — two inscriptions on same pillar).
  • "Faxian visits Chandragupta II" — Chinese pilgrim = Gupta golden age.
  • Iron Pillar: "99% pure, 1600 years without rust" — still in Delhi.

Exam Traps

  • The Allahabad (Prayag) Pillar has two inscriptions: an Ashokan inscription (Mauryan era) AND Harishena's Prayag Prashasti (Gupta era) — don't confuse them.
  • Vikramaditya is a title of Chandragupta II, not Samudragupta.
  • Faxian visited during Chandragupta II's reign; Xuanzang (Hiuen Tsang) visited during Harsha's reign — two different pilgrims, two different periods.
  • Nalanda was founded by Kumaragupta I (Gupta period); it reached its peak under Harsha and later kings.
