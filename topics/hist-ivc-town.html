Town planning and architecture PREMAINS

Overview

The town planning and architecture of Harappan cities is their most celebrated achievement — a grid-layout of streets, standardised baked-brick construction, a sophisticated underground drainage system, and public buildings like the Great Bath at Mohenjo-daro. Harappan town planning was unparalleled in the ancient world for its public hygiene infrastructure and stands as the earliest example of deliberate urban planning in South Asia. MPSC asks heavily on specific structures (Great Bath, Granary), site-specific features, and what Harappan urbanism reveals.

In-Depth

Grid Layout and Street Planning

  • Cities divided into two distinct zones: a Citadel (raised platform, west) housing public/administrative buildings and an Lower Town (east) with residential areas.
  • Streets were laid on a cardinal grid (north-south and east-west) — major streets ~9–10 m wide, with lanes.
  • Intersections are right-angled — systematic urban design.
  • Evidence at: Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Dholavira, Kalibangan.

Standardised Bricks

A remarkable Harappan feature: bricks everywhere have a standard ratio of 1:2:4 (height:width:length). Common size: ~7×14×28 cm (varies slightly by site). This standardisation across thousands of km suggests:

  1. A centralised authority setting standards, OR
  2. Powerful trade guilds/craft associations transmitting standards.

Drainage and Sanitation

Harappan drainage is the most advanced of any contemporary civilisation:

  • Every house had a brick-lined drain connecting to covered street drains.
  • Street drains emptied into larger collector drains leading outside the city.
  • Manholes (inspection chambers) at regular intervals — the earliest known sewer manholes.
  • Evidence at Mohenjo-daro, Lothal (Gujarat), Dholavira (Gujarat).
  • By contrast, Mesopotamian cities (Ur, Uruk) and Egyptian cities lacked such systematic underground drainage.

House Architecture

  • Private houses: 1–2 storeys; rooms opening into central courtyards; no windows facing the street (privacy culture).
  • Each house had a bathroom and drainage connection.
  • Wells: Many private wells; some public wells.
  • Doors opened into side lanes, not main streets.
  • No temples identified with certainty (contrast with Mesopotamia which had ziggurats) — religious life may have been home-centred.

Dholavira — A Distinctive Harappan City

Dholavira (Kutch, Gujarat; excavated R.S. Bisht from 1990) is unique among Harappan cities:

  • Three-part division: Citadel, Middle Town, Lower Town.
  • A large rectangular signboard with Harappan script symbols (possibly the world's earliest signboard) found near the northern gate.
  • Elaborate water conservation: 16 reservoirs, dams across seasonal streams.
  • UNESCO World Heritage Site (2021).

Maharashtra Connection

Maharashtra's contribution to Harappan urbanism study is indirect but significant:

  • Daimabad (Ahmednagar) has a Harappan-phase layer showing contact with northern Harappan urban culture — bricks and artefacts.
  • The Deccan College (Pune) has contributed significantly to excavation methodology and chronological dating of Harappan-related Chalcolithic sites in Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • Great Bath: Mohenjo-daro; 12×7×2.4 m; bitumen-sealed.
  • Brick ratio: 1:2:4 standardised across all Harappan sites.
  • Fire altars: unique to Kalibangan (Rajasthan).
  • Dockyard (disputed): Lothal (Gujarat) — S.R. Rao's interpretation.
  • Water reservoirs: Dholavira (16 reservoirs) — Gujarat.
  • Grid layout: north-south + east-west streets; right-angle intersections.
  • No confirmed temples; no monumental religious architecture.
  • Dholavira UNESCO WHS: 2021.

Memory Aids

  • "Great Bath = Mohenjo-daro, Fire Altars = Kalibangan, Dockyard = Lothal, Water = Dholavira."
  • Brick ratio: "1-2-4: thin, medium, long."
  • Drainage: Harappan = best ancient drainage = manholes + covered street drains + house connections.

Exam Traps

  • Great Bath is at Mohenjo-daro, NOT Harappa — commonly confused.
  • Fire altars are at Kalibangan, NOT Mohenjo-daro — Kalibangan is the exception.
  • The Lothal dockyard interpretation is disputed — some call it a water tank; MCQs often ask which feature is "unique to Lothal" = the dockyard.
  • Harappan cities had no confirmed temples — this sets them apart from Mesopotamia; don't assume temples were present.

  1. [Asked] The 'Great Bath' of the Harappan civilisation is located at which site? — Mohenjo-daro
  2. [Asked] Fire altars, suggesting a ritual fire worship practice, have been found at which Harappan site? — Kalibangan (Rajasthan)

  4. [Expected] The elaborate water conservation system with 16 reservoirs is a distinctive feature of which Harappan site? — Dholavira (Gujarat)
  5. [Expected] The Harappan drainage system is considered superior to contemporary civilisations because? — Covered drains, manholes, household drainage connections — systematic public sanitation

  1. [Expected] "Harappan town planning was the most sophisticated of any Bronze Age civilisation." Critically examine with evidence.
  2. [Expected] Compare the urban planning features of Mohenjo-daro and Dholavira as examples of Harappan city design.
  3. [Expected] What does the absence of confirmed temples in Harappan cities suggest about religion and governance in the IVC?
