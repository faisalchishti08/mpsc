Babur and foundation; Humayun PREMAINS

Overview

Babur (1483–1530) founded the Mughal Empire in India with his victory at the First Battle of Panipat (1526) over Ibrahim Lodi — beginning three centuries of Mughal rule. His son and successor Humayun (1530–1556) lost the empire to Sher Shah Suri (1540) and regained it shortly before his death, setting the stage for Akbar's consolidation. MPSC asks about Panipat battles, Babur's tactics, Humayun's exile and the Baburnama.

In-Depth

Babur — Background

Babur was born in 1483 in Fergana (present Uzbekistan), a descendant of Timur (Tamerlane) on his father's side and Genghis Khan on his mother's side. He failed to hold Samarkand (his prime ambition), and turned south into the Indian subcontinent. He made five incursions into India before his decisive campaign.

First Battle of Panipat (21 April 1526)

  • Babur vs Ibrahim Lodi (last Delhi Sultan).
  • Babur's army (~12,000) was vastly outnumbered by Lodi's force (~100,000 + 100 elephants).
  • Babur used two decisive innovations: Ustad Ali and Mustafa's artillery (gunpowder/cannon — first decisive use in north India) and the tulughma (flanking) tactic (Rumi/Ottoman tactic of encircling the enemy's flanks with mobile cavalry).
  • Ibrahim Lodi was killed; Babur captured Delhi and Agra.

Second Battle of Panipat (context for exam)

Not Babur's battle — fought in 1556 between Hemu and Akbar/Bairam Khan (restoring Mughal rule). Don't confuse with the First.

Babur's Other Battles

  • Battle of Khanwa (1527): vs Rana Sanga (Sangram Singh) of Mewar — Babur proclaimed Ghazi (holy warrior) and won despite Rana Sanga's formidable alliance.
  • Battle of Chanderi (1528): vs Medini Rai of Malwa.
  • Battle of Ghaghra (1529): vs Afghan confederacy of Bihar and Bengal.

By 1529 Babur controlled north India from Kabul to Bengal.

The Baburnama

Babur wrote his memoirs in Chaghatai Turkic: the Baburnama (Tuzuk-i-Baburi) — the first true autobiography in Islamic literature; a detailed account of his campaigns, flora, fauna and court. Later translated into Persian under Akbar.

Humayun (1530–1540, 1555–1556)

Babur died in 1530; Humayun inherited an unstable empire. Key events:

Humayun's Tomb (Delhi) — built by his widow Haji Begum (1570) — is the first great double-domed Mughal monument, UNESCO World Heritage Site and precursor to the Taj Mahal.

Significance of Babur's Rule

  • Introduced gunpowder and cannon as decisive battlefield tools in India.
  • Tulughma and araba (field artillery on carts) tactics transformed Indian warfare.
  • Laid the territorial base of the Mughal empire.
  • Persian culture and Timurid aesthetics entered India through Babur's court.

Maharashtra Connection

Limited direct connection. However, Babur's defeat of Ibrahim Lodi ended the Delhi Sultanate whose influence reached the Deccan. Humayun's exile at the Safavid Persian court (1540–55) brought back Persian artistic influences that shaped Mughal art — visible in the Deccan later. The Second Battle of Panipat (1556) — Humayun's legacy — allowed Akbar to consolidate power and eventually turn Mughal attention to the Deccan.

Key Facts

  • Babur born: 1483, Fergana; descended from Timur (father) and Genghis (mother).
  • First Battle of Panipat: 21 April 1526 — Babur vs Ibrahim Lodi.
  • Decisive tactics: artillery (tulughma) — first large-scale cannon use in north India.
  • Battle of Khanwa (1527): vs Rana Sanga (Mewar).
  • Baburnama: autobiography in Chaghatai Turkic.
  • Humayun defeated at Kanauj (1540) by Sher Shah Suri.
  • Humayun's exile: Safavid court (Shah Tahmasp), Persia, 1540–1555.
  • Humayun's Tomb built 1570 by Haji Begum — first garden tomb in India.
  • Humayun died 1556 — fell from library steps.

Exam Traps

  • First Panipat (1526) ≠ Second Panipat (1556) — First is Babur vs Ibrahim Lodi; Second is Akbar/Bairam Khan vs Hemu.
  • Humayun's Tomb was built after his death by his wife, not by Humayun himself.
  • Babur wrote in Chaghatai Turkic, not Persian — Akbar had it translated to Persian.
  • Khanwa (1527) was Babur vs Rana Sanga, not Ibrahim Lodi's surviving forces.
