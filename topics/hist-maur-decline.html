Decline of the Mauryas PREMAINS

Overview

The decline of the Mauryan Empire (after 232 BCE) was a complex, multi-causal process — not a single event — leading to its final collapse around 185 BCE when Pushyamitra Shunga assassinated Brihadratha, the last Mauryan emperor. MPSC asks this topic to test understanding of causation in ancient Indian history: economic strain, administrative over-reach, succession crises, and the role of Brahmanical reaction are all debated by historians.

Causes of Decline — Multiple Theories

1. Ashoka's pacifist policy (Romila Thapar's view) Ashoka's emphasis on dhamma (non-violence, religious tolerance, renunciation of conquest) allegedly weakened the military and demoralised the army by forbidding aggressive campaigns. However, critics note the empire survived ~47 years after Ashoka without immediate collapse — this was likely a contributing, not sole, factor.

2. Economic strain and agrarian exhaustion Maintaining a massive army, bureaucracy, welfare works (wells, hospitals, rest houses), and Ashoka's extensive Buddhist/pillar-building programme drained the treasury. Punch-marked coins show a decline in silver purity (debased coinage) indicating fiscal stress. Agricultural expansion had limits in sub-Himalayan and Deccan margins.

3. Administrative over-centralisation The empire was vast (extending from Afghanistan to Tamil Nadu borders) but lacked the communication technology to sustain tight central control. Governors (Kumaras) in distant provinces (Taxila, Ujjain, Tosali) became increasingly autonomous. After a strong emperor like Ashoka, weaker successors could not maintain the system.

4. Brahmanical reaction — the Pushyamitra thesis H.C. Raychaudhuri and others argue Ashoka's Buddhist patronage alienated Brahmin administrators. Pushyamitra Shunga — the Mauryan commander-in-chief (Senapati) — assassinated Brihadratha during a military review (parade). Pushyamitra was a Brahmin and performed Ashvamedha (horse sacrifice) to legitimise his rule — seen as a Brahminical reassertion. However, this "anti-Buddhist reaction" theory is debated; Pushyamitra did not systematically persecute Buddhists (Sanchi Stupa's toranas were built under Shunga rule).

5. Foreign invasions The Bactrian Greeks (Indo-Greeks) under Demetrius invaded northwestern India (c. 200–185 BCE), detaching Gandhara and pushing into the Punjab. This was both a cause and symptom of Mauryan weakness. The empire had already lost effective control of the northwest before Pushyamitra's coup.

6. Weak successors After Ashoka, no single ruler controlled the whole empire. The empire was divided, with different branches of the family ruling different regions (western India, Deccan). None had Chandragupta's or Ashoka's administrative genius.

The 185 BCE Coup

Pushyamitra Shunga was the Senapati (commander-in-chief) of the Mauryan army under Brihadratha. Harsha's Harshacharita (written ~630 CE) records that Pushyamitra killed Brihadratha in public while reviewing the army, then proclaimed himself king. This founded the Shunga dynasty, which ruled Magadha (though a reduced territory) until ~73 BCE.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. However, the decline of the Mauryas is the direct context for the rise of the Satavahana dynasty in the Deccan (including Maharashtra). The Satavahanas (from the Andhra-Maharashtra region) filled the power vacuum after Mauryan control receded from the Deccan. This makes the Mauryan decline the essential background to Maharashtra's own early medieval history.

Key Facts

  • Ashoka died: 232 BCE; last Mauryan (Brihadratha): killed 185 BCE.
  • Pushyamitra Shunga: Mauryan Senapati who assassinated Brihadratha.
  • Pushyamitra performed: Ashvamedha (horse sacrifice) — 2 times, per Puranas.
  • Bactrian Greeks (Demetrius) invaded NW India: c. 200–185 BCE.
  • Deccan vacuum → Satavahanas rose.
  • Duration of Mauryan decline: approximately 47 years (232–185 BCE).
  • Source: Pusyamitra's coup described in Harshacharita (Bana, 7th century CE).

Exam Traps

  • Pushyamitra was NOT a foreign invader — he was the Mauryan Senapati (internal coup).
  • Ashoka's "pro-Buddhist" policy as the cause is a debated theory (Thapar), not a settled fact — MPSC options sometimes present it as the only cause.
  • The Shunga dynasty did NOT wipe out Buddhism — Sanchi's toranas were built under Shungas.
  • Do not confuse the Indo-Greek invasion (external) with Pushyamitra's coup (internal) — both happened around 185 BCE but are separate events.
