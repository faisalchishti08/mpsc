Mughal administration — Mansabdari, Zabt PREMAINS

Overview

Mughal administration (1526–1707) was a sophisticated Persian-Turkic system that evolved under successive rulers, most fully developed under Akbar (1556–1605). It rested on the Mansabdari system for military-civil service, the Zabt (Todar Mal's) revenue system, and a hierarchical provincial structure. It influenced British colonial administration and is among the most exam-tested topics in MPSC GS Paper 1.

In-Depth

Central Administration

The Emperor was the supreme authority — source of all law, justice and military command. He was aided by four principal ministers:

Qazi-ul-Quzat (Chief Justice) administered the judicial system; the Emperor also held mazalim (court of complaints) for direct petitions.

Mansabdari System (Akbar's Core Innovation)

Every officer of the state — civil and military — held a mansab (rank):

  • Zat rank: personal status and salary (from 10 to 10,000 zat, higher for princes).
  • Sawar rank: number of cavalry to be maintained (always ≤ zat rank).
  • Three categories of mansabdars: those maintaining sawar equal to zat, half, and one-third.
  • Mansabs were not hereditary — granted and revoked by the Emperor.
  • Payment: either via jagir (revenue assignment) or naqdi (cash). Most preferred jagir.

Jagirdar vs Zamindar:

  • Jagirdar: Mughal revenue assignee for troop maintenance; rights temporary.
  • Zamindar: hereditary local landholder who paid revenue to the state; not a Mughal appointment.

Under Aurangzeb: jagir crisis — more mansabdars than available jagirs; led to corruption and weakening of central authority.

Akbar created 15 subas (provinces); Aurangzeb expanded to 21 subas (adding Deccan provinces).

Revenue System — Zabt (Ain-i-Dahsala)

Developed by Raja Todar Mal under Akbar (formalised 1580):

  • Land measurement: by ilahi gaz (standard yard); land classified as polaj, parauti, chachar, banjar.
  • Actual yield x prevailing price over 10-year average = state demand (about 1/3 of produce).
  • Zabt system = cash assessment; batai system = crop sharing (used where Zabt wasn't feasible).
  • Revenue collected by amalguzars and patwaris; kabuliyat (agreement) with peasants.
  • Other revenue: customs (dastur), forest taxes, tribute from vassal states.

Military Organization

  • Standing army maintained at the centre (Ahadis — gentleman troopers directly under the Emperor).
  • Mansabdari cavalry formed the bulk.
  • Artillery (tufang) introduced since Babur; significantly developed under Akbar.
  • Elephant corps and navy (limited).
  • Mir Bakhshi oversaw the Daag o Chehra (branding of horses + descriptive rolls of soldiers) to prevent substitution — adopted from Sher Shah Suri.

Judicial System

  • Emperor was the highest court.
  • Qazi (Islamic law) at each level; applied sharia for Muslims.
  • Hindus generally governed by their own customary law; panchayas for local disputes.
  • Kotwal (city police chief) maintained law and order; dealt with petty crime.
  • Punishment system included fines, imprisonment, corporal punishment, execution.

Karkhanas (Imperial Workshops)

State-run workshops that produced luxury goods — textiles, carpets, arms, jewellery. Supervised by the Mir Saman. The best artisans worked exclusively for the royal court.

Maharashtra Connection

Akbar's Deccan campaigns brought Maharashtra under partial Mughal control; Aurangzeb ruled from the Deccan for 25 years with his headquarters at Aurangabad (founded by Malik Ambar; expanded by Aurangzeb). The Mughal provincial system influenced Maratha administration — the Peshwa's administration borrowed the pargana and revenue classification concepts. Todar Mal's Zabt influenced revenue measurement in Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • Four central ministers: Wazir, Mir Bakhshi, Sadr-us-Sudur, Mir Saman.
  • Mansabdari: Zat (personal) + Sawar (cavalry) — not hereditary.
  • Akbar's provinces: 15 subas; Aurangzeb expanded to 21.
  • Revenue: Ain-i-Dahsala (1580), architect Todar Mal, fixed at 1/3 of produce.
  • Daag o Chehra — horse-branding + soldier rolls (from Sher Shah).
  • Ahadis = gentleman troopers directly under the Emperor.
  • Kotwal = city police chief.
  • Jagir crisis under Aurangzeb → major cause of Mughal decline.

Exam Traps

  • Mir Bakhshi was NOT Commander-in-Chief — he was paymaster/administrator of military; actual battlefield command was Emperor's or appointed generals'.
  • Jagir ≠ ownership — it was a revenue assignment, not land ownership; could be revoked.
  • Zabt system ≠ Todar Mal's invention — Todar Mal implemented and systematised a pre-existing measurement approach; Sher Shah was the real originator.
  • Diwan of a suba reported independently to the centre, not to the Subedar — this was a deliberate check on provincial governors.

Questions

Prelims (MCQ-style):

  1. [Asked] Which Mughal official was responsible for military administration (recruitment and pay) but was NOT commander-in-chief? — Mir Bakhshi
  2. [Asked] The Ain-i-Dahsala revenue system was introduced by which minister of Akbar? — Raja Todar Mal (1580)
  3. [Expected] In the Mansabdari system, 'sawar' rank determined? — The number of cavalry (horsemen) to be maintained
  4. [Expected] At the provincial (suba) level, who independently reported revenue matters to the central government? — Diwan (not the Subedar)
  5. [Expected] The 'Daag o Chehra' system (branding of horses and soldier rolls) was borrowed by Akbar from? — Sher Shah Suri

Mains (descriptive):

  1. [Asked] Critically analyse the Mansabdari system of the Mughal Empire. How far did it serve as an effective instrument of imperial control? (~250 words)
  2. [Expected] Explain the Zabt (Ain-i-Dahsala) revenue system of Akbar. How did it improve on earlier revenue practices?
  3. [Expected] Examine the checks and balances built into Mughal provincial administration.
