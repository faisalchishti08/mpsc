Soil types — black regur, laterite, alluvial PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

Maharashtra has four main soil types — black cotton soil (regur), laterite, alluvial, and red/mixed soils — each tied to a specific geological and geographic zone. Black soil (regur) dominates over 60% of the state and is the foundation of Maharashtra's cotton and sugarcane agriculture. Soil types, their districts, crops, and formation are a recurring MPSC Geography topic that also connects to agriculture and environment.

Black Cotton Soil (Regur) — Dominant Soil

  • Covers over 60% of Maharashtra's land area — the largest soil type.
  • Formed from the Deccan Trap basalt volcanic rocks (~65 million years old), making it the product of the same ancient volcanic event.
  • Key property: Self-ploughing (also called self-churning) — shrinks and forms deep cracks in dry weather; swells and becomes sticky in wet weather; this natural churning aerates the soil without human tillage.
  • Also called "regur" (Telugu/official term) or "black cotton soil" (British colonial term) or "Kaali Maati" in Marathi.
  • Chemical: High montmorillonite clay content; good moisture retention; high fertility for cotton and sugarcane; can have calcium carbonate nodules (kankars).
  • Colour: Black to dark grey due to humus and iron-titanium compounds.
  • Main districts: Nagpur, Amravati, Wardha (classic cotton belt), Jalna, Aurangabad, Solapur, Nashik.

Laterite Soil — Konkan Hills

  • Found on the Sahyadri slopes and hills of Konkan (Ratnagiri, Sindhudurg) and some Satara-Kolhapur hill areas.
  • Formed by intense leaching in high-rainfall tropical conditions — silica and lime are washed away, leaving iron and aluminium oxides (hence red-brick colour).
  • Hardens on drying — this characteristic is used in construction (laterite blocks/bricks are a traditional Konkan building material, visible in old colonial-era and fort structures).
  • Very low fertility for food crops; suitable for cashew, areca nut, rubber, coconut plantations.

Alluvial Soil — River Valleys

  • Found in river valleys and Konkan coastal plains: Konkan rice-growing belt, Tapi valley (Khandesh — banana), Krishna-Bhima plains.
  • Divided into Khadar (newer, lighter, fertile, flood plains) and Bhangar (older, harder, less fertile, uplands).
  • High fertility due to regular sediment deposits; suitable for intensive cropping.

Red Soil — Eastern Vidarbha

  • Found in the eastern forested districts (Gadchiroli, Gondia, Chandrapur) where crystalline rocks underlie the surface.
  • Also in fringe areas of Nashik and Ahmednagar where basalt gives way to granite.
  • Less productive; supports tribal subsistence farming.

Soil Erosion in Maharashtra

  • Vidarbha and Marathwada: Wind erosion and sheet erosion from over-tilling black soil.
  • Konkan: Severe gully erosion on Sahyadri slopes; laterite soils are especially vulnerable once the forest cover is removed.
  • Watershed development: Maharashtra government runs Jalyukt Shivar Abhiyan (2015–) for water conservation and soil erosion control in drought-prone areas.

Maharashtra Connection

The black soil cotton economy of Vidarbha (Wardha, Nagpur, Yavatmal) has historical roots — the British-era railway opened Vidarbha's cotton to global markets via Bombay, and cotton cooperatives followed. Maharashtra's chronic farmer crisis in Vidarbha is directly tied to cotton cultivation on black soil — input-intensive, rain-dependent, price-volatile. The Jalyukt Shivar Abhiyan (watershed development scheme) targeted black and red soil areas for micro-watershed creation to reduce runoff and increase soil moisture.

Key Facts

  • Black cotton soil: >60% of Maharashtra; formed from Deccan Trap basalt; self-ploughing; main crop: cotton, sugarcane
  • Laterite soil: Konkan hills (Ratnagiri, Sindhudurg); hardens on exposure; main crop: cashew, coconut
  • Alluvial soil: River valleys (Konkan coast, Tapi, Krishna-Bhima plains); most fertile; main crop: rice, banana
  • Red soil: Eastern Vidarbha (Gadchiroli, Gondia, Chandrapur); crystalline rock origin
  • Self-ploughing = montmorillonite clay expansion-contraction
  • Jalyukt Shivar Abhiyan: 2015 — watershed scheme for Deccan plateau

Exam Traps

  • Black soil's self-ploughing ability is due to montmorillonite clay, NOT any biological process.
  • Laterite soils are found on Konkan hills (high rainfall, leaching), NOT the Konkan coastal plains — alluvial soils dominate the coastal plains.
  • Red soil in Maharashtra = eastern Vidarbha (Gondia, Gadchiroli) — do NOT place red soil in Nashik or Pune.
