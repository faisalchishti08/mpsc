Slave/Mamluk dynasty PREMAINS

Overview

The Slave (Mamluk) dynasty (1206–1290 CE) was the first dynasty of the Delhi Sultanate, founded by Qutb-ud-din Aibak after the death of Muhammad Ghori (1206 CE). The term "Slave" refers not to social status but to the mamluk (slave-soldier) system by which Turkish rulers elevated loyal military slaves to the highest offices. The dynasty established the administrative and cultural foundations of the Sultanate — the Qutb Minar is their most enduring monument. MPSC tests key rulers, their administrative innovations, and the Qutb complex.

In-Depth

Foundation and Qutb-ud-din Aibak (1206–1210 CE)

Qutb-ud-din Aibak was Muhammad Ghori's most trusted general and viceroy of India. On Ghori's assassination (1206 CE), Aibak proclaimed himself Sultan at Lahore. Key facts:

  • Title: Lakh Baksh ("Giver of Lakhs") for his generosity.
  • Began construction of the Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque (Delhi) — the first mosque in India — and the Qutb Minar.
  • Also built the Adhai Din ka Jhonpra mosque (Ajmer).
  • Died in 1210 CE falling from a horse while playing polo (chaugan).
  • Reigned only 4 years as Sultan; short but foundational.

Iltutmish (1211–1236 CE) — The Real Consolidator

Shamsuddin Iltutmish is considered the true organiser of the Delhi Sultanate:

  • Completed the Qutb Minar (Aibak built 3 storeys; Iltutmish added 2 more).
  • Organised the Iqta system: assigned revenue-yielding territories (iqtas) to military officers (muqtis/walis) in lieu of salary — the foundation of Sultanate fiscal administration.
  • Established the Turkish nobility (Chahalgani or "Group of Forty") — an oligarchy of 40 Turkish slave officers who supported and checked the Sultan.
  • Introduced silver tanka (coin) and copper jital — regularised coinage.
  • Received the Caliphate's investiture (1229 CE) from the Abbasid Caliph of Baghdad, legitimising his rule in Islamic terms.
  • Resisted the Mongol threat: avoided direct confrontation with the Mongols advancing under Genghis Khan by refusing to give refuge to Jalaluddin of Khwarazm (a wise strategic decision — saved India from Mongol invasion for decades).
  • Nominated his daughter Razia Sultana as successor — unprecedented.

Razia Sultana (1236–1240 CE)

Razia — daughter of Iltutmish — was the first and only woman Sultan of Delhi:

  • Appeared in public without a veil; held court; led armies.
  • Faced opposition from the Turkish nobility (Chahalgani) who resented her authority.
  • Her relationship with the Abyssinian noble Malik Yakut (whom she appointed Amir-i-Akhur / Master of the Stables) was used to discredit her.
  • Deposed by the Turkish nobles; killed in 1240 CE (some accounts say near Kaithal, Haryana).
  • Noted by historian Minhaj-us-Siraj (author of Tabaqat-i-Nasiri) with admiration for her ability.

Balban (1266–1287 CE) — The Strongman

Ghiyasuddin Balban (c. 1200–1287 CE) was initially a slave of Iltutmish and a member of the Chahalgani. He served as regent for weak sultans before seizing the throne himself:

  • Destroyed the Chahalgani (Group of Forty) — systematically eliminated the powerful Turkish oligarchy.
  • Established absolute monarchy: theory of divine right of the Sultan (niyabat-i-khudai — "Shadow of God on Earth"); no minister could challenge the Sultan.
  • Introduced Persian court etiquette: sijda (prostration before the Sultan) and paibos (kissing the Sultan's feet) — humbling even the highest nobles.
  • Ran an efficient spy system (barids) to check corruption and rebellion.
  • Suppressed rebellions with extreme severity (executions, exemplary punishment of rebels and governors).
  • Held back Mongol invasions — stationed troops in Punjab; his son Prince Muhammad Khan died fighting Mongols (1285 CE) — Balban's grief was intense; he died shortly after, 1287 CE.
  • Court historian: Amir Khusrau and Amir Hasan flourished in this period.

Qutb Complex — Monument

  • Qutb Minar (Delhi): 72.5 m high; 5 storeys; built by Aibak (1 storey) and Iltutmish (4 storeys); later repaired/modified by Firoz Shah Tughlaq and Sikandar Lodi. UNESCO World Heritage Site (1993). India's tallest medieval stone tower.
  • Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque ("Might of Islam"): first mosque in India, built incorporating columns from 27 demolished Hindu and Jain temples.
  • Iron Pillar of Delhi (Gupta era, c. 400 CE): stands within the Qutb complex — 99.7% pure iron, 1,600+ years without rusting.
  • Iltutmish's Tomb: first mausoleum of a Muslim ruler in India — Persian-style domed tomb.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. The Slave dynasty's southern administrative reach did not extend to Maharashtra. However, the Iqta system introduced by Iltutmish became the template for Alauddin Khilji's later administrative reforms — which directly impacted the Deccan/Maharashtra when Alauddin's forces took Devagiri in 1296 CE. The Qutb Minar is referenced in competitive examinations as a medieval monument benchmark.

Exam Traps

  • Qutb Minar was started by Aibak but completed by Iltutmish — questions often ask who built it; the correct answer involves both.
  • Razia was Iltutmish's daughter, NOT Aibak's.
  • The Chahalgani was destroyed by Balban — NOT by Iltutmish (who actually created it).
  • The Iron Pillar inside Qutb complex is NOT a Slave dynasty monument — it is a Gupta-era pillar (c. 400 CE), predating the Sultanate by 800 years.
