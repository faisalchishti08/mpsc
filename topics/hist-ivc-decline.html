Decline — theories PREMAINS

Overview

The decline of the Harappan civilisation (~1900–1300 BCE) is one of the most debated questions in Indian archaeology. A once-thriving urban civilisation abandoned its major cities over ~200–300 years — but why? Theories range from Aryan invasion (now largely discredited) to climate change, river changes, floods, epidemics, and over-exploitation. The MPSC tests on the major theories and the evidence for/against each.

In-Depth

Timeline of Decline

  • ~1900 BCE: Signs of stress appear — Mohenjo-daro shows evidence of squatters reusing structures, decline in craft quality.
  • ~1800–1700 BCE: Major Indus Valley cities (Mohenjo-daro, Harappa) essentially abandoned or drastically depopulated.
  • ~1700–1300 BCE: Late Harappan cultures persist in Gujarat (Rangpur, Rojdi), eastern Punjab/Haryana/Rajasthan — smaller, less urban, regional.
  • ~1300–1000 BCE: Final vestiges; replaced by Painted Grey Ware culture in northwest and Gangetic plains.

Current Consensus

No single theory explains the decline. The current scholarly consensus is multi-causal:

  1. Climate change (weakening monsoon ~2000 BCE) reduced agricultural yields and freshwater.
  2. River channel changes (Sarasvati/Ghaggar drying, Indus course shifts) disrupted water supply.
  3. Trade disruption (Mesopotamian connections broken).
  4. Disease and urban stress in overcrowded declining cities.
  5. Migration east to the Ganga plain — this is not pure "collapse" but demographic shift toward wetter regions. The Late Harappan in east Punjab, Haryana, and Rajasthan persisted for centuries.

The Aryan Question

Wheeler's Aryan Invasion Theory (AIT) once dominated — but is now largely abandoned:

  • The "massacre" skeletons at Mohenjo-daro were found in different stratigraphic layers — not a single event.
  • No weapons of Aryan invaders, no burned cities, no battle evidence in the archaeological record.
  • The Rigveda's "fort-breaking" (purandhara) likely refers to clouds/monsoon in metaphor, not literal city destruction.
  • The 2019 Rakhigarhi DNA study found no Steppe/Aryan ancestry in Mature Harappan skeletons — Aryan migration came after the urban decline.
  • Most contemporary scholars prefer the term "Aryan Migration" (gradual cultural influx ~1500–1200 BCE) over "invasion."

Late Harappan Cultures

After the urban phase, regional Late Harappan cultures emerged:

  • Cemetery H culture (Harappa region, ~1900–1300 BCE): Different burial practices, distinctive pottery — possibly a new population or cultural shift.
  • Jhukar culture (Sindh, ~1900 BCE): Coarser pottery, fewer craft items — late phase at Chanhu-daro.
  • Gujarat Late Harappan (Rangpur, Rojdi): Maintained more Harappan features longer; shifted south.

Maharashtra Connection

The Jorwe Chalcolithic culture (Maharashtra, ~1400–700 BCE) shows evidence of late-phase decline too — smaller sites, reduced craft production, possible drought-related abandonment at Inamgaon (~1000 BCE). This parallel with Harappan decline (drought + abandonment) may reflect the same regional climate stress affecting the Deccan.

Key Facts

  • IVC urban decline: ~1900–1700 BCE for major cities.
  • Aryan Invasion Theory: proposed by Mortimer Wheeler; now largely discredited.
  • Climate change evidence: 2012 Nature study (Giosan et al.) — weakening monsoon ~2000 BCE.
  • Rakhigarhi DNA (2019): no Steppe ancestry in Mature Harappan — rules out Aryan invasion causing collapse.
  • Late Harappan: Cemetery H culture (Harappa area); Jhukar culture (Sindh).
  • Current consensus: multi-causal — climate + river + trade + disease.

Exam Traps

  • The Aryan Invasion Theory is largely discredited — don't present it as the accepted explanation.
  • Decline was not sudden — gradual over ~200 years, ~1900–1700 BCE for cities.
  • Late Harappan cultures persisted after urban collapse — IVC did not simply "disappear."
  • Wheeler's skeleton evidence: found in different layers, not one massacre — the "massacre" interpretation is wrong.
