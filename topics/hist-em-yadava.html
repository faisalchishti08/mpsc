Yadavas of Devagiri (Maharashtra) PREMAINSMH

Overview

The Yadava dynasty of Devagiri (c. 850–1317 CE) was the last major indigenous dynasty of Maharashtra before the establishment of the Delhi Sultanate's control over the Deccan. Ruling from Devagiri fort (near Daulatabad, Aurangabad district, Maharashtra), the Yadavas built a powerful state that patronised Marathi language and culture — the earliest significant Marathi literary works were composed under their rule. Their defeat first by Alauddin Khilji's general Malik Kafur (1294 CE) and final conquest by the Khilji and Tughlaq sultans marks the end of Maharashtra's independent medieval history. In-Depth

Origins and Rise

The Yadavas claimed descent from the mythological Yadava clan of Lord Krishna. They initially served as feudatories of the Rashtrakutas and then the Western Chalukyas of Kalyani. The early Yadava chieftain Dridhaprahara established the dynasty, but real prominence began under:

  • Bhillama V (c. 1185–1193 CE): overthrew the Western Chalukyas; established Yadava independence; conquered Vidarbha and Marathwada. He was checked by the Hoysalas at the Battle of Lakshmeshvara (c. 1190 CE).
  • Singhana (c. 1210–1246 CE): greatest Yadava militarily; extended the kingdom from the Tungabhadra to the Tapti river; controlled most of modern Maharashtra.
  • Ramachandra (c. 1271–1309 CE): last significant Yadava king; initially tributary to Alauddin Khilji after 1296; eventually killed by Malik Kafur (1310–1312 CE).
  • Harapaladeva (Shankaradeva): last Yadava ruler; executed by Malik Kafur/Qutb-ud-din Mubarak Shah (c. 1317–1318 CE).

Devagiri Fort — Strategic Importance

Devagiri (Sanskrit: Deva = god, giri = mountain — "Hill of the Gods") is a volcanic basaltic hill rising 183 m above the Deccan plateau near modern Daulatabad (Aurangabad district). The fort was:

  • Almost impregnable — steep sides, moat, concentric walls.
  • Located at the junction of north-south and east-west Deccan trade routes.
  • Renamed Daulatabad (City of Fortune) by Muhammad bin Tughlaq when he briefly tried to shift the Delhi Sultanate's capital here (c. 1327 CE) — one of the most derided administrative experiments in Indian history.

Economy and Trade

The Yadava kingdom was wealthy from:

  • Trade routes: Devagiri's location on the Dakshinapatha generated customs revenue from north-south trade.
  • Agriculture: Vidarbha and Marathwada cotton; Godavari-basin agriculture.
  • Ports: indirect access to Konkan ports (Bharuch, Sopara, Dabhol) through tribute-paying subordinates.
  • The hoards of gold, silver, and elephants captured by Alauddin Khilji's commander Ulugh Khan (later Malik Kafur) in 1296 CE from Devagiri were enormous — a primary motivation for Khilji's Deccan campaigns.

Yadava Administration

  • Mahamandalesvara: powerful regional governor (near-autonomous).
  • Nayakas: military commanders who held land grants (naya) in lieu of salary; forerunner of the Nayaka system that later appeared under Vijayanagara.
  • Revenue: land revenue through hereditary deshmukhs (village headmen who collected and remitted taxes) — an institution that persisted in Maharashtra through the Maratha and British periods.

Jnaneshwar/Dnyaneshwar (1275–1296 CE): wrote the Dnyaneshwari at age ~15–16 in Newase (Ahmednagar district, Maharashtra). He is the fountainhead of the Varkari Panth — the devotional movement centred on Pandurang Vitthal (Vithoba) at Pandharpur (Solapur district, Maharashtra). The Varkari tradition's sacred pilgrimage (wari) to Pandharpur continues to this day with millions of pilgrims.

Hemadpant (c. 1260–1320 CE): Minister of Ramachandra Yadava; systematised administrative records in Marathi (earlier, Persian-influenced documents were replacing Sanskrit); associated with a style of dry-stone construction (Hemadpanthi architecture) characterised by interlocking stones without mortar, found in many Maharashtra temples.

Fall of the Yadavas

  • 1296 CE: Alauddin Khilji (then a general of his uncle Sultan Jalaluddin) raided Devagiri by surprise — the first Muslim raid deep into the Deccan. He captured Yadava king Ramachandra and extracted a massive tribute: gold, silver, pearls, and 1,000 horses.
  • 1307–1312 CE: Alauddin (now Sultan) sent Malik Kafur to the Deccan; Ramachandra became a tributary; then his successor Shankaradeva revolted; he was killed by Kafur.
  • 1317 CE: Last Yadava king Harapaladeva defeated and killed; Devagiri absorbed into the Delhi Sultanate.

Exam Traps

  • Dnyaneshwari was written in 1290 CE — the Yadava period — NOT during the Maratha period.
  • The 1296 raid was by Alauddin Khilji as a general (not yet sultan) — he used the Deccan loot to fund his own coup against Sultan Jalaluddin Khilji.
  • Hemadpant is associated with the Yadavas, not the Marathas — though his administrative/architectural legacy persisted into the Maratha period.
  • Daulatabad (renamed from Devagiri) was Muhammad bin Tughlaq's doing — NOT Alauddin Khilji's.
