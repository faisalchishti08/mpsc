Later Vedic period — changes PREMAINS

Overview

The Later Vedic period (~1000–600 BCE) marks a fundamental transformation from the tribal, cattle-centred Rigvedic society to a settled agricultural civilisation in the Ganga-Yamuna Doab. Characterised by use of iron tools, expansion eastward, growth of powerful monarchies, elaboration of the varna system, and increasingly complex Brahmanical rituals, this period directly precedes the emergence of the 16 Mahajanapadas. MPSC tests the contrast with Early Vedic, the significance of iron, the new geographic focus, and changes in polity and society.

In-Depth

Geographic Shift

The Later Vedic people moved eastward — the Ganga-Yamuna Doab and Doab regions (now UP, Bihar) became the new heartland. The texts of this period (Atharvaveda, Yajurveda, Samaveda, Brahmanas, Upanishads, epics in their early forms) reflect a settled, agricultural society rather than the nomadic pastoral world of the Rigveda.

Key regions: Kuru-Panchala (upper Doab, modern Haryana/UP) — the most culturally dominant. Kosala, Kashi, Videha — eastern UP and Bihar.

Iron Age — The Key Material Change

  • Iron (Syama Ayas = "dark metal") appears in Later Vedic texts (~1000 BCE); contrasted with copper/bronze (Lohita Ayas = "red metal").
  • Iron enabled: forest clearance (iron axes on dense Gangetic plains vegetation), heavy ploughs (iron ploughshares → deeper cultivation), and better weapons.
  • This drove the agricultural revolution of the Ganga plain and enabled expansion and settlement.
  • Associated archaeological culture: Painted Grey Ware (PGW) (~1200–600 BCE) — iron-using, settled villages across Doab; sites at Hastinapur, Kurukshetra, Mathura.

Social Changes

  • Varna system rigidified: Four varnas — Brahman, Kshatriya, Vaishya, Shudra — became hereditary and more rigid.
  • Brahman supremacy: Brahmans controlled elaborate sacrificial rituals (yajnas), making them indispensable and increasingly wealthy.
  • Women's status declined: Excluded from Upanayana (sacred thread ceremony) and higher education; widow remarriage frowned upon; increasing restrictions.
  • Untouchability (prototype): A fifth group below Shudras began emerging in late Later Vedic texts — Chandala, Nishada.
  • Gotra (patrilineal lineage) system became important for marriage rules (gotra exogamy).

Economic Changes

  • Agriculture dominant: Iron ploughs, rice and wheat cultivation, settled villages.
  • Craft specialisation: Goldsmith (Suvarnakara), Weaver, Potter, Carpenter, Barber — distinct occupational groups.
  • Trade: Internal trade growing; Vanik (trader/merchant) class emerges.
  • Nishka still used as currency; no coins yet.
  • Land ownership (Kshetra) becomes important — contrast with cattle wealth in Early Vedic.

Religious Changes

  • Brahmanical religion elaborate — complex rituals requiring expensive sacrifices accessible only to the wealthy.
  • New religious concepts in Upanishads: Brahman (Universal Soul), Atman (Individual Soul), their identity (Aham Brahmasmi; Tat tvam asi), Karma, Rebirth, Moksha — a reaction against ritual excess.
  • Emergence of heterodox traditions: The complexity and cost of Brahmanical rituals + social rigidity of varna → led to the rise of Buddhism and Jainism (~6th–5th c. BCE) as reform movements.
  • Atheistic or agnostic tendencies: Upanishads begin questioning the efficacy of external ritual in favour of inner knowledge.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. The Later Vedic eastward expansion brought Brahmanical culture into the Doab and later the Deccan over centuries. Maharashtra's Brahmanical traditions (Deshastha, Saraswat, Chitpavan Brahmins) trace their heritage to this Later Vedic-Upanishadic tradition. The Godavari–Pravara valley of Maharashtra was part of the cultural zone that gradually absorbed Vedic-Brahmanical practices as expansion continued south of the Vindhyas.

Key Facts

  • Later Vedic heartland: Kuru-Panchala (upper Doab, Haryana/W.UP).
  • Iron = Syama Ayas (dark metal); copper = Lohita Ayas.
  • Archaeological culture: Painted Grey Ware (PGW) (~1200–600 BCE).
  • Key PGW sites: Hastinapur, Kurukshetra, Mathura.
  • Royal sacrifice: Ashvamedha (territorial), Rajasuya (coronation), Vajapeya (rejuvenation).
  • Women: excluded from Upanayana (sacred thread).
  • Varna: becomes hereditary and rigid in Later Vedic.
  • Reaction → Buddhism, Jainism (~6th c. BCE).

Memory Aids

  • "Iron → Forest → Farm → Town → Kingdom" — the Later Vedic cascade.
  • "PGW = Painted Grey Ware = iron-age Doab culture."
  • Sacrifices: "A-R-V" = Ashvamedha (horse/territory), Rajasuya (coronation), Vajapeya (chariot race/rejuvenation).
  • "Later = Larger — larger kingdoms, larger rituals, larger inequalities."

Exam Traps

  • Painted Grey Ware (PGW) is Later Vedic; do NOT confuse with Northern Black Polished Ware (NBP) which is Mauryan/later period.
  • The term Syama Ayas = iron (dark metal); Ayas alone in Rigveda = copper/bronze, NOT iron.
  • Upanishads are part of Later Vedic corpus, not Rigvedic — they come late in the Later Vedic period.
  • Ashvamedha is a territorial claim sacrifice — the horse roams for a year, land it covers belongs to the king (or is contested). It is NOT a coronation sacrifice (Rajasuya is).
