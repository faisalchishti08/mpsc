Vedanta PREMAINS

Overview

Vedanta (Uttara Mimamsa = "later inquiry") is the most influential of India's six orthodox philosophical schools, founded on Badarayana's Brahmasutras (~2nd c. BCE) as a systematic interpretation of the Upanishads. Three major sub-schools developed under Shankara (Advaita), Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita), and Madhva (Dvaita) — representing different understandings of the relationship between the individual soul (jiva), the world (jagat), and Ultimate Reality (Brahman). Vedanta's influence on Indian religion, philosophy, and reform movements is pervasive. MPSC tests the three sub-schools and their founders.

In-Depth

Foundation

  • Badarayana (also called Vyasa): composed the Brahmasutras (~2nd c. BCE) — 555 aphorisms synthesising the Upanishadic teaching; also called Vedantasutras or Uttaramimamsasutras.
  • Vedanta literally = "end of the Vedas" (Veda = knowledge + anta = end); refers both to the Upanishads (the final section of Vedic texts) and to the philosophical school interpreting them.
  • The "three canonical texts" of Vedanta (Prasthanatrayi): Upanishads + Bhagavad Gita + Brahmasutras — all Vedanta schools must comment on all three.

Adi Shankara (788–820 CE) — Advaita Vedanta

  • The most influential Vedantic philosopher; toured India debating opponents, establishing four mathas (monasteries): Sringeri (south), Dwaraka (west), Puri (east), Badrinath (north).
  • Maya (avidya/illusion): the apparent multiplicity of the world is due to cosmic ignorance; when knowledge arises, only Brahman remains.
  • Brahman: Nirguna (without attributes) — the ultimate reality; pure consciousness-existence-bliss (Sat-Chit-Ananda).
  • Liberation (Moksha): through jnana (knowledge) — realising "Aham Brahmasmi" (I am Brahman) and "Tat tvam asi" (Thou art that).
  • Vivekachudamani: his key didactic work; also wrote commentaries on the Brahmasutras, Upanishads, and Bhagavad Gita.

Ramanuja (1017–1137 CE) — Vishishtadvaita

  • Based in Srirangam (Tamil Nadu); belonged to the Sri Vaishnava tradition.
  • Brahman = Saguna (with attributes) = Vishnu/Narayana with qualities of infinite goodness.
  • Jiva and world are real but exist as attributes (modes) of Brahman — like the soul is to the body.
  • Liberation through Bhakti (devotion) and Prapatti (surrender to God) — not purely through knowledge.
  • Sri Bhashya: commentary on Brahmasutras; major Vishishtadvaita text.

Madhva (Madhvacharya, 1238–1317 CE) — Dvaita

  • Born in Karnataka; founded the Udupi Krishna Mutt.
  • Five eternal distinctions (pancha-bheda): God-jiva, God-matter, jiva-jiva, jiva-matter, matter-matter.
  • Strong devotion to Vishnu/Krishna; some souls are predestined to liberation, some to eternal damnation, some eternally free.

Modern Vedanta

  • Swami Vivekananda (1863–1902): Practical Vedanta; took Advaita to the West (Chicago Parliament of Religions, 1893); founded Ramakrishna Math and Mission (1897).
  • Sri Aurobindo: Integral Vedanta — synthesis of Vedanta with evolutionary philosophy.

Maharashtra Connection

Vedanta has deep Maharashtra connections:

  • Adi Shankara visited Maharashtra; Kolhapur and other sites in his tour.
  • Sant Dnyaneshwar's Dnyaneshwari: a Marathi commentary on the Bhagavad Gita (one of the three Prasthanatrayi texts) — essentially a Vedanta text in Marathi.
  • Sant Tukaram, Ramdas: their philosophical positions draw from both Advaita and Vishishtadvaita strands.
  • Swami Vivekananda: addressed audiences in Pune and Mumbai during his Indian tours; his lectures at Pune remain influential.
  • Ramakrishna Math, Nagpur and other Maharashtra centres.

Key Facts

  • Vedanta = "End of the Vedas"; text: Brahmasutras (Badarayana).
  • Three sub-schools: Advaita (Shankara), Vishishtadvaita (Ramanuja), Dvaita (Madhva).
  • Shankara: 8th c. CE; 4 mathas; Maya; Nirguna Brahman; liberation via jnana.
  • Ramanuja: 11th–12th c. CE; Saguna Brahman; liberation via bhakti.
  • Madhva: 13th c. CE; Udupi; five eternal distinctions.
  • Prasthanatrayi = Upanishads + Bhagavad Gita + Brahmasutras.
  • Swami Vivekananda: Chicago 1893; Ramakrishna Mission (1897).

Memory Aids

  • Shankara's four mathas: "North-South-East-West" = Badrinath-Sringeri-Puri-Dwaraka.
  • Advaita = "Non-dual; only Brahman; Maya; jnana".
  • Vishishtadvaita = "Qualified non-dual; Ramanuja; bhakti; Vishnu".
  • Dvaita = "Dual; Madhva; Udupi; five distinctions".

Exam Traps

  • Advaita (Shankara) accepts Nirguna Brahman (without attributes); Vishishtadvaita (Ramanuja) accepts Saguna Brahman (with attributes = Vishnu).
  • Prasthanatrayi has 3 texts — Brahmasutras, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita — not just the Upanishads.
  • Madhva's Dvaita ≠ Samkhya's Dualism — different meanings of "duality"; Madhva's is God-world-soul; Samkhya's is Purusha-Prakriti.
