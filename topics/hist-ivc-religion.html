Religion, art and seals PREMAINS

Overview

Harappan religion is studied through material evidence alone — seals, terracotta figurines, fire altars, burials, and structures — since the script remains undeciphered. The evidence suggests worship of a Mother Goddess, proto-Shiva figure, animals (especially bull, pipal tree), and possibly water (Great Bath). There are no confirmed temples; religion appears to have been home-centred or conducted at community spaces. Harappan religion contains elements that may connect to later Hinduism, though direct continuity is debated.

Seals and Script

  • Approximately 2,500 seals discovered from IVC sites; mostly square, made of steatite (soapstone).
  • Each seal typically shows an animal + Harappan script inscription above.
  • The Harappan script (~400–600 signs) is a pictographic/logosyllabic system; written right to left (boustrophedon in some cases).
  • NOT deciphered despite many attempts — the lack of a bilingual inscription (like the Rosetta Stone) remains the obstacle.
  • Seals were probably used as trade/identity markers — pressed into clay to seal goods or identify ownership.

The "Proto-Shiva" Question

The Pashupati Seal (Mohenjo-daro) depicts a seated male in yogic posture surrounded by animals. John Marshall (1931) identified this as "proto-Shiva" — a lord of animals (Pashupati) with yogic/meditative elements prefiguring Shiva's iconography. Counter-arguments:

  1. The figure may be an early depiction of a deity common to multiple Asian cultures.
  2. The "three faces" is disputed — may be a profile view.
  3. Direct continuity from IVC to Vedic/Hindu tradition is not established.

The debate remains unresolved but the seal is widely cited as evidence of religious continuity.

Nature Worship and Animal Symbolism

  • Pipal tree (Ficus religiosa) — shown in seals with deity inside the tree; sacred in subsequent Hindu and Buddhist traditions (Bodhi tree = pipal).
  • Bull (humped zebu): Central religious animal; likely sacrificial; analogous to Vedic and Tamil Sangam bull-worship.
  • Tiger, elephant, rhinoceros, crocodile: Appear on seals, suggesting a symbolic or ritual relationship with wild animals.

Religious Continuity Debate

Scholars like D.D. Kosambi, Shereen Ratnagar argue that some Harappan religious elements — Mother Goddess, pipal worship, ritual bathing, sacred animals — continued into post-Harappan religious life and influenced later Hinduism. Others (like Romila Thapar) urge caution: the script is undeciphered, so religious texts and meanings cannot be read; the material parallels may be coincidental.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. However, the pipal tree and bull worship evident in Harappan religion have deep resonance in Maharashtra's ritual culture (Nandi/bull in Shaivite shrines; pipal worship in villages). Maharashtra's Chalcolithic (Jorwe) sites at Inamgaon show terracotta figurines that may represent a local continuation of Mother Goddess traditions.

Key Facts

  • Most common seal motif: Unicorn (~60% of all seals).
  • Pashupati seal: Mohenjo-daro; identified as "proto-Shiva" by John Marshall (1931).
  • Harappan script: ~400–600 signs; undeciphered; written right to left.
  • Fire altars unique to: Kalibangan.
  • Seals material: Steatite (soapstone).
  • Great Bath: ritual bathing; at Mohenjo-daro.
  • Pipal tree: depicted on seals; later sacred in Hindu and Buddhist traditions.
  • No confirmed temples identified in Harappan cities.

Exam Traps

  • The Harappan script is NOT deciphered — do not claim any known meaning for specific signs.
  • Pashupati seal is at Mohenjo-daro, NOT Harappa.
  • Fire altars are ONLY at Kalibangan, not a universal Harappan feature.
  • The unicorn seal is the most common motif — not the bull or elephant.
