Early Vedic (Rigvedic) polity, society, economy PREMAINS

Overview

The Early Vedic (Rigvedic) period (~1500–1000 BCE) represents the phase when Indo-Aryan-speaking peoples settled in the Sapta Sindhu (seven-river) region of the northwest Indian subcontinent. The primary source is the Rigveda — 10 mandalas of hymns. Society was tribal, semi-nomadic, cattle-centred, and relatively egalitarian compared to later Vedic phases. MPSC tests on geographical spread, social structure, polity, economy, and the contrast with Later Vedic society.

In-Depth

Geography — Sapta Sindhu

The Early Vedic people lived in the Sapta Sindhu region — the land of seven rivers: Sindhu (Indus), Vitasta (Jhelum), Asikni (Chenab), Parushni (Ravi), Vipas (Beas), Shutudri (Sutlej), Sarasvati. The Ganga and Yamuna are mentioned rarely in the Rigveda — the civilisation was centred in the Punjab region.

Polity

  • Basic unit: Kula (family) → Grama (village) → Vis (clan) → Jana (tribe) → Rashtra (kingdom).
  • Tribal chief: Rajan — not a hereditary absolute monarch; power limited by tribal assemblies.
  • Assemblies: Sabha (smaller council, elders/nobles) and Samiti (larger popular assembly); both had significant power over the Rajan.
  • Purohita (chief priest) and Senani (commander) were key advisors.
  • No permanent capital, no fixed territory — tribal, semi-nomadic.
  • Battles: Dasarajna (Battle of Ten Kings) — recorded in Rigveda (Book 7); King Sudas of the Bharata tribe (guided by Vasishtha) defeated a confederation of ten tribes near the Parushni (Ravi) river. Most famous early Vedic battle.

Society

  • Largely egalitarian — no rigid caste system in early phase.
  • Two major divisions: Arya (the community) and Dasa/Dasyu (possibly pre-Aryan peoples or enemies; darker-complexioned, different speech, without nose — snub-nosed or noseless?).
  • Varna (originally "colour") had a more fluid social meaning in Rigveda — not yet the rigid four-fold system of later texts.
  • Women's position: Relatively better than later — women could participate in sacrifices, attend Sabha, had property rights in some forms. Notable Rigvedic women poets (brahmagadinis): Vishvavara, Ghosa, Lopamudra, Apala, Yami.
  • Gotra (lineage) system beginning; patrilineal families.
  • Levirate marriage (niyoga) and widow remarriage were permitted.

Economy

  • Cattle-centred: Cattle were the primary wealth unit; wars fought over cattle. Word for war = Gavishti (desire for cows).
  • Agriculture: Beginning but secondary — wheat and barley cultivated; Yava (barley) frequently mentioned.
  • Barter economy; Nishka (gold ornament/disc) used as a unit of exchange — no coined money.
  • Crafts: Carpenter (Takshan), Blacksmith (Karmara), Weaver, Leather-worker mentioned.
  • Pastoralism: Dominant; cattle-herding and horse-rearing central to wealth and warfare.

Religion

  • Worship of nature deities: Indra (storm/war — most hymns, ~250/1028 suktas), Agni (fire — 2nd most), Varuna (cosmic order/water), Soma (sacred plant/ritual drink), Surya (sun), Usha (dawn), Maruts (storm gods).
  • Yajnas (fire sacrifices): Central ritual — Agnihotra (daily), Ashvamedha (horse sacrifice, political), Rajasuya (royal coronation).
  • Gods were not idol-worshipped — no temples, no idol worship in Rigvedic period.
  • Rta (cosmic order/moral law) and Dharma (duty) were key concepts.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific — the Rigvedic heartland is Punjab/NW. However, the Sarasvati river (associated with Maharashtra's Ahmednagar district's Pravara-Godavari system as a later cultural reference) and the yajna tradition that shaped Brahmanical culture in Maharashtra connect the Vedic heritage. The later Vedic expansion southward eventually reached the Deccan, shaping Maharashtra's Brahmanical traditions (Deshastha, Chitpavan Brahmins).

Key Facts

  • Rigveda: 10 mandalas, 1028 suktas, ~10,552 verses.
  • Heartland: Sapta Sindhu (seven rivers, Punjab region).
  • Major deity: Indra (most hymns).
  • Battle of Ten Kings: Dasarajna — Sudas of Bharatas vs 10-tribe coalition.
  • Assemblies: Sabha + Samiti — limited the Rajan.
  • War = Gavishti (cattle-seeking).
  • Notable women poets: Vishvavara, Ghosa, Lopamudra, Apala.
  • No idol worship, no temples in Rigvedic period.

Memory Aids

  • "Indra most hymns (~250), Agni second, Varuna = cosmic order."
  • Seven rivers: "SiViAs-PaViShSa" — Sindhu, Vitasta, Asikni, Parushni, Vipas, Shutudri, Sarasvati.
  • "Gavishti = War = Cow-seeking" — cattle is everything.
  • "Sabha = small, Samiti = big" — two Vedic assemblies.

Exam Traps

  • Indra, not Brahma or Vishnu, is the most important Rigvedic deity.
  • The varna system in Rigvedic period is NOT rigid — Purushsukta (hymn describing varnas) is in Book 10, considered a later addition.
  • Rigveda mentions the Ganga only once, Yamuna twice — it's primarily a Punjab text.
  • Idol worship and temples do NOT exist in the Rigvedic period — they develop much later.
