Temple architecture — Nagara, Dravida, Vesara PREMAINS

Overview

Indian temple architecture evolved from the Gupta period (4th–6th c. CE) into three major styles — Nagara (north), Dravida (south), and Vesara (mixed/Deccan) — each defined by the form of the shikhara (tower over the sanctum), plan, and decorative scheme. Temple architecture is central to MPSC art and culture syllabus: Prelims asks style identification and key examples; Mains asks comparative analysis.

In-Depth

Structural Components of a Temple

Before the styles, understand the vocabulary:

  • Garbhagriha (sanctum): innermost chamber housing the deity; square plan.
  • Shikhara/Vimana: tower above the garbhagriha — the defining stylistic element.
  • Mandapa: pillared hall for worshippers, in front of the garbhagriha.
  • Antarala: vestibule connecting mandapa and garbhagriha.
  • Gopuram: gateway tower in South Indian (Dravida) temple complexes.
  • Prakara: enclosure wall around the temple complex.
  • Amalaka: flattened ribbed disc on top of the Nagara shikhara.
  • Kalasha: pot-shaped finial on top of the amalaka.

Nagara Style (Northern Style)

Developed in central and northern India; shikhara is curvilinear (rekha/latina) — gradually tapering like a beehive:

Odishan sub-style (distinct within Nagara):

  • Deul (shikhara) + Jagamohana (mandapa with pyramid roof).
  • Examples: Lingaraja temple (1090 CE, Bhubaneswar), Jagannath temple (Puri, 12th c.), Sun Temple Konark (1250 CE — most ambitious; wheel motif; 24 wheels; UNESCO WHS).

Khajuraho temples (Chandela, 950–1050 CE): Sekhari-style; famous for erotic sculptures (represent 10% of total carvings; rest are devotional) — UNESCO WHS; Madhya Pradesh.

Dravida Style (Southern Style)

Developed in Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh; shikhara (vimana) is pyramidal with horizontal tiers (not curvilinear); the gopuram (gateway tower) becomes increasingly dominant over time and taller than the main vimana:

DynastyPeriodKey TempleFeature
Pallava7th–9th c.Shore Temple (Mahabalipuram), Kailasanatha (Kanchipuram)Rock-cut → structural; early Dravida
Chola9th–13th c.Brihadeeswara (Thanjavur, 1010 CE)Tallest vimana (66 m); no shadow at noon; UNESCO WHS
Vijayanagara14th–16th c.Vitthala Temple (Hampi), Virupaksha (Hampi)Stone chariot; musical pillars
Nayaka16th–18th c.Meenakshi temple (Madurai)Giant gopurams; elaborate corridors

Brihadeeswara temple (Thanjavur): built by Raja Raja Chola I (1010 CE) — 66 m vimana, entirely of granite; the shadow of the shikhara does not fall on the ground at noon (engineering marvel); UNESCO WHS; the finest Chola monument.

Vesara Style (Mixed / Deccan)

Developed in the Deccan (Karnataka, Maharashtra) by Chalukya, Rashtrakuta, Hoysala dynasties — a fusion of Nagara shikhara form with Dravida plan:

DynastyPeriodKey FeatureExamples
Chalukya (Badami)6th–8th c.Early Vesara; cave + structural blendPattadakal temples (UNESCO WHS); Virupaksha temple
Rashtrakuta8th–10th c.Rock-cut Kailasa temple (Ellora)Kailasa temple, Ellora — largest rock-cut structure in the world
Hoysala11th–13th c.Star-shaped plan; schist stone; lathe-turned pillars; intricate horizontal bandsChennakeshava (Belur), Hoysaleshvara (Halebid)

Kailasa temple, Ellora (Cave 16): built by Rashtrakuta king Dantidurga / Krishna I (c. 757–775 CE) — carved top-down from a single basalt rock outcrop; 90 m long, 50 m wide, 30 m high; the most ambitious rock-cut structure in the world; UNESCO WHS.

Hoysala distinctive features: Intricate soapstone (chloritic schite) carvings; horizontal friezes of elephants, horses, scrolling leaves, mythological scenes; star-shaped (stellate) plan for both temple and platform; no gopurams; Chennakeshava temple, Belur (1117 CE) and Hoysaleshvara, Halebid (12th c.) are the finest examples.

Maharashtra Connection

The Deccan/Vesara style is directly Maharashtra-relevant:

  • Kailasa temple, Ellora (Aurangabad, Maharashtra) — the crown jewel; UNESCO World Heritage Site (part of Ajanta-Ellora).
  • Pattadakal is in Karnataka but the style originated in the Deccan heartland under the same Chalukya kingdom that covered Maharashtra.
  • Yadava temples (12th–13th c.) in Maharashtra continued the Hemadpanthi style — named after minister Hemadri (Hemadpant) of the Yadava kingdom; characterised by black basalt stone; examples include Ambarnath temple (Thane), Gondeshwar temple (Sinnar, Nashik).
  • Hemadpanthi style temples widespread in rural Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • Three styles: Nagara (north, curvilinear), Dravida (south, pyramidal), Vesara (Deccan, mixed).
  • Amalaka = ribbed disc on top of Nagara shikhara.
  • Gopuram = gateway tower of Dravida complexes.
  • Brihadeeswara (Thanjavur): Raja Raja Chola I, 1010 CE, 66 m vimana; shadow-less noon.
  • Kailasa temple, Ellora: Rashtrakuta (Krishna I, ~757–775 CE); largest rock-cut structure.
  • Konark Sun Temple: 1250 CE, Odisha; 24 wheels; UNESCO WHS.
  • Hoysala: star-shaped plan, soapstone carvings, no gopuram.
  • Hemadpanthi temples: Maharashtra's regional style (black basalt, Yadava period).

Exam Traps

  • Vesara ≠ just "southern" — it's a Deccan/mixed style; Karnataka/Maharashtra temples are Vesara, not Dravida.
  • Kailasa temple (Ellora) = Rock-cut monolith — not built stone by stone; common MCQ catch.
  • Gopuram is the GATEWAY tower, not the main shikhara; in South Indian temples the gopuram is bigger than the vimana in later periods.
  • Hoysala temples have NO gopuram — a distinguishing feature; they are compact jewel-box temples.
