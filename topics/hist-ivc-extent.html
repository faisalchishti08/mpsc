Geographical extent and major sites PREMAINS

Overview

The Indus Valley Civilisation (IVC), also called the Harappan Civilisation, was the world's largest Bronze Age civilisation (~2600–1900 BCE at its mature phase), spanning the Indus basin, Gujarat, Rajasthan, and parts of the Ganga-Yamuna Doab. Its geographic extent (~1.3 million sq km at peak) dwarfed contemporaneous Mesopotamia and Egypt. MPSC tests extensively on site locations, the civilisation's spread, and the major excavated cities — both as MCQs and Mains geography-of-civilisation questions.

Geographic Extent

At its maximum the IVC covered approximately 1.3 million sq km — including present-day:

  • Pakistan (Sindh, Punjab, Balochistan)
  • Northwestern India (Rajasthan, Gujarat, Punjab, Haryana)
  • Parts of Uttar Pradesh (Alamgirpur, Hulas)
  • Parts of Jammu & Kashmir (Manda)
  • Parts of Maharashtra (Daimabad — southernmost site)
  • Afghanistan (Shortughai — northernmost/eastern outlier)

This extent makes IVC larger than Mesopotamia and Egypt combined in area — a commonly asked fact.

Largest Sites

By area (archaeological mounds):

  1. Rakhigarhi (Hisar, Haryana): Estimated ~350 hectares — largest Harappan site; ongoing excavations revealing scale.
  2. Mohenjo-daro (Larkana, Sindh, Pakistan): ~250 hectares; most excavated.
  3. Harappa (Montgomery, Punjab, Pakistan): ~150 hectares; first discovered (1921).
  4. Dholavira (Kutch, Gujarat): ~100 hectares; distinctive water management system.
  5. Ganweriwala (Punjab, Pakistan): ~80 hectares (poorly excavated).

Discovery and Excavation History

  • 1921: Harappa excavated by D.R. Sahni (Archaeological Survey of India, ASI).
  • 1922: Mohenjo-daro excavated by R.D. Banerji (ASI).
  • Sir John Marshall (Director-General, ASI) published the findings and named the civilisation "Indus Valley Civilisation" (1924).
  • Subsequent excavations: Lothal (S.R. Rao, 1955–62), Dholavira (R.S. Bisht, 1990 onwards), Rakhigarhi (ongoing).

The Sarasvati River Question

Many IVC sites are located not on the Indus but on what is now a dry river channel in Rajasthan/Haryana — identified by some scholars as the Ghaggar-Hakra river, hypothesised to be the Vedic Sarasvati. Sites like Kalibangan, Banawali, Rakhigarhi are on this channel. This has led some scholars to prefer the name Sindhu-Sarasvati Civilisation; the debate is ongoing but not settled.

Maharashtra Connection

Daimabad (Ahmednagar district) is the southernmost significant Harappan site — it has a Mature Harappan layer in addition to its dominant Chalcolithic (Jorwe, Savalda, Malwa) layers. The Daimabad bronzes (chariot, rhinoceros, elephant, buffalo) are contemporaneous with Harappan and post-Harappan phases. Maharashtra thus represents the extreme southern limit of Harappan contact and cultural diffusion.

Key Facts

  • IVC extent: ~1.3 million sq km (larger than Mesopotamia + Egypt).
  • Mature Harappan period: ~2600–1900 BCE.
  • First site excavated: Harappa (D.R. Sahni, 1921).
  • Mohenjo-daro: R.D. Banerji, 1922.
  • Largest Harappan site: Rakhigarhi (Haryana, ~350 ha).
  • Southernmost major site: Daimabad (Maharashtra).
  • Northernmost/eastern outlier: Shortughai (Afghanistan).
  • Easternmost site: Alamgirpur (UP) or Hulas (UP).
  • Named "IVC" by: Sir John Marshall (1924).

Memory Aids

  • Largest to smallest: "R-M-H-D" = Rakhigarhi, Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Dholavira.

  • IVC > Egypt + Mesopotamia in area.

Exam Traps

  • Rakhigarhi (Haryana) is the largest Harappan site, NOT Mohenjo-daro — common reversal.
  • Harappa was excavated in 1921, Mohenjo-daro in 1922 — reverse is wrong.
  • IVC was not limited to the Indus river — most Indian sites are in Rajasthan, Gujarat, Haryana.
  • The "name" IVC was given by John Marshall, not by Sahni or Banerji (they excavated, he named).
