Painting traditions — miniature, folk PREMAINS

Overview

Indian painting traditions span from prehistoric rock art (Bhimbetka, 30,000+ BCE) through ancient murals (Ajanta, 2nd c. BCE–6th c. CE), medieval miniatures (Rajput, Mughal, Pahari), to living folk traditions (Madhubani, Warli, Pattachitra). MPSC tests the identification of painting schools, their patrons, key features, and folk paintings — especially Maharashtra's Warli tradition.

In-Depth

Ancient and Medieval Mural Painting

Bhimbetka Rock Shelters (Madhya Pradesh):

  • Prehistoric rock paintings; earliest dated to 30,000 BCE (Upper Palaeolithic); depict animals, hunting, dance, domestic life.
  • UNESCO WHS (2003).

Ajanta Murals (5th–6th c. CE, Maharashtra):

  • Finest surviving ancient mural paintings; Buddhist themes (Jataka tales, Bodhisattvas).
  • Technique: Fresco secco — painted on dry lime plaster.
  • Cave 1: Bodhisattva Padmapani; Cave 16: The Dying Princess; Cave 17: most paintings.
  • Pigments: lapis lazuli (blue), ochre, lamp black — mineral-based.

Badami and Ajanta style also seen at Sittanavasal (Tamil Nadu) and Sigiriya (Sri Lanka).

Miniature Painting Schools

Miniature painting developed from manuscript illustration (9th–12th c.) into elaborate court art:

Mughal Miniature Details:

  • Humayun brought Persian masters Mir Sayyid Ali and Abdus Samad from Safavid court.
  • Akbar's Hamzanama: ~1,400 paintings on cloth, 14 volumes, 14 years (1562–1577); largest illustrated manuscript.
  • Jahangir: greatest naturalist; Ustad Mansur painted the Dodo, Siberian Crane, Bengal Florican with scientific accuracy.
  • Basawan (Akbar's court): best Indian-born Mughal painter; combined Indian anatomy with Persian composition.
  • After Aurangzeb's court restrictions, Mughal artists dispersed to Rajput courts → enriched Rajput painting.

Rajput Painting Sub-Schools:

  • Mewar (Udaipur): earliest; flat perspective; Rasikapriya, Ramayana themes.
  • Bundi-Kotah: hunting scenes, animals; vibrant.
  • Kishangarh: idealized female beauty; Bani Thani — the "Indian Mona Lisa" (portrait of a singer-devotee of Savant Singh, ruler of Kishangarh); now on an Indian postage stamp.

Pahari Sub-Schools:

  • Basohli: bold, vivid colours; jewel-like; early (17th c.); Gita Govinda.
  • Kangra: most famous Pahari school (18th c.); soft, lyrical; Radha-Krishna romance; linear elegance; Nainsukh (18th c.) is the master.
  • Guler: transitional; Naturalistic.

  • Warli painting does not yet have a national GI tag (as of knowledge cutoff) but is protected under Maharashtra's cultural schemes.

Maharashtra Connection

Warli painting is Maharashtra's signature folk art:

  • Origin: Warli tribe of the Sahyadri/Nashik/Palghar/Thane tribal belt.
  • Traditionally painted by women on mud walls for festivals (especially marriage).
  • Medium: White (rice paste pigment) on brown/ochre (cow dung) background.
  • Geometric vocabulary: Circle = sun/moon; Triangle = mountain/trees; Square = sacred ground; Straight line = human stick figure. No perspective; top-down view.
  • Tarpa dance — a common Warli motif showing men and women dancing in a circle around a Tarpa (wind instrument) player.
  • Jivya Soma Mhase — the most celebrated Warli artist; credited with taking Warli to canvas and urban galleries (1970s onward); Padma Shri recipient.
  • Warli paintings now appear on textiles, merchandise, public murals across India.

Exam Traps

  • Bani Thani is Kishangarh school (Rajasthan), not Mewar or Bundi.
  • Ustad Mansur (Jahangir's court) painted naturalist subjects — birds and animals; not Akbar's Hamzanama artist.
  • Warli paintings are white on brown (not the reverse); painted by tribal women (not men traditionally).
  • Madhubani (Bihar) ≠ Warli (Maharashtra) — both are folk paintings but different states, styles, communities.
