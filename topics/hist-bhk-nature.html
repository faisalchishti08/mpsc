Bhakti movement — origins, philosophy, saguna/nirguna PREMAINS

Overview

The Bhakti movement was a devotional reform movement (broadly 7th–17th c.) that swept India, emphasising direct personal devotion to God over ritual, caste hierarchy, and priestly mediation. It began in South India with the Alvars and Nayanars (6th–9th c.), spread to Maharashtra through the Varkari tradition (13th c. onward), and reached North India in the 15th–17th centuries. The movement is philosophically grounded in the Vedantic idea of love (bhakti) as the path to liberation, as articulated by Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita) and developed independently by regional saint-poets. MPSC treats the Bhakti movement as a major topic — especially the Maharashtra saints (Dnyaneshwar, Namdev, Eknath, Tukaram, Ramdas).

In-Depth

Philosophical Roots

  • Bhakti (Sanskrit) = devotion, love, worship; derived from bhaj = to share/serve/love.
  • Narada Bhakti Sutras (attributed to Narada Muni): defined pure bhakti as love of God for its own sake, independent of rewards.
  • Shandilya Bhakti Sutras: another foundational text.
  • Philosophically: Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita (11th–12th c.) provided the theological framework for bhakti by positing Brahman as Saguna (personal God with attributes) and liberation through devotion.

*Note: Most saints combined elements of both; strict categorisation is scholarly convenience.

Key Features of the Bhakti Movement

  1. Anti-ritualism: critiqued empty Vedic rituals; challenged Brahminical monopoly.
  2. Caste rejection: saints from all castes (Kabir = weaver, Namdev = tailor, Tukaram = kunbi/farmer, Chokhamela = Mahar/untouchable, Sena = barber); condemned caste discrimination.
  3. Vernacular literature: composed in local languages (Marathi, Hindi, Punjabi, Telugu, Tamil) rather than Sanskrit — making teachings accessible to common people.
  4. Personal God: emphasis on personal relationship with God through love and surrender.
  5. Social reform: implicitly challenged caste hierarchy; some explicitly preached equality (Kabir, Nanak, Raidas).
  6. Kirtan/Abhangas/Padas: devotional compositions sung publicly; music as medium of spiritual teaching.

Social Impact

  • Women saints: Mirabai (Rajasthan), Janabai (Maharashtra — Namdev's servant), Muktabai (Dnyaneshwar's sister), Lalleshwari (Kashmir Sufi-Bhakti).
  • Dalit saints: Chokhamela (Mahar caste, 14th c. Maharashtra — first Dalit saint poet), Raidas/Ravidas (cobbler, UP, 15th c.), Sena (barber), Kabir (weaver).
  • Created a counter-culture to caste orthodoxy; laid social groundwork that later influenced 19th-c. reform movements (Phule, Ambedkar, Periyar).

Bhakti and Sufi Synthesis

  • The Bhakti movement and Sufi movement (Islamic mystical tradition) both emphasised direct personal experience of the divine, love, and equality — creating zones of Hindu-Muslim spiritual exchange.
  • Sufi saints and Bhakti saints often drew from each other; Kabir is the greatest example (critiqued both Hindu and Muslim orthodoxy).

Maharashtra Connection

Maharashtra's Bhakti tradition — the Warkari movement — is one of the most organised and socially inclusive in India:

  • Warkari Sampradaya: devotees (Warkaris) undertake annual pilgrimage (Wari) to Pandharpur on Ashadhi + Kartiki Ekadashi; hundreds of thousands participate.
  • Chain of saints: Dnyaneshwar (13th c.) → Namdev (13th–14th c.) → Eknath (16th c.) → Tukaram (17th c.) → Ramdas (17th c.) — an unbroken 400-year tradition.
  • Vitthal (Pandurang): the personal God at the centre; combines Vaishnava (Vishnu-Krishna) identity with Maratha local tradition.
  • Abhangas: devotional Marathi poems; the primary literary form of the Warkaris.
  • Chokhamela (14th c., Mahar caste): Dalit saint; his abhangas are part of the Warkari corpus; stood at the temple gate (not allowed inside); wrote movingly of discrimination.
  • Maharashtra's Bhakti saints composed in Marathi — giving the language its classical literary foundation (basis for Marathi's Classical Language status in 2024).

Key Facts

  • Bhakti = devotion to personal God; vernacular; anti-caste; anti-ritualism.
  • Saguna = God with form; Nirguna = formless God.
  • South bhakti first: Alvars (Vaishnava) + Nayanars (Shaiva), 6th–10th c.
  • Maharashtra: Warkari sampradaya; Vitthal; Pandharpur.
  • Chokhamela: first Dalit Bhakti saint; Mahar caste; 14th c. Maharashtra.
  • Bhakti-Sufi synthesis: Kabir most prominent example.

Exam Traps

  • Bhakti movement did NOT begin in North India — it originated in South India (6th c.) and moved northward.
  • Nirguna Bhakti (Kabir, Nanak) ≠ Advaita Vedanta — Nirguna here means formless personal God, not impersonal Brahman.
  • Ramanuja supported Saguna bhakti (Vishishtadvaita) — often confused with Shankara's nirguna Advaita.
