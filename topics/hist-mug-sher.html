Sher Shah Suri and administration PREMAINS

Overview

Sher Shah Suri (1486–1545) ruled India for only five years (1540–1545) but implemented administrative, revenue and infrastructure reforms of lasting significance that even the Mughals adopted. He is considered one of the ablest administrators in Indian history — often compared with Alauddin Khilji and Akbar. MPSC asks frequently about his administrative reforms, the Grand Trunk Road, and the Sur Empire.

In-Depth

Rise to Power

Born Farid Khan in Sasaram (Bihar), son of Hasan Khan Sur — a jagirdar under the Lodi Sultans. He earned the title "Sher Shah" (Lion King) from the Afghan ruler of Bihar, Bahar Khan Lohani, after killing a tiger barehanded. He served under Babur initially, then aligned with the Afghan chiefs against Humayun.

  • Battle of Chausa (1539): first major victory over Humayun; took the title of Emperor.
  • Battle of Kanauj/Bilgram (1540): decisive defeat of Humayun; Sher Shah became undisputed ruler of north India.

He died in 1545 during the siege of Kalinjar fort in Bundelkhand — a firecracker blast in the fort's armoury caused his death.

Administrative Reforms

Sher Shah divided his empire into 47 sarkars (districts), each divided into parganas (sub-districts), overseen by revenue officials. His administrative structure:

Revenue System (the Most Significant)

Sher Shah's revenue reforms directly influenced Akbar's later Zabt system:

  • Land measurement: measured land using the Sikandari gaz (yard); classified as polaj (cultivated), parauti (temporarily uncultivated), chachar (fallow for 3–4 years), banjar (long fallow).
  • State share: fixed at 1/4 to 1/3 of produce depending on land quality.
  • Patta (title deed) issued to peasants; qabuliyat (agreement) signed between peasant and state — first time peasants got formal revenue documents.
  • Eliminated middlemen (zamindars) in revenue collection, dealing directly with peasants.
  • Prevented tax evasion using branding of horses and detailed muster rolls for soldiers.

Military Reforms

  • Dahsala (branding of horses) and chehra (descriptive rolls) of soldiers — direct recruitment and payment in cash; prevented substitution of inferior horses.
  • Central treasury paid soldiers directly — reduced dependence on nobles.

Infrastructure

  • Grand Trunk Road (GT Road): rebuilt and extended the ancient Uttarapath from Sonargaon (Bengal) to Peshawar (Punjab) — approximately 2,400 km. Planted shade trees (serai rest-houses) every kos, dug wells, posted cavalry for courier service.
  • Built 1,700 sarais (rest-houses) along roads; separate quarters for Hindus and Muslims.
  • Rohtas Fort (Punjab): major fortress built on the Jhelum river (now Pakistan, UNESCO World Heritage Site).
  • Purana Qila (Old Fort), Delhi: Sher Shah built the Qila-i-Kuhna Mosque inside Purana Qila — notable for its elegant five-arched screen using red sandstone.

Currency Reform

Introduced the silver rupee (Rupiya) of ~178 grains — standardised coinage that became the basis of the Indian currency system. Also issued copper dam and gold mohur.

Maharashtra Connection

Limited direct connection — Sher Shah's empire did not include the Deccan. However, his administrative model was inherited by the Mughals who later conquered the Deccan. His revenue principles influenced Todar Mal's Zabt system under Akbar, which was later applied in Maharashtra under Mughal and eventually Peshwa administration.

Key Facts

  • Birth name: Farid Khan; title "Sher Shah" from killing a tiger.
  • Defeated Humayun at Chausa (1539) and Kanauj (1540).
  • Died: 1545, at Kalinjar (firecracker blast during siege).
  • 47 sarkars, each with Shiqdar (law) and Munsif (revenue).
  • Land classes: polaj, parauti, chachar, banjar.
  • Patta and qabuliyat — first formal peasant revenue documents.
  • Grand Trunk Road: Sonargaon to Peshawar, ~2,400 km.
  • Introduced silver rupee (Rupiya) as standard coin.
  • Purana Qila (Delhi) and Rohtas Fort are his architectural legacies.

Memory Aids

  • Sher Shah's reforms = "Land → Army → Road → Coin" (four pillars).
  • Land types: "P-P-C-B" → Polaj, Parauti, Chachar, Banjar (decreasing cultivation).
  • GT Road: Bengal to Punjab (east to west).
  • Rupee: Sher Shah introduced it (not the Mughals or British).

Exam Traps

  • Silver Rupee was introduced by Sher Shah Suri, not by Akbar or the British.
  • Patta and qabuliyat were Sher Shah's innovations — Akbar adopted the revenue idea but the Zabt system is Todar Mal/Akbar's, not Sher Shah's term.
  • Rohtas Fort in Pakistan (Punjab) ≠ Rohtas Fort in Bihar — Sher Shah's Rohtas is in present Pakistan.
  • Sher Shah ruled only 5 years (1540–1545) — MPSC may try to inflate his reign.
