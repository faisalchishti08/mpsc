Manipuri (Manipur) PREMAINS

Overview

Manipuri is the classical dance tradition of Manipur (northeast India), rooted in the Vaishnavite Ras Lila tradition of Krishna worship introduced to Manipur in the 18th century. It is distinguished by its soft, flowing, swaying movements (no sharp footwork), the use of the cylindrical Potloi skirt (for female performers), and its meditative quality. MPSC tests state, Ras Lila origin, and costume distinctives.

In-Depth

Origin and Development

  • Manipur has an ancient indigenous dance tradition (Lai Haraoba — ritual dances for local deities).
  • Vaishnavism was introduced to Manipur by Maharaja Bhagyachandra (Jai Singh) in the 18th century (~1779) — he had a vision of the Ras Lila and introduced it to Manipuri dance; is credited with composing the five Ras Lila formats.
  • Five Ras Lilas: Maharas, Vasantaras, Kunja Ras, Nitya Ras, Diva Ras — each for a specific season/occasion.
  • Tagore's discovery: Rabindranath Tagore invited Manipuri teachers to Shantiniketan (1919) — bringing the form to national attention and making it part of the Visva-Bharati curriculum.

Technical Features

Costume (male)White *dhoti*, *dhol* drum while dancing
ThemesKrishna-Radha love (Ras Lila); also Shiva, Durga
MusicManipuri classical (*Pena* string instrument; *Pung* drum, *Kartal* cymbals)
Aesthetic*Lasya* (gentle, feminine style) predominates

Pung Cholom: a male dance form performed while playing the pung (barrel drum) — athletic, vigorous, in contrast to the female Ras Lila. This is the masculine counterpart within the Manipuri tradition.

Exponents

  • Guru Bipin Singh (1918–2000): key figure in modern Manipuri; brought it to concert stage; trained exponents across India.
  • Jhaveri Sisters (Nayana, Ranjana, Suvarna, Darshana): Mumbai-based; brought Manipuri to Maharashtra; Padma Shri recipients; trained under Guru Bipin Singh.

Maharashtra Connection

The Jhaveri Sisters from Mumbai are the most famous link — they studied under Guru Bipin Singh and established Manipuri dance in Maharashtra. Their institution in Mumbai has trained hundreds of Manipuri dancers in the state, making it the most widely practised northeast Indian classical form in Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • State: Manipur (northeast India).
  • Origin: Vaishnavite Ras Lila introduced by Maharaja Bhagyachandra (~1779).
  • Five Ras Lilas: Maharas, Vasantaras, Kunja Ras, Nitya Ras, Diva Ras.
  • Aesthetic: Lasya (soft, gentle, feminine).
  • Female costume: Potloi (cylindrical barrel skirt).
  • Male dance: Pung Cholom (drum-dance).
  • Tagore invited to Shantiniketan: 1919.
  • Maharashtra link: Jhaveri Sisters (Padma Shri).

Memory Aids

  • Manipuri = Manipur + Ras Lila + soft swaying + Potloi skirt.
  • No sharp footwork = "Gentle, not stomping" (opposite of Kathak's tatkar).
  • Bhagyachandra = "Manipur's Vaishnava king + 18th c + Ras Lila."

Exam Traps

  • Manipuri has NO sharp footwork — heels barely touch the ground; common trap to confuse with Kathak.
  • Potloi is the cylindrical skirt — a very specific feature unique to Manipuri female performers.
  • Tagore's role: he brought it to national attention via Shantiniketan; he did not originate or revive it.
