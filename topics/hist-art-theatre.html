Theatre and folk arts (incl. Maharashtra — Tamasha, Lavani) PREMAINSMH

Overview

Indian theatre traditions range from ancient Sanskrit drama to medieval devotional forms (Yakshagana, Ramleela) and Maharashtra's distinctive Tamasha and Lavani folk theatre. The Natyashastra defines classical drama; regional folk forms carry social commentary and community identity. Maharashtra's theatre scene — from Tamasha to the pioneering Marathi professional stage — is central to MPSC MH syllabus.

In-Depth

Classical Sanskrit Theatre

Natyashastra (Bharata Muni, ~2nd c. BCE) defined the rules of classical drama: 10 types of drama (rupaka), dramatic unity, stagecraft, and the rasa theory (9 emotions). Major Sanskrit playwrights:

  • Kalidasa (Gupta period, 4th–5th c. CE): Abhijnanasakuntalam (Shakuntala), Meghaduta, Malavikagnimitram — Sanskrit literary zenith.
  • Bhavabhuti (7th–8th c. CE): Uttararamacharita, Malatimadhava.
  • Shudraka (~3rd c. CE): Mricchakatika (The Little Clay Cart) — a social drama with a poor hero.

Tamasha — Maharashtra's Signature Folk Theatre

Tamasha is the most important folk theatre form of Maharashtra:

  • Origin: Evolved in the 18th century in the Peshwa period; Persian/Urdu tamasho (entertainment) → Marathi Tamasha.
  • Lavani is the primary musical-dance element of Tamasha — sensuous shringara songs performed by Nartakis (female performers) to the beat of dholki (drum); Tasha and Sambal percussion instruments also used.
  • Two forms:
  • Faras (Sang): humorous skits (Faras); the Songadya (comedian/jester) is central; comic dialogues.
  • Vag: narrative play; devotional or social themes.
  • Shahirs (folk poets) compose Lavani and social commentary — tradition continues; Annabhau Sathe (revolutionary Shahir, 1920–1969) is most celebrated.
  • Vithabai Narayangaokar: legendary Tamasha Lavani performer; called the "Queen of Lavani."
  • Dholki is the central percussion instrument; nathya (bell-anklets) worn by performers.
  • Social notes: Tamasha traditionally performed by communities like Kolhati (acrobatic performers) and Mahar; historically marginalised and associated with lower-caste performance communities.

Lavani — Distinct Sub-Form

  • Lavani (from lavanya = beauty) is both a song form and dance form.
  • Shringara (romantic) Lavani: Most popular; deals with love, separation, sensuality.
  • Nirguni Lavani: Philosophical/devotional — Warkari tradition influence.
  • Powadas (ballads): military narrative songs distinct from Lavani; celebrate heroes (Shivaji, etc.); performed by Shahirs.
  • 9 types of Lavani recognised in classical taxonomy; performed in a mehfil (seated audience) style.

Marathi Professional Theatre

Maharashtra pioneered modern Indian theatre:

  • Vishnudas Bhave (1819–1901): Father of Marathi theatre; wrote first Marathi play (Sita Swayamvar, 1843).
  • Bal Gandharva (Narayan Shripad Rajhans, 1888–1967): Most celebrated Marathi theatre star; performed female roles (stri-bhumika) with extraordinary voice and grace; the Bal Gandharva Rangamandir in Pune is named after him.
  • Sangeet Natak (Natyasangeet): musical theatre where songs advance narrative; a uniquely Marathi contribution; composers like Govindrao Tembe and Keshavrao Bhosale shaped this tradition.
  • Kirloskar Natak Mandali (1880s): major early professional theatre company; popularised Sangeet Natak.

Exam Traps

  • Lavani ≠ Tamasha: Lavani is the song-dance element within Tamasha; Tamasha is the full theatre form.
  • Kudiyattam (Kerala) got UNESCO recognition in 2008 — the first Indian performing art on the UNESCO Intangible Heritage list; often confused with Ramleela which also has UNESCO recognition.
  • Annabhau Sathe was Dalit (Matang community), not Brahmin — his Tamasha and writings explicitly challenged caste; this distinction matters for MPSC social history questions.
  • Bal Gandharva's real name: Narayan Shripad Rajhans — don't confuse his stage identity with his birth name.
