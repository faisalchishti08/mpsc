Dnyaneshwar — Dnyaneshwari PREMAINSMH

Overview

Sant Dnyaneshwar (also Jnanadeva; 1275–1296 CE) is the foundational saint of the Warkari Sampradaya of Maharashtra and one of the greatest philosopher-poet saints of India. His Dnyaneshwari (1290 CE) — a Marathi commentary on the Bhagavad Gita — is the most celebrated work in Marathi literature and one of the reasons Marathi received Classical Language status in 2024. He died young at Alandi near Pune through Sanjeevan Samadhi at the age of 21. MPSC treats Dnyaneshwar as a major topic: his works, family, philosophy, and legacy.

In-Depth

Life

  • Born: 1275 CE, Apegaon village, Paithan Taluka, Aurangabad district (now Chhatrapati Sambhajinagar district), Maharashtra.
  • Family: Father Vitthal Pant (Vitthalpant); mother Rukmini Bai.
  • Siblings: Nivruttinath (elder brother, guru), Sopandev, Muktabai (sister — also a saint).
  • Guru: Nivruttinath (his own elder brother); Nivruttinath's guru was Gahininath (Nath Sampradaya).
  • Death: Alandi, Pune district; Sanjeevan Samadhi (voluntary, alive burial) at age 21 (~1296 CE); Alandi is now a major pilgrimage site.
  • Family background: Father Vitthal Pant had renounced family life before marriage (Sannyasa); on guru's orders he returned and married — making his children outsiders to the Brahmin community. The children faced severe social ostracism. Dnyaneshwar and siblings sought recognition from the Panchang (Brahmin community) at Paithan.

Dnyaneshwari — Key Features

  • Full title: Bhavartha Dipika ("Lamp of the Inner Meaning").
  • Written in Ovi metre (distinctive 4-line Marathi metre: first three lines rhyme, fourth is different).
  • Dictated to his disciple Sachidananda Baba; completed at Nevase, Ahmednagar district.
  • Interpreted the Sanskrit Bhagavad Gita for ordinary Marathi-speaking people — democratising Vedantic knowledge.
  • Blends Vedanta (particularly Advaita influenced), Nath Sampradaya yoga traditions, and Vaishnava bhakti in a unique synthesis.
  • Considered the foundation of Marathi literature (with Mukundaraj's Viveksindhu as slightly earlier but less celebrated).

Philosophy

  • Advaita-flavoured Vishishtadvaita: synthesises non-dualism with devotion; Dnyaneshwar is not a pure Advaita Vedantist.
  • Nath Sampradaya (of Gorakshanath): Nivruttinath brought hatha-yoga/Nath tradition; Dnyaneshwar integrated it with bhakti.
  • Central teaching: Knowledge (Dnyana) and Devotion (Bhakti) are not opposed — the Bhagavad Gita's teachings encompass both; "Amhi jato amuchya gava, amcha rama rama vava" (Let us go to our home, chanting Rama's name).

Alandi and Pilgrimage

  • Alandi (Indrayani river, Pune district): site of Dnyaneshwar's Sanjeevan Samadhi and his temple.
  • The dindi (palanquin) of Dnyaneshwar leads the main Wari procession from Alandi to Pandharpur every Ashadhi Ekadashi — the most sacred procession of the Warkari tradition.
  • Thousands of devotees join the Alandi-to-Pandharpur walk (~250 km).

Legacy

  • Warkari Sampradaya: Dnyaneshwar is the foundational saint; subsequent Warkaris (Namdev, Eknath, Tukaram) built on his legacy.
  • Marathi Language: his Ovi compositions are the gold standard of Marathi literary style; cited in Marathi Classical Language status petition.
  • Abhangas by Dnyaneshwar are still sung at Pandharpur.
  • Amrutanubhava: many scholars consider it a more original philosophical achievement than even Dnyaneshwari.

Maharashtra Connection

Dnyaneshwar is central to Maharashtra's cultural identity:

  • Dnyaneshwari (1290 CE): founding text of Marathi literature; in Ovi metre; commentary on Bhagavad Gita.
  • Alandi (Pune district): his samadhi; pilgrimage start-point.
  • Wari tradition: Dnyaneshwar's dindi leads Ashadhi Ekadashi Wari from Alandi to Pandharpur.
  • Born in Apegaon, Paithan Taluka, Aurangabad — both districts have deep Dnyaneshwar connections.
  • Nevase (Ahmednagar): where Dnyaneshwari was completed/dictated; Nevase Vitthal Temple.
  • Marathi Classical Language status (2024) explicitly cites Dnyaneshwari as foundational literary evidence.

Exam Traps

  • Dnyaneshwari = commentary on Bhagavad Gita, NOT Ramayana, NOT Upanishads.
  • Amrutanubhava ≠ Dnyaneshwari — two separate works.
  • Sanjeevan Samadhi: voluntary; at age 21; NOT death by illness.
  • Guru was Nivruttinath (his own brother) — unusual; candidates sometimes guess Ramanuja or another saint.
