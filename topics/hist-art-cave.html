Cave architecture — Ajanta, Ellora, Elephanta (Maharashtra) PREMAINSMH

Overview

Cave architecture represents India's earliest surviving monumental architecture — rock-cut shrines, monasteries and temples carved from living rock by Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain communities between the 3rd century BCE and 8th century CE. Ajanta, Ellora, and Elephanta — all in Maharashtra — are among the most significant in the world, all UNESCO World Heritage Sites. Extremely high MPSC MH frequency.

In-Depth

Types of Rock-Cut Architecture

  • Chaitya griha: Prayer hall with a stupa at the far end; horseshoe-shaped facade with a large arch window (chaitya arch/window).
  • Vihara: Monastery; rooms for monks around a central courtyard.
  • Lena / Guha: Generic cave shrine.

Ajanta Caves (Maharashtra)

Location: Aurangabad district, carved into a horseshoe-shaped gorge on the Waghora river; 30 caves.

Paintings — the defining feature of Ajanta:

  • Considered the finest surviving examples of ancient Indian painting; using fresco secco (pigment on dry plaster; some argue true fresco).
  • Themes: Jataka tales (the Buddha's previous lives), scenes of life at the Vakataka royal court, depictions of trade, nature.
  • Cave 1: finest paintings — Bodhisattva Padmapani (holding lotus) and Bodhisattva Vajrapani (most celebrated murals); also narrative panels of Jataka tales.
  • Cave 2: ceiling decoration; Jataka panels.
  • Cave 16: The Dying Princess — considered the most moving painting.
  • Cave 17: largest number of intact paintings.
  • Pigments derived from minerals: lapis lazuli (blue), ochre (yellow, red), lamp black (black).
  • Rediscovery by the British: John Smith of the 28th Madras Cavalry in April 1819 — while on a tiger hunt; local people already knew of the caves.

Architecture: Cave 9 and 10 are earliest chaitya halls (2nd century BCE); cave 19 and 26 are Mahayana-period chaityas with elaborate stone Buddha figures.

UNESCO WHS: Ajanta Caves (1983).

Location: Aurangabad district (30 km from Aurangabad city); 34 caves; multi-religious:

Cave 16 — Kailasa Temple: The crown jewel:

  • Commissioned by Rashtrakuta king Krishna I (~757–775 CE).
  • Carved top-down from a single basalt rock hillside — an approach requiring pre-planning without error.
  • Covers more area than the Parthenon in Athens; 90 m long, 50 m wide, 30 m high.
  • Depicts the Ramayana, Mahabharata and Puranic scenes in bas-relief.
  • Dedicated to Shiva; the central tower represents Mt. Kailash.

Cave 10 (Buddhist) — "Vishvakarma" or Carpenter's Cave: a magnificent chaitya hall with a Buddha in teaching posture (dharmachakra mudra); 7th century CE.

UNESCO WHS: Ellora Caves (1983).

Elephanta Caves (Maharashtra)

Location: Gharapuri island in Mumbai Harbour; 7 km from the Gateway of India; accessible by boat.

  • Cave 1 (the Great Cave): the main Hindu rock-cut temple; dedicated to Shiva.
  • Dates to 6th century CE (probably Kalachuri dynasty, possibly early Chalukya or Rashtrakuta).
  • Trimurti (Maheshmurti) — the centrepiece: a monumental 6-metre triple-faced image of Shiva as Creator (Brahma), Preserver (Vishnu), Destroyer (Rudra/Mahesh) — one of the greatest sculptures in Indian art.
  • Other panels: Nataraja (Shiva as lord of dance), Ardhanarishvara (half Shiva, half Parvati), Gangadhara (Shiva receiving Ganga).
  • Damaged during Portuguese rule (used for target practice).
  • UNESCO WHS: 1987.

Karla and Bhaja Caves (Maharashtra)

  • Karla (or Karle): near Lonavala, Pune district; Cave 8 — one of the largest and oldest chaitya halls in India (2nd c. BCE); 45 m long; finest Hinayana chaitya.
  • Bhaja caves: also near Lonavala; 22 caves; 2nd c. BCE; notable for a vihara and chaitya; famous for a relief carving of a warrior on an elephant.
  • Nasik caves (Pandu Lena): near Nasik city; 24 caves; Hinayana Buddhist (1st c. BCE to 3rd c. CE).
  • Pitalkhora: in Aurangabad district; earliest group (~3rd c. BCE); shows Mauryan influence.

Maharashtra Connection

Maharashtra is the single most important state for cave architecture in India:

  • Ajanta (Aurangabad) — greatest painting site.
  • Ellora (Aurangabad) — multi-religious; Kailasa temple.
  • Elephanta (Mumbai) — greatest Shaiva sculpture.
  • Karla, Bhaja (Pune) — oldest surviving rock-cut chaityas.
  • Nasik, Pitalkhora, Aurangabad caves — additional Buddhist sites.
  • UNESCO WHS: Ajanta (1983), Ellora (1983), Elephanta (1987) — three of Maharashtra's heritage jewels.

Key Facts

  • Ajanta: 30 caves, Aurangabad, on Waghora river; 1983 UNESCO WHS.
  • Rediscovered by John Smith, April 1819.
  • Finest painting: Cave 1 (Bodhisattva Padmapani and Vajrapani).
  • Ellora: 34 caves — 12 Buddhist, 17 Hindu, 5 Jain; 1983 UNESCO WHS.
  • Kailasa temple (Ellora Cave 16): Rashtrakuta Krishna I, ~757–775 CE; top-down rock-cut.
  • Elephanta: Gharapuri island, Mumbai, 6th c. CE; 1987 UNESCO WHS.
  • Trimurti at Elephanta: 6-metre triple-faced Shiva (greatest Indian sculpture).
  • Karla cave: largest/oldest chaitya hall in India (2nd c. BCE), near Lonavala.

Exam Traps

  • Ajanta = paintings; Ellora = sculptures/architecture — they are 30 km apart; both UNESCO but different primary fame.
  • Kailasa temple is Cave 16 at ELLORA, not Ajanta — common mix-up.
  • Elephanta's Trimurti is Shiva (not Brahma) as the central figure — it represents Shiva in three aspects, not the Brahma-Vishnu-Shiva triad.
  • Ajanta was known locally before 1819 — "rediscovery" by John Smith is a British/colonial framing; the caves were never truly "lost."
