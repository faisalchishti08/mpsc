Maharashtra Lokayukta PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

The Maharashtra Lokayukta is an independent anti-corruption and public grievance institution established under the Maharashtra Lokayukta and Upa-Lokayuktas Act, 1971. It investigates complaints against public functionaries including ministers, legislators, and officers. Maharashtra was among the first states to establish a Lokayukta institution. MPSC tests the Act year, composition, jurisdiction, and powers.

In-Depth

Legislative Basis

  • Maharashtra Lokayukta and Upa-Lokayuktas Act, 1971 — one of India's earliest state Lokayukta laws.
  • Based on the recommendations of the First Administrative Reforms Commission (ARC, 1966–70) headed by Morarji Desai, which recommended Ombudsman-type institutions at state level.
  • The concept is derived from the Scandinavian Ombudsman tradition.
  • The Lokayukta's annual report is presented to the Governor and laid before the state legislature.

Powers and Functions

  1. Investigation: Investigates complaints of corruption, maladministration, or gross negligence against public functionaries within jurisdiction.
  2. Suo motu action: Can initiate investigation on its own, without a formal complaint.
  3. Summoning: Can summon documents, records, and witnesses.
  4. Recommendation: Can recommend to the Governor/CM:
  • Removal of the concerned official
  • Prosecution under Prevention of Corruption Act
  • Award of compensation to the complainant
  1. No executive power: Lokayukta cannot directly punish — it can only recommend; the state government may or may not comply (a major limitation).

Key Features and Criticisms

  • CM excluded: Maharashtra's Lokayukta Act exempts the Chief Minister from its jurisdiction — a fundamental weakness. Critics argue this makes the institution partially toothless.
  • No suo motu jurisdiction over CM: Even if there are public reports of CM corruption, Lokayukta cannot independently investigate.
  • Karnataka contrast: Karnataka's Lokayukta (under Justice S.R. Hegde) was much stronger — it had powers to investigate CMs and even resulted in the resignation of a Karnataka CM (B.S. Yediyurappa, 2011). Maharashtra's is comparatively weaker.
  • Recommendations not binding: The government may ignore Lokayukta recommendations — there is no mechanism to enforce compliance.

Maharashtra ACB (Anti-Corruption Bureau): The Maharashtra ACB is the executive police agency for corruption investigation — conducts trap operations, registers cases under Prevention of Corruption Act. Distinct from the Lokayukta (quasi-judicial investigative body).

Maharashtra Connection

Maharashtra's Lokayukta was an early institution (1971) but has been criticised for weak powers — particularly the CM exemption. The 2013 Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act (Central) required states to also have Lokayuktas — Maharashtra already had one. High-profile investigations by the Lokayukta against ministers have occasionally led to resignations and prosecutions in Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • Established under: Maharashtra Lokayukta and Upa-Lokayuktas Act, 1971
  • One of India's earliest Lokayukta laws
  • Lokayukta: Retired SC judge or HC Chief Justice; appointed by Governor; tenure 5 years
  • CM excluded from Lokayukta's jurisdiction — a key criticism
  • Recommendations: advisory, not binding on government
  • Lokpal (national): Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013 (central law)
  • Maharashtra ACB: separate from Lokayukta; police investigation body

Exam Traps

  • Maharashtra Lokayukta was established in 1971 — NOT 1991 or 2013.
  • The Chief Minister is NOT within Maharashtra Lokayukta's jurisdiction (as of original Act).
  • Lokayukta recommendations are NOT binding — only advisory; state may or may not comply.
