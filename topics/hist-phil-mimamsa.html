Mimamsa PREMAINS

Overview

Mimamsa (also Purva Mimamsa = "prior/early inquiry") is one of India's six orthodox philosophical schools, founded by Jaimini and systematised in the Mimamasasutras (~4th c. BCE–1st c. CE). It is the school of Vedic ritual interpretation — arguing that the Vedas are eternal, authorless, and infallible, and that the performance of Vedic rituals (karma) leads to liberation. Mimamsa is uniquely ritualistic and stands in contrast to Vedanta's emphasis on knowledge. MPSC tests it primarily alongside the other five schools.

In-Depth

Core Philosophy

Eternal Vedas (Vedapaurusheya): Mimamsa's most fundamental claim — the Vedas were not authored by any human or divine being; they are eternal (nitya), self-authoritative (swatahpramanya), and infallible. This is the strongest assertion of Vedic authority among all schools.

Dharma as the Central Concern: Mimamsa is primarily an interpretation of the Vedic injunctions (vidhi) — what one ought to do (positive injunctions) and what one ought not to do (negative prohibitions). Dharma = that which is commanded by the Vedas.

Karma-Kanda: Mimamsa focuses exclusively on the karma-kanda (ritual action section) of the Vedas — the sacrificial injunctions in the Samhitas and Brahmanas — rather than the jnana-kanda (knowledge section = Upanishads, which Vedanta focuses on).

Theory of Liberation: Early Mimamsa did not have a clear liberation doctrine (focused on dharma and heavenly rewards from rituals). Later Mimamsakas (especially Kumarila Bhatta and Prabhakara) developed the position that the fruit of Vedic action performed without desire (nishkama karma) purifies the soul and ultimately leads to liberation.

Kumarila Bhatta's anti-Buddhist arguments were so powerful that he is credited (in tradition) with contributing to the intellectual decline of Buddhism in India. He debated Buddhist scholars and demolished their philosophical positions using Vedic epistemology.

Epistemology

Mimamsa accepts six pramanas (sources of valid knowledge) — the most in any Indian school:

  1. Pratyaksha (perception)
  2. Anumana (inference)
  3. Upamana (comparison)
  4. Shabda (verbal testimony)
  5. Arthapatti (postulation/presumption — inferring a necessary fact from a given situation)
  6. Anupalabdhi (non-perception — perceiving the absence of something)

Arthapatti example: "Devadatta is alive but not at home" → He must be somewhere else (postulating a necessary conclusion). This is Mimamsa's most original epistemological contribution.

God in Mimamsa

Early Mimamsa did not require God — the Vedas are self-authoritative and eternal without needing a divine author. This makes early Mimamsa practically atheistic on the question of creation (though it does not deny God's existence, it finds him irrelevant to ritual duty).

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. However:

  • The Agnihotra ritual (Vedic fire sacrifice) and related Vedic yajnas have continued in the Brahmin communities of Maharashtra (especially the Chitpavan Brahmins who dominated the Peshwa administration); these communities maintained Mimamsa-based ritual learning.
  • Pune's Sanskrit scholars (under Peshwa patronage) engaged with Mimamsa interpretation in their Dharmashastra texts.

Key Facts

  • Founder: Jaimini; text: Mimamasasutras.
  • Focus: Vedic ritual interpretation; Karma-Kanda (not Upanishads).
  • Vedas are eternal, authorless (Vedapaurusheya).
  • Six pramanas: adds Arthapatti and Anupalabdhi to the standard four.
  • Major commentators: Shabara, Kumarila Bhatta, Prabhakara.
  • Kumarila Bhatta — powerful anti-Buddhist polemicist (7th c. CE).
  • Paired with: Vedanta (Purva/Uttara Mimamsa).

Exam Traps

  • Mimamsa focuses on Karma-Kanda (rituals), NOT Jnana-Kanda (Upanishads) — that is Vedanta.
  • Arthapatti (postulation) is Mimamsa's unique pramana — don't confuse with anumana (inference, which is Nyaya's specialty).
  • Early Mimamsa was essentially atheistic on God-as-creator — later developments added liberation doctrine; this is a common confusion.
