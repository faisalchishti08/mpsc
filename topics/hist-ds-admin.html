Sultanate administration, economy, society PREMAINS

Overview

Delhi Sultanate administration (1206–1526) shaped north India's governance for three centuries under five dynasties — Slave, Khilji, Tughlaq, Sayyid and Lodi. It introduced a Persian-Turkic administrative model overlaid on existing Indian structures, creating institutions (Iqta, Diwan-i-Wizarat) that influenced later Mughal governance. MPSC asks both factual MCQs (ministries, taxes) and Mains questions on the socioeconomic impact of Sultanate rule.

In-Depth

Central Administration

The Sultan was the supreme authority — head of state, army and judiciary. He was aided by four key ministries:

Ministry (Diwan)HeadFunction
Diwan-i-WizaratWazirFinance; the most powerful minister
Diwan-i-ArizAriz-i-MumalikMilitary affairs (not Commander-in-Chief)
Diwan-i-InshaDabir-i-KhasRoyal correspondence and decrees
Diwan-i-RisalatChief SadrReligious affairs, grants, appeals

Alauddin Khilji added Diwan-i-Mustakhraj (a special department to recover revenue arrears) and strengthened the Diwan-i-Bandagan for slave management.

Provincial and Local Administration

  • Iqta system — the Sultanate's defining fiscal-administrative tool. The Sultan assigned iqta (revenue-yielding territories) to military officers (muqtis/walis) in lieu of cash salary. They collected revenue, maintained troops, and remitted surplus to the centre. Iltutmish systematised the iqta; Alauddin attempted to pay soldiers in cash and curtail iqta abuses; Muhammad bin Tughlaq revived and expanded it.
  • Below the province (iqta): shiq (district), pargana, and village (headed by muqaddam).
  • Chief village officer: Patwari maintained land records; Chowdhury assisted revenue collection.

Revenue and Economy

Land revenue (kharaj) was the principal resource, typically 1/3 to 1/2 of produce depending on ruler and region. Under Alauddin Khilji it was fixed at 50% of produce for the doab area. Other taxes:

  • Jizya — poll tax on non-Muslims; later abolished by Akbar.
  • Zakat — 2.5% wealth tax on Muslims.
  • Khamsa — 1/5 of war booty to the state; 4/5 to soldiers.

Alauddin Khilji's market reforms (Sabazon ka Bazar, Kapra Bazar, Mandi) fixed prices for grain, cloth and cattle — a pioneering attempt at price control recorded in detail by Ziauddin Barani's Tarikh-i-Firuz Shahi.

Firuz Shah Tughlaq created vast irrigation infrastructure — four major canals including the Rajput Canal from Sutlej; introduced agriculture grants (takkavi loans) and prohibited illegal taxes (over 20 unauthorised cesses removed). He also instituted a public works department (Diwan-i-Khairat).

Society

  • Three-tier social order: Turkish/Afghan ruling nobility; ulema (Islamic scholars, judges); Hindu majority (peasants, artisans, traders).
  • Slavery was institutionalised — Iltutmish himself was a slave; the Slave dynasty's power rested on trained Turkish and Abyssinian slaves (bandagan-i-khas).
  • Alauddin tried to break the hold of the four pillars of power: wealth, heredity, espionage, and marriage alliances — imposing stringent rules forbidding nobles from intermarrying without royal consent.
  • Trade flourished under the Sultanate: Arab and Central Asian merchants, Indian guilds (shrenis), and the growth of Delhi, Lahore, and Daulatabad as commercial hubs.

Judiciary

  • Qazi-ul-Quzat (Chief Justice) applied Sharia law.
  • Muhtasibs enforced public morality.
  • The Sultan also dispensed justice personally (mazalim court — court of complaints).
  • Non-Muslims followed their own personal law administered by Hindu panchayas or village councils, with Sultanate courts handling criminal and revenue matters.

Maharashtra Connection

The Deccan came under Sultanate influence primarily under Muhammad bin Tughlaq who shifted the capital to Daulatabad (Devgiri) in 1327 — a short-lived experiment affecting Maharashtra directly. The break-up of Tughlaq power in the Deccan led to the Bahmani Sultanate (1347) headquartered in Gulbarga (present-day Karnataka/Maharashtra region). The Yadava kingdom of Devgiri (Maharashtra's medieval successor to Rashtrakutas) was subjugated by Alauddin Khilji in 1296.

Key Facts

  • Four key ministries: Wizarat, Ariz, Insha, Risalat.
  • Iqta system systematised by Iltutmish.
  • Alauddin's kharaj: 50% of produce in the doab.
  • Market reforms by Alauddin: three main mandis (grain, cloth, cattle).
  • Capital shifted to Daulatabad: Muhammad bin Tughlaq, 1327.
  • Firuz Tughlaq's canals: 4 major canals from Yamuna and Sutlej.
  • Jizya abolished later by Akbar (1564).
  • Chief Justice title: Qazi-ul-Quzat.

Memory Aids

  • Four ministries → "WAIR": Wizarat, Ariz, Insha, Risalat.
  • Iqta → "I-L-A-M-F": Introduced (Iltutmish), Abused (Alauddin reformed it), Muhammad expanded, Firuz continued.
  • Alauddin's 3 sabzis = 3 markets: grain, cloth, cattle.

Exam Traps

  • Ariz-i-Mumalik was NOT the Commander-in-Chief — he administered the army but the Sultan commanded; don't confuse with Senapati.
  • Iqta ≠ jagir: Iqta was in theory revocable/transferable and assigned revenue rights (not land ownership); jagir (Mughal) was later and more hereditary.
  • Firuz Shah Tughlaq is often misread as a reformer ignoring his reinstatement of jizya (he extended it to Brahmins who had earlier been exempt).
  • Muhammad bin Tughlaq shifted capital to Daulatabad (not Deogiri separately) — same fort renamed.
