Rashtrakutas (Maharashtra) — Ellora, Kailasa temple PREMAINSMH

Overview

The Rashtrakuta dynasty (753–973 CE) was one of the most powerful empires of medieval India, ruling from Manyakheta (Malkhed, Gulbarga district, Karnataka) and controlling most of the Deccan and large parts of north and south India at their peak. They are critical for MPSC (MH tag) because: their core territory included Maharashtra; they created the Kailasa Temple at Ellora (Cave 16) — the greatest rock-cut monument in the world; and their multilingual court produced early Kannada and Apabhramsha literature. They fought the tripartite struggle for Kanauj with the Gurjara-Pratiharas and Palas.

In-Depth

Rise and Origins

The Rashtrakutas overthrew the Chalukyas of Badami. Dantidurga (c. 735–756 CE) — the founder — defeated the Chalukya king Kirtivarman II and established Rashtrakuta supremacy. He performed a Hiranyagarbha (golden womb) ceremony at Ujjain, where the Gurjara-Pratihara king held the golden umbrella for him — a symbolic assertion of paramount power.

Kailasa Temple — Ellora Cave 16

The Kailasa Temple (Ellora Cave 16) is the supreme monument of the Rashtrakutas and one of the greatest architectural achievements of the world:

  • Built by Krishna I (c. 756–773 CE) as a tribute to Shiva (Mount Kailash).
  • Cut from a single monolithic rock from the top downward — approximately 200,000 tonnes of rock excavated; no external materials used.
  • Dimensions: 82 m long × 47 m wide × 33 m high — larger than the Parthenon in Athens.
  • The complex includes: main shikhara (tower), mandapa (hall), 16 subsidiary shrines, Nandi shrine, victory columns (dhvajastambha), elephant statues, galleries.
  • The Ravana-anugraha panel (Ravana shaking Mount Kailash with Parvati clinging to Shiva) is among the most celebrated Indian sculptures.
  • Style: Dravidian in the main tower; Nagara elements in the subsidiary shrines; synthesises both — hence "Vesara" style origin point.
  • UNESCO World Heritage Site: Ellora Caves declared WHS in 1983 (same year as Ajanta).

Ellora Caves Complex — Full Picture

Ellora contains 34 caves:

  • Caves 1–12: Buddhist (mainly 5th–7th century CE) — e.g., Cave 10 (Vishvakarma — large Buddhist chaitya), Cave 12 (Tin Thal — three-storied monastery).
  • Caves 13–29: Hindu (5th–11th century CE) — Cave 16 (Kailasa) is the crown.
  • Caves 30–34: Jain (8th–11th century CE) — intricate carvings; Cave 32 (Indra Sabha) notable.

This unique co-existence of Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain caves at one site reflects the Rashtrakuta policy of religious tolerance.

Political Reach and Tripartite Struggle

The Rashtrakutas intervened repeatedly in the tripartite struggle for Kanauj between three imperial powers:

  • Gurjara-Pratiharas (NW India, Rajasthan).
  • Palas (Bengal-Bihar).
  • Rashtrakutas (Deccan).

Each power fought the other two for control of the Gangetic plain and the symbolic prize of Kanauj.

Govinda III (c. 793–814 CE) was the most successful — he defeated both Pratiharas and Palas and even received tribute from the king of Sri Lanka. Arab traveller Sulaiman (851 CE) called the Rashtrakuta kingdom (Balhara) one of the four great empires of the world (alongside the Caliphate, Byzantium, and Tang China).

Culture and Literature

  • Amoghavarsha I: composed Kavirajamarga — the earliest extant work of Kannada literature; also composed Prashnotara Ratnamalika (Sanskrit). He was a devoted Jain and reportedly cut off his finger as an offering to Jain deity Manikya Nandi.
  • Adikavi Pampa, Ponna, Ranna ("Three Gems of Kannada") flourished under Later Rashtrakutas.
  • Sanskrit poet Trivikrama Bhatta wrote Nalachampu (earliest champu — prose-verse mixed) in Rashtrakuta court.
  • The Apabhramsha language (precursor to Marathi) was used in court — early evidence of proto-Marathi.

Maharashtra Connection

Core Maharashtra topic. The Rashtrakuta heartland was in the Deccan — present-day Karnataka and Maharashtra:

  • Capital Manyakheta (Malkhed) is near the Maharashtra–Karnataka border.
  • Ellora Caves (Aurangabad district, Maharashtra) — UNESCO WHS 1983.
  • Kailasa Temple: built by Krishna I — one of Maharashtra's greatest heritage sites.
  • The Rashtrakutas ruled Marathwada, Vidarbha, and Konkan.
  • They are the immediate predecessors of the Yadava dynasty (which ruled from Devagiri, Maharashtra).
  • Early Marathi language development occurred under Rashtrakuta patronage.

Key Facts

  • Dynasty founder: Dantidurga (c. 735 CE); overthrew Chalukyas of Badami.
  • Kailasa Temple: built by Krishna I; Ellora Cave 16; single rock cut; height 33 m.
  • Ellora: 34 caves (Buddhist 1–12, Hindu 13–29, Jain 30–34); UNESCO WHS 1983.
  • Longest-reigning Rashtrakuta: Amoghavarsha I (64 years).
  • Kavirajamarga: by Amoghavarsha I — earliest extant Kannada literary text.
  • Arab traveller Sulaiman (851 CE): called Rashtrakuta kingdom one of 4 great world empires.
  • Greatest military ruler: Govinda III — defeated both Pratiharas and Palas.
  • Dynasty ended: 973 CE by Tailapa II (Later Chalukya).

Exam Traps

  • Ellora's Kailasa Temple is Cave 16 (Hindu); do NOT say Cave 10 (Buddhist chaitya) or Cave 32 (Jain).
  • Kailasa Temple was built by Krishna I, not Dantidurga or Amoghavarsha.
  • Ellora and Ajanta are both in Aurangabad district and both UNESCO WHS 1983 — same year, two sites.
  • The Rashtrakuta capital was Manyakheta (Malkhed) in Karnataka, not in Maharashtra — though their territory included Maharashtra.
