Akbar — conquests, Din-i-Ilahi, Mansabdari PREMAINS

Overview

Akbar (1542–1605, r. 1556–1605) is widely regarded as the greatest of the Mughal emperors, who consolidated the empire across the subcontinent, developed a sophisticated administrative system (Mansabdari, Zabt), and promoted a syncretic religious philosophy (Din-i-Ilahi) and cultural synthesis. His reign is the pivot of Mughal history and MPSC's single most-asked Mughal topic — Prelims (Mansabdari, Din-i-Ilahi, Nine Gems) and Mains (religious policy, administration, art).

In-Depth

Early Reign and Consolidation

Akbar was born at Umarkot (Sindh) in 1542 and became emperor at 13 after Humayun's death (1556). Real power initially held by regent Bairam Khan (1556–1560).

Second Battle of Panipat (1556): Akbar/Bairam Khan defeated Hemu (Hindu minister of Adali Shah Sur) who had briefly captured Delhi. Hemu was wounded, captured and executed; the Mughal throne secured.

Key conquests:

  • Malwa (1561), Gondwana (1564), Rajputana.
  • Chittor (1568) and Ranthambore (1569): two most important Rajput forts captured.
  • Gujarat (1572–73): gave Akbar access to the sea and Portuguese trade.
  • Bengal (1576): Battle of Rajmahal — Bengal governor Daud Khan defeated.
  • Kabul (1581), Kashmir (1586), Sindh (1591), Orissa (1591).
  • Deccan campaigns (1590s): Ahmednagar, Khandesh, Berar — partial control.

Rajput Policy

Akbar's most celebrated political innovation: alliance with Rajputs through matrimony and service rather than subjugation:

  • Married Harka Bai (Jodha Bai) of Amber (Jaipur), daughter of Raja Bihar Mal — first Hindu queen in the Mughal harem.
  • Rajput nobles appointed to high Mansab ranks (Raja Todar Mal — revenue; Raja Man Singh — general).
  • Abolished jizya (1564) on non-Muslims.
  • Allowed Rajput traditions in court; respected Hindu festivals.
  • Resistant Rajputs (Rana Pratap of Mewar) faced military action — Battle of Haldighati (1576) (Akbar's general Man Singh vs Rana Pratap).

Mansabdari System

The defining administrative system of the Mughal Empire:

ConceptDetails
MansabA rank (from 10 to 10,000 and above) held by mansabdars
ZatPersonal rank determining pay and status
SawarNumber of cavalry (horsemen) to be maintained
JagirRevenue assignment to pay for troops (not ownership)
JagirdarMansabdar who held a jagir (most did)
  • Ranks were granted/revoked at the Emperor's will — not hereditary.
  • Highest mansab (10,000) usually reserved for princes.
  • Todar Mal (revenue minister) and Abul Fazl (chief secretary/chronicler) were key non-military mansabdars.

Revenue System — Zabt (Todar Mal's Bandobast)

Akbar's revenue system under Raja Todar Mal:

  • Ain-i-Dahsala (1580): ten-year average of prices and yields → fixed revenue demand.
  • Land classified by quality and productivity; revenue paid in cash (not kind).
  • Zabt (also called Todar Mal's Bandobast): standardised revenue across the empire.
  • Sikandari gaz (measuring rod) for land measurement.

Din-i-Ilahi (1582)

Akbar's syncretic religious movement — not a new religion but a discipleship (silsila) centred on the emperor:

  • Drew from Islam, Hinduism, Jainism, Zoroastrianism, Christianity.
  • Followers pledged personal loyalty to Akbar; no forced conversion.
  • Only ~18 followers (almost entirely courtiers); Birbal was the only Hindu to join.
  • Preceded by Ibadat Khana (House of Worship, 1575) at Fatehpur Sikri — weekly religious debates.
  • Sulh-i-Kul (universal peace) was the underlying principle.

Architecture

  • Fatehpur Sikri — built near Agra (1571–85); new capital; houses Jama Masjid, Buland Darwaza (commemorating Gujarat conquest), Diwan-i-Aam, Diwan-i-Khas, Panch Mahal, Ibadat Khana; UNESCO World Heritage Site.
  • Agra Fort (red sandstone) rebuilt in its current form under Akbar.

Maharashtra Connection

Akbar's Deccan campaigns (1590s) brought Mughal authority into Maharashtra for the first time — Khandesh (Asirgarh fort, 1601) and Berar (now Vidarbha) came under Mughal control. Ahmednagar resisted under Chand Bibi (defender against Akbar's son Murad's siege). The Mughal-Deccan contact under Akbar set the stage for Aurangzeb's prolonged Deccan wars.

Key Facts

  • Born: 1542, Umarkot (Sindh); became emperor 1556 at age 13.
  • Regent initially: Bairam Khan (1556–1560).
  • Second Battle of Panipat: 1556 vs Hemu.
  • Jizya abolished: 1564.
  • Married Harka Bai (Jodha Bai) of Amber — Rajput matrimonial alliance.
  • Battle of Haldighati: 1576 vs Rana Pratap.
  • Mansabdari: zat (personal) + sawar (cavalry) ranks.
  • Revenue: Ain-i-Dahsala (1580), Todar Mal.
  • Din-i-Ilahi introduced: 1582; only ~18 followers; Birbal only Hindu.
  • Fatehpur Sikri built: 1571–85; capital shifted back to Agra 1585.
  • Court chronicle: Akbarnama by Abul Fazl.

Exam Traps

  • Din-i-Ilahi ≠ a new religion — it was a discipleship order; Akbar did NOT convert people; no scriptural text.
  • Birbal ≠ Todar Mal — Birbal (humour/wit) and Raja Todar Mal (revenue) are two different Navaratnas; both Hindus but different roles.
  • Second Battle of Panipat (1556) ≠ Third (1761) — Second is Akbar vs Hemu; Third is Ahmad Shah Abdali vs Marathas.
  • Jizya abolished by Akbar (1564), not by Babur or Humayun. Aurangzeb reinstated jizya (1679).
