Chandragupta Maurya and Chanakya PREMAINS

Overview

Chandragupta Maurya (~321–297 BCE) was the founder of the Mauryan Empire — the first pan-Indian empire that unified most of the subcontinent under a single administration for the first time. Aided by Chanakya (Kautilya/Vishnugupta), he overthrew the Nanda dynasty of Magadha (~321 BCE), expelled Macedonian garrisons from the northwest (~322–319 BCE), and expanded to include even parts of Afghanistan (after defeating Seleucus I Nicator in ~305 BCE). MPSC tests on the Chandragupta-Chanakya relationship, the defeat of the Nandas, the Seleucid treaty, and Chandragupta's religious conversion.

In-Depth

Chanakya (Kautilya/Vishnugupta) — The Mastermind

Chanakya is one of the most significant political theorists in Indian history:

  • Born: Taxila (according to tradition); Brahmin; possibly from the Chanaka gotra.
  • Known by three names: Chanakya (father's name Chanaka), Kautilya (pen name — "crooked/crafty"), Vishnugupta (given name).
  • His political treatise: Arthashastra — comprehensive manual on statecraft, economics, foreign policy, military strategy, and law (rediscovered in 1905 by R. Shamasastry).
  • Motivation for helping Chandragupta: according to tradition, humiliated by the last Nanda king (Dhana Nanda) — vowed revenge and found the perfect instrument in the young Chandragupta.

Rise to Power — Defeating the Nandas

Traditional accounts (Mudrarakshasa by Vishakhadatta; Jain texts; Classical sources):

  1. Chandragupta was possibly a young man from the frontier region (Moriya/Maurya clan — peacock clan).
  2. He may have met Alexander the Great during the latter's Indian campaign (~326 BCE) — Classical sources call him "Sandrocottos."
  3. Chanakya trained and guided Chandragupta, assembling an army using forest peoples (Atavikas) and frontier soldiers.
  4. Initial strategy: attack the Nanda heartland directly — failed.
  5. Revised strategy (Chanakya's lesson from watching a woman scold her child): attack from the periphery inward. Chandragupta first conquered the frontier provinces and slowly encircled Pataliputra.
  6. ~321 BCE: Dhana Nanda (last Nanda king) defeated; Chandragupta crowned at Pataliputra.

Wars and Expansion

  • ~322–319 BCE: Expelled Macedonian garrisons from Punjab and Sindh left by Alexander's successors.
  • ~305 BCE: War with Seleucus I Nicator (who tried to reconquer Alexander's Indian territories). Chandragupta won decisively.
  • Treaty with Seleucus (~303 BCE): Chandragupta received the territories of Arachosia (Kandahar), Gedrosia (Balochistan), Aria (Herat), and Paropamisadae (Kabul) in exchange for 500 war elephants. Seleucus sent his ambassador Megasthenes to Chandragupta's court.
  • A matrimonial alliance (Seleucus gave his daughter to Chandragupta — or a Seleucid princess) may have been part of the treaty (debated).

Extent of the Empire

Chandragupta's empire at its peak (~297 BCE) covered:

  • Modern India (except deep Tamil South and Kalinga under successors), Pakistan, Afghanistan, and possibly parts of Iran.
  • This is the first time such a large part of the subcontinent came under one administration.

Administration

  • Capital: Pataliputra (modern Patna) — described in detail by Megasthenes.
  • Elaborate bureaucratic system: 30 departments (based on Arthashastra's model) overseeing roads, rivers, markets, public works, espionage, and revenue.
  • The empire was divided into provinces with royal princes as governors (Kumaras).
  • A vast spy network (Guptacharas) monitored officials and citizens.

Abdication and Jain Conversion

According to Jain tradition:

  • Towards end of reign (~297 BCE), Chandragupta learned of an impending famine.
  • Handed over power to his son Bindusara.
  • Accompanied the Jain monk Bhadrabahu south to Shravanabelagola (Karnataka).
  • Spent his final years as a Jain ascetic and died by Sallekhana (ritual fasting unto death) at Shravanabelagola.
  • This account is accepted by Jain tradition and supported by inscriptions at Shravanabelagola mentioning "Chandragupta."

Maharashtra Connection

  • The Sopara/Nalasopara (Palghar district, near Mumbai) Ashokan edict (rock edict) suggests Mauryan administration extended into Maharashtra's Konkan coast by Ashoka's time — the administrative network built by Chandragupta made this possible.
  • Chandragupta's conversion to Jainism and migration to Shravanabelagola (Karnataka) via the Deccan route means he may have passed through the Maharashtra-Karnataka border region.

Key Facts

  • Chandragupta Maurya: founded Mauryan Empire ~321 BCE.
  • Defeated Dhana Nanda (~321 BCE); drove out Macedonians (~322–319 BCE).
  • War with Seleucus I Nicator (~305 BCE): received Arachosia, Gedrosia, Aria, Paropamisadae; gave 500 elephants.
  • Megasthenes: Seleucid ambassador sent to Chandragupta's court.
  • Chanakya's Arthashastra: found 1905 by Shamasastry.
  • Abdicated ~297 BCE; son Bindusara succeeded.
  • Died by Sallekhana at Shravanabelagola (Jain tradition).
  • Chandragupta's three names: Chanakya, Kautilya, Vishnugupta (Chanakya's names).

Exam Traps

  • The three names (Chanakya/Kautilya/Vishnugupta) all refer to the same person — his advisor, NOT Chandragupta.
  • Chandragupta died at Shravanabelagola (Karnataka) by Sallekhana — NOT in Pataliputra, NOT in a battle.
  • Seleucus was defeated by Chandragupta (~305 BCE) — Seleucus was Alexander's general/successor who tried to reclaim India and LOST.
  • Megasthenes' Indica describes Chandragupta's court, NOT Alexander's — Megasthenes came after Alexander.
