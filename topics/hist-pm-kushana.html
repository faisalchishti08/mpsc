Kushanas — Kanishka, Gandhara/Mathura art PREMAINS

Overview

The Kushana dynasty (c. 30–375 CE) was a Central Asian empire that ruled the vast arc from Central Asia through Afghanistan, Pakistan, and northern India up to the Ganga plains. Their greatest ruler Kanishka I (c. 78 CE or 127 CE onwards) was a major Buddhist patron. The Kushanas are studied for Gandhara and Mathura schools of art, the Silk Road trade, the fourth Buddhist council, and as the context for the flowering of Mahayana Buddhism. Perennial MPSC topic with both Prelims (art school ID, Kanishka facts) and Mains (art comparison, Buddhism) angles.

In-Depth

Kushana Origins and Empire

The Kushanas (Guishuang) were one of the five Yüeh-chih tribes of Central Asia who migrated after being displaced by the Xiongnu (Huns) c. 165 BCE. Under Kujula Kadphises, they unified the Yüeh-chih and invaded Bactria and then India (c. 30 CE). His son Vima Kadphises conquered northern India.

Kanishka I — The Greatest Kushana

Kanishka I is the most important Kushana ruler. His exact accession date is debated:

  • 78 CE theory: Kanishka started the Shaka Era (still used in the Indian National Calendar). Most textbooks accept this.
  • 127 CE theory: more recent scholarly consensus; but the 78 CE Shaka Era link to Kanishka is itself debated (some attribute the era to Chashtana).

Key facts about Kanishka:

  • Capital: Purushapura (Peshawar, modern Pakistan).
  • Also held Mathura as second capital.
  • Fourth Buddhist Council: convened by Kanishka in Kundalvan/Kashmir under the presidency of Vasumitra (rapporteur: Ashvaghosha). This council produced commentaries on the Tripitaka (Mahavibhasha) and was mainly attended by Mahayana monks.
  • Patron of Mahayana Buddhism and of great scholars: Ashvaghosha (Buddhist poet, wrote Buddhacharita and Saundarananda), Nagarjuna (Madhyamaka philosopher), Charaka (physician, Charaka Samhita revised in Kanishka's court), Parsva (Buddhist teacher).

Kanishka's Coins and Titulature

Kanishka's coins are uniquely multi-religious: they show Greek gods (Helios, Selene), Iranian gods (Mithra, Nana), and the Buddha — the earliest coin representations of the Buddha. His coins use Greek script initially and later Bactrian (a Greek-alphabet script for an Iranian language). His title: Maharaja Rajatiraja Devaputra Shahanushahi — King of Kings, Son of God, Great King.

Gandhara School of Art

The Gandhara school (also called Greco-Buddhist or Romano-Buddhist art) flourished in modern Afghanistan and Pakistan (ancient Gandhara region — Peshawar valley, Swat valley, Taxila) under Indo-Greek and Kushana patronage.

  • Medium: predominantly blue-grey schist stone (some in stucco/terracotta for later sites).
  • The Buddha depicted with:
  • Greco-Roman features: curly/wavy hair, toga-like sanghati (robe), Apollo-like face.
  • Ushnisha (cranial protuberance — depicted as topknot).
  • Realistic drapery with folded cloth (vs flat stylised Indian tradition).
  • Subjects: Life of the Buddha in narrative panels; Bodhisattvas with mustaches and crowns.
  • Key sites: Taxila, Hadda, Butkara Stupa, Bamiyan (Afghanistan).

Mathura School of Art

The Mathura school developed indigenously in Mathura (Uttar Pradesh) using spotted red sandstone (Sikri sandstone) — no Greek influence.

  • Buddha depicted with:
  • Shaved head or minimal hair (no Apollo-curls).
  • Transparent/clinging robe showing body beneath; right shoulder often bare.
  • Flame ushnisha (later Mathura development — flame of enlightenment atop head).
  • Broad chest, voluptuous physique — Indian ideal of physical vigour.
  • The Mathura school also produced Jain images (Tirthankaras) and Hindu deities — more pluralist.
  • Key sites: Katra mound (Mathura), Govindnagar (Mathura).

Silk Road Trade

Kushanas controlled the central section of the Silk Road connecting China (silk) to Rome (spices, dyes). This gave them enormous revenue and facilitated cultural exchange: Buddhism spread to Central Asia and China via Kushana territory. Key commodities: silk, lapis lazuli (from Afghanistan), horses, cotton textiles.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. The Kushana empire did not extend to Maharashtra — their southern limit was roughly the Malwa plateau. However, Gandhara and Mathura art directly influenced the Buddhist cave art of Maharashtra: the Kanheri caves (Mumbai), Nashik caves, and Ajanta (later phases) show the Mathura-influenced Buddha images and the transition to Gupta-style art. Nagarjuna, one of Kanishka's court intellectuals and founder of Madhyamaka philosophy, has a connection to Nagarjunakonda (Andhra Pradesh) — adjacent to Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • Kushana capital: Purushapura (Peshawar).
  • Greatest ruler: Kanishka I (accession debated: 78 CE or 127 CE).
  • Shaka Era (78 CE): linked to Kanishka in most textbooks; Indian National Calendar uses Shaka Era.
  • Fourth Buddhist Council: Kundalvan/Kashmir; president: Vasumitra; rapporteur: Ashvaghosha.
  • Kanishka's scholars: Ashvaghosha, Nagarjuna, Charaka, Parsva.
  • Gandhara art: blue-grey schist, Greco-Roman Buddha features.
  • Mathura art: red sandstone (Sikri), indigenous Indian features.
  • Coins of Kanishka: first to depict the Buddha on coins.
  • Kushana ended by: Gupta expansion (east) and Sassanid Persians (west), by mid-4th century CE.

Memory Aids

  • "Gandhara = Greek; Mathura = Made-in-India" — to distinguish the two art schools.
  • Kanishka = "K-P-V-A" — Kanishka, Purushapura, Vasumitra (4th council), Ashvaghosha.
  • Shaka Era 78 CE: "78 = Kanishka countdown" — Saka Samvat = Indian National Calendar base.
  • Gandhara material: Schist (grey); Mathura: Sandstone (red).

Exam Traps

  • The Shaka Era (78 CE) is associated with Kanishka, NOT with the Shaka (Scythian) kings — confusing terminology; it's called Shaka Era but likely started by Kanishka.
  • Vasumitra presided over the Fourth Buddhist Council under Kanishka — do NOT write Moggaliputta Tissa (who presided at Ashoka's Third Council in Pataliputra).
  • Gandhara art is in Afghanistan/Pakistan, NOT in India proper — Taxila is in Pakistan.
  • Both Gandhara AND Mathura were patronised by Kushanas — they are not rival chronological schools but parallel regional schools.
