Khilji dynasty — Alauddin's reforms PREMAINS

Overview

The Khilji dynasty (1290–1320 CE) was the second dynasty of the Delhi Sultanate. Its greatest ruler Alauddin Khilji (r. 1296–1316 CE) was the most powerful of all Delhi Sultans — he repelled the Mongols, conquered the Deccan (including Maharashtra's Devagiri in 1296), and implemented radical economic reforms (market control, price regulation) that remain unique in Indian history. MPSC tests Alauddin's reforms exhaustively — they are model answers for Mains "administrative innovations" questions.

Alauddin Khilji — Rise to Power

Alauddin was the nephew and son-in-law of Jalaluddin Firuz. In 1296 CE, he raided Devagiri (Yadava capital, Maharashtra) without the Sultan's permission — the massive loot funded his coup. On returning, he invited Jalaluddin to meet him, then murdered his uncle and proclaimed himself Sultan. He justified it by gifting enormous wealth to the army.

Military Campaigns

Mongol Repulsion: The Mongols invaded India several times during Alauddin's reign — the most serious threat to the Sultanate. Alauddin defeated them in several major battles:

  • Battle of Kili (1299 CE): his general Zafar Khan defeated a large Mongol force.
  • Battle of Amroha (1305 CE): Mongol force of 50,000 crushed.
  • Battle of Ravi (1306 CE): final major Mongol invasion defeated.

Alauddin kept a standing professional army of 475,000 cavalry (direct imperial pay — bypassing the Iqta system) to deal with this threat.

Deccan/South India Campaigns (through general Malik Kafur):

  • 1296 CE: Alauddin himself raided Devagiri (Maharashtra) — extracted tribute from Ramachandra Yadava.
  • 1307–1312 CE: Malik Kafur reconquered Devagiri; then campaigned further south:
  • Warangal (Kakatiya kingdom, Telangana, 1309) — sacked.
  • Dwarasamudra (Hoysala kingdom, Karnataka, 1310) — submitted.
  • Madurai (Pandya kingdom, Tamil Nadu, 1311) — southernmost reach; Malik Kafur reached Rameswaram.
  • This is the first time a north Indian army reached the extreme south of India.

Alauddin's Administrative and Economic Reforms

Alauddin's most distinctive legacy is his market control system — unprecedented in medieval India:

Four Market Reforms:

  1. Shahana-i-Mandi (Grain Market): Fixed prices for wheat, barley, rice, other grains. Enforced by a superintendent (Shahana). Grain dealers had to register and sell only from designated markets.
  1. Sarai Adl (Cloth Market): Fixed prices for cotton, silk, livestock, and cattle. Each commodity had a price schedule.
  1. Market for Horses, Cattle, Livestock: Horses were vital for the army; brought under price control to prevent overpricing.
  1. Market for General Goods (slaves, livestock, other): Fourth market overseeing miscellaneous commodities.

Enforcement machinery:

  • Diwan-i-Riyasat: controller of markets (overall).
  • Shahana-i-Mandi: superintendent of each market.
  • Barids and Munhiyans: secret agents reporting violations; severe punishment for black-marketeering (cutting off nose/ears in some records).
  • Result: Alauddin maintained the largest army in Indian history at relatively low cost — soldiers paid in cash at fixed low prices could buy necessities cheaply.

Land Revenue Reforms:

  • Introduced direct land revenue measurement — abolished intermediaries (khuts, muqaddams, chaudhris); peasants paid the state directly.
  • Biswa system: standardised measurement of land.
  • Revenue assessed at 50% of produce (highest in medieval India).

Prohibition and Social Control:

  • Banned wine, cannabis, gambling.
  • Restricted nobles' social gatherings and marriages without royal permission (to prevent anti-Sultan conspiracies).

Currency: Maintained the silver tanka as the standard coin.

Mongol Threat and the Army

Alauddin's military reforms were driven by the Mongol threat:

  • Replaced the Iqta-based army (feudal, unreliable) with a direct-pay centrally-controlled cavalry.
  • Introduced branding of horses (dagh) and a muster roll (chehra) to prevent fraud and substitution.
  • This system prefigured Akbar's Mansabdari dagh system.

Maharashtra Connection

High relevance for Maharashtra. Alauddin's 1296 CE raid on Devagiri (Maharashtra) is the entry point of Delhi Sultanate power in the Deccan. The Yadava capital Devagiri was in Aurangabad district. The Khilji raids effectively ended Yadava independence and brought Marathwada under Sultanate influence. This is the direct predecessor to the Bahmani Sultanate (which controlled Maharashtra from 1347 CE).

Exam Traps

  • Alauddin's market control system was specifically designed to reduce army costs to fund the large Mongol-deterrent army — it was NOT purely an ideological welfare measure.
  • Malik Kafur conducted the Deccan/south campaigns; Alauddin personally went to Devagiri only in 1296 CE.
  • The dagh (branding) and chehra (muster roll) system is associated with Alauddin Khilji — NOT with Akbar (Akbar's Mansabdari dagh is a later separate reform).
  • Alauddin raised land revenue to 50% — much higher than Gupta (1/6 to 1/4) or earlier Sultanate norms.
