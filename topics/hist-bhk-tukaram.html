Tukaram — Abhangas PREMAINSMH

Overview

Sant Tukaram (1598–1650 CE) is the most beloved saint-poet of Maharashtra and the fourth pillar of the Warkari Sampradaya. A kunbi (farmer/peasant) from Dehu near Pune, Tukaram's Abhangas (devotional hymns) number over 4,500 and represent the pinnacle of Marathi devotional poetry. His compositions are immediately accessible — written in the language of ordinary Maharashtrian village life — and his central message is that Vitthal (God) can be reached through pure, unconditional love regardless of caste, wealth, or scholarship. The Pandharpur Wari's most famous palanquin is Tukaram's. MPSC treats Tukaram as one of the most important Maharashtra-specific topics.

In-Depth

Life

  • Born: ~1598 CE, Dehu village, Pune district (on the Indrayani river, near Alandi).
  • Caste: Kunbi (Vani/Moré family) — farmer/peasant/trader class; lower-middle caste in Maratha social hierarchy.
  • Personal life: Married twice (first wife Rakhubai died in famine; second wife Avali/Jijai); experienced personal tragedies — first wife's death, son's death, financial ruin during famine.
  • Guru: No formal human guru; Vitthal (God) himself appeared to Tukaram in a dream and initiated him; his lineage guru was Babaji Chaitanya (disciple of Keshavachaitanya, disciple of Namdev).
  • Writing medium: Abhangas were written on palm leaves (pothi).
  • Crisis: Brahmin community of Dehu, led by Rameshwarbhat, objected to a low-caste man writing scriptures in Marathi. Forced Tukaram to immerse the palm leaves in the Indrayani river. Legend: After 13 days, Vitthal returned the pothi (palm leaf books) from the river intact — proof of divine sanction.
  • Shivaji's offer: Shivaji reportedly sent gifts to Tukaram; Tukaram declined, saying a saint cannot accept royal wealth.
  • Death: ~1650 CE; Vaikuntha Gamana ("ascent to Vaikuntha/heaven") — traditional account: Tukaram ascended bodily to Vaikuntha (did not die physically); from Dehu.

Abhangas — Key Features

  • ~4,500 Abhangas (some counts give 4,600+).
  • Written in simple, direct Marathi — village idiom, proverbs, images from farming life.
  • Central deity: Vitthal of Pandharpur (Vithoba).
  • Themes: Love of God, social equality, critique of hypocrisy (Brahmin ritualism), futility of wealth, value of devotion over learning.
  • Collections: Tukaram Gatha (compiled later; ~4,500+ abhangas).
  • Abhangas in Adi Granth: Some of Tukaram's compositions were known to Sikh traditions.

Gatha Immersion Miracle

  • Rameshwarbhat (Brahmin priest of Dehu) led the opposition: argued that only Brahmins can write scripture; a Kunbi (Tukaram) writing Abhangas was against dharma.
  • Tukaram was forced to immerse his palm-leaf manuscripts in the Indrayani river.
  • After 13 days, Vitthal appeared in Tukaram's dream (and to Rameshwarbhat) saying the Abhangas were divinely protected. The pothi emerged from the river intact.
  • This episode is one of the most famous hagiographic events of Maharashtra's bhakti tradition.

Social Philosophy

  • Most radical Warkari in social terms: Tukaram explicitly challenged Brahmin claims to social and spiritual superiority.
  • Famous Abhanga: "Brahmana kuli janmalo, bhubahar mhi khandito" — I was born in a Brahmin household [referring to past life metaphor], but I break through superficiality.
  • Chokhamela: Tukaram acknowledged and honoured Chokhamela (Dalit saint, 14th c.) in his abhangas — cross-generational solidarity across the most marginalised community.
  • Critique of ritual: Tukaram's abhangas repeatedly dismiss fasting, pilgrimage, caste rituals, and Vedic yajna as spiritually irrelevant without devotion.

Chivnyas (Wit and Wordplay)

  • Tukaram's Abhangas are famous for chivnyas — pithy, sometimes humorous sayings that strike the reader like a jab.
  • Used paradox, irony, and village wisdom to make profound spiritual points.
  • Unlike Dnyaneshwar's elevated philosophical prose, Tukaram spoke as a villager to villagers.

Legacy

  • Tukaram Palkhi (Dehu to Pandharpur): the second largest Wari procession; followed by millions.
  • Tukaram Beej (2nd day of bright fortnight of Phalgun): Vaikuntha Gamana day; major festival in Maharashtra.
  • Marathi proverbs: many Marathi proverbs and folk sayings are from Tukaram's Abhangas; deeply embedded in popular culture.
  • Phule, Ambedkar: both cited Tukaram as a predecessor of social reform thinking in Maharashtra.
  • Sant Tukaram (1936 film): Marathi film by V. Damle and S. Fattelal; won the Venice Film Festival Best Film jury award — rare international honour for a Marathi work.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Born and died at Dehu, Pune district — pilgrimage centre; on Indrayani river.
  • Tukaram Palkhi from Dehu to Pandharpur: one of Maharashtra's most celebrated annual events; millions walk.
  • Tukaram's Kunbi background connects him to the farming backbone of Maharashtra society.
  • His abhangas are recited at every Warkari gathering; the Ashadhi Wari cannot be understood without Tukaram.
  • Tukaram Beej = state-level festival observation.
  • Indrayani river, Dehu: where the Gatha immersion miracle occurred; the river holds sacred significance in Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • Born: ~1598 CE, Dehu, Pune district.
  • Caste: Kunbi (Moré family).
  • ~4,500 Abhangas: simple Marathi; Vitthal devotion.
  • Gatha immersion: Forced into Indrayani; returned after 13 days.
  • Guru: Vitthal himself (in dream) + spiritual lineage through Namdev.
  • Declined Shivaji's gifts: maintained poverty/renunciation.
  • Death/Vaikuntha Gamana: ~1650 CE, Dehu.
  • Tukaram Palkhi: Dehu → Pandharpur; Ashadhi Wari.

Memory Aids

  • Tukaram = "Kunbi + Dehu + 4500 Abhangas + Gatha in river 13 days + Vaikuntha Gamana".
  • Contrast: "Dnyaneshwar=philosopher (Gita commentary); Tukaram=poet (village voice, Abhangas)".
  • Palkhi: "Alandi = Dnyaneshwar; Dehu = Tukaram" (both on Indrayani river, near Pune).

Exam Traps

  • Tukaram ≠ Kunbi caste means untouchable — Kunbi is a farming/peasant caste (OBC), not Dalit; Chokhamela was Mahar (untouchable/Dalit).
  • Vaikuntha Gamana ≠ death in traditional account — bodily ascent to heaven; this is what makes Tukaram unique among Warkari saints (others have samadhis).
  • Gatha immersion episode: the opposition was from Rameshwarbhat (Brahmin), not from Muslim rulers or foreign pressure.
  • Tukaram's guru was NOT Eknath — though Eknath preceded him; his spiritual lineage is through Namdev's disciple tradition (Babaji Chaitanya).
