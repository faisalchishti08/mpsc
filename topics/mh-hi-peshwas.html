Peshwa period and Maratha confederacy PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

The Peshwas (1713–1818) were the hereditary Prime Ministers of the Maratha Empire, all from the Chitpavan Brahmin Bhat family of Sas-wad (Pune). Under the Peshwas, Pune became the political capital of the Maratha Confederacy, which at its peak controlled most of India. The Maratha Confederacy consisted of five chiefs: Sindhia (Gwalior), Holkar (Indore), Bhosale (Nagpur), Gaekwad (Baroda), and the Peshwa (Pune). MPSC tests Peshwa names, their achievements, and major events including the Third Battle of Panipat (1761).

Bajirao I — The Supreme Peshwa

  • Bajirao Peshwa I (1720–1740): Called "the greatest cavalry commander of medieval India" by military historians.
  • Fought over 40 battles; never defeated.
  • Expanded Maratha power to Delhi: Battle of Delhi (1737) — Bajirao raided Delhi; Mughal Emperor Muhammad Shah sued for peace; Marathas established suzerainty over Malwa and Gujarat.
  • Battle of Bhopal (1737): Defeated Nizam ul-Mulk (Asaf Jah I) who allied with Mughals to stop Bajirao.
  • Mastani affair: Bajirao fell in love with Mastani (daughter of Bundela Rajput chieftain Chhatrasal and a Muslim woman Ruhaini) — a cross-religious relationship; caused controversy; Mastani died when Bajirao died in 1740 (age 39, fever at Raverkhedi, MP).
  • Shaniwar Wada (Pune, 1732): Bajirao's palace-fortress in Pune — HQ of Peshwai; now a UNESCO tentative list candidate; partially destroyed by fire (1828); popular tourist site.

Third Battle of Panipat (14 January 1761)

  • Between: Maratha Confederacy (under Vishwas Rao — son of Nanasaheb, nominal commander; actual commander Vishwasrao + Sadashivrao Bhau) vs. Ahmad Shah Abdali (Durrani Afghan + Rohillas + Awadh Nawab Shuja ud-Daula).
  • Result: Catastrophic Maratha defeat; Vishwasrao killed; Sadashivrao Bhau killed; ~50,000–75,000 Maratha soldiers killed (disputed; some sources say more). Called "Marathyancha mahavinas" (the great destruction of the Marathas).
  • Aftermath: Balaji Bajirao (Nanasaheb Peshwa) died of grief shortly after hearing the news (June 1761); Maratha power in the north permanently weakened; British later exploited this.
  • Significance: Ended Maratha ambitions to establish pan-Indian empire; created a power vacuum eventually filled by the British.

Nana Phadnavis — "The Maratha Machiavelli"

  • Nana Phadnavis (real name: Balaji Janardan Bhanu) — de facto ruler during Sawai Madhavrao's minority (1774–1795) and beyond.
  • A brilliant diplomat; managed the Maratha Confederacy amidst internal conflicts and British pressure.
  • Formed the First Anglo-Maratha War (1775–82) coalition — thwarted British advance; Treaty of Salbai (1782).
  • Called "the Machiavelli of the Marathas" by British administrators.

End of Peshwai

  • Treaty of Bassein (December 31, 1802): Baji Rao II signed a subsidiary alliance with British — ceded sovereignty; a watershed moment.
  • Third Anglo-Maratha War (1817–18): British defeated remaining Maratha chiefs; Battle of Koregaon-Bhima (1 January 1818) — British-Mahar soldiers (East India Company) defeated a much larger Peshwa force — a historic moment for Dalits in Maharashtra; Bhima Koregaon victory pillar (Vijay Stambh) becomes an annual gathering site for Dalits.
  • Peshwai abolished 1818; last Peshwa Baji Rao II exiled to Bithur (Kanpur).

Maharashtra Connection

Shaniwar Wada (Pune) is Maharashtra's most iconic Peshwa monument. The Bhima Koregaon Stambha (1818 battle commemorating Mahar soldiers' victory over Peshwa) is both a historical monument and a site of ongoing social-political significance — annual gatherings and occasional violence (notably January 2018) link this history to current Maharashtra politics.

Exam Traps
