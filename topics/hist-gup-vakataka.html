Vakatakas and Ajanta (Maharashtra) PREMAINSMH

Overview

The Vakataka dynasty (c. 250–500 CE) was the dominant power of the Deccan and Maharashtra during the Gupta period, ruling from Nandivardhana (Nagardhan, near Nagpur) and later from Vatsagulma (Washim, Maharashtra). Their political alliance with the Guptas — through the marriage of Prabhavati Gupta (Chandragupta II's daughter) to Vakataka king Rudrasena II — directly enabled the creation of Ajanta's Buddhist cave paintings (Phase II), making this a critical Maharashtra GK and art history topic for MPSC.

In-Depth

Vakataka Origins and Dynasty

The Vakatakas were a Brahmin dynasty claiming Vishnu Vrishni lineage. The dynasty split into two main branches:

  • Nandivardhana branch (main line): Pravarasena I → Rudrasena I → Prithvisena I → Rudrasena II → Prabhavati Gupta's regency → Pravarasena II → Narendrasena → Prithvisena II.
  • Vatsagulma branch (secondary): Sarvasena → Vindhyasena → Pravarasena II (Vatsagulma).

Pravarasena I (c. 270–330 CE) was the greatest early Vakataka — he performed four Ashvamedha yajnas and used the title Samrat. He controlled the entire Deccan from the Vindhyas to the Tungabhadra.

Gupta-Vakataka Alliance and Prabhavati Gupta

Prabhavati Gupta (daughter of Chandragupta II) was married to Rudrasena II of the Vakataka Nandivardhana branch. Rudrasena II died young; Prabhavati ruled as regent for her sons for nearly 20 years (c. 385–405 CE). During her regency:

  • She introduced Gupta administrative practices (land grants, Sanskrit inscriptions) into the Deccan.
  • She bore the title Paramabhagavata (devoted to Vishnu) — reflecting Gupta religious orientation.
  • Extensive copper plate grants survive from her regency — the Riddhapur plates and Poona plates are key epigraphic sources.
  • Her regency effectively made the Vakataka kingdom a Gupta protectorate, allowing Gupta cultural influence to penetrate the Deccan.

Ajanta — The Vakataka Achievement

Ajanta Caves (Aurangabad district, Maharashtra) — 30 rock-cut Buddhist caves carved into a horseshoe-shaped cliff above the Waghora river. Two phases of activity:

Phase I (Hinayana/Satavahana period, c. 1st century BCE – 2nd century CE): Caves 9, 10, 12, 13, 15A — plain chaitya halls and viharas; simple early Buddhist art (Buddha in symbolic form).

Phase II (Mahayana/Vakataka period, c. 460–480 CE): The bulk of the paintings — Caves 1, 2, 16, 17, 19, 26 — were carved/painted in an intense 20-year burst of activity under Vakataka king Harishena (c. 460–477 CE) and his nobles/vassals who competed as cave donors:

  • Cave 1: finest paintings — the famous Padmapani Bodhisattva (lotus-bearing Avalokiteshvara) and Vajrapani Bodhisattva; Mahajanaka Jataka narrative.
  • Cave 2: ceiling paintings; Hariti and Panchika (mother-goddess/protector deity scenes).
  • Cave 16: Dying Princess painting — Nanda's wife fainting on hearing of Nanda's renunciation; donor was Varahadeva (Vakataka prime minister).
  • Cave 17: most paintings; Simhala Avadana, Vessantara Jataka; donor was a vassal (amatya) of Harishena.
  • Cave 19: Mahayana chaitya with standing Buddha in niche above entrance.
  • Cave 26: large chaitya with 15-m reclining Parinirvana Buddha.

After Harishena's death (c. 477 CE), the Vakatakas collapsed — the cave project was abandoned suddenly (unfinished paintings visible in several caves). The caves were rediscovered by British officer John Smith of the Madras Army in 1819 while tiger-hunting.

Painting technique at Ajanta:

  • Not true fresco (buon fresco); rather tempera-like: lime plaster on rock surface, then clay/cow-dung/vegetable fibre layer, then fine plaster, then paint applied to dry surface.
  • Pigments: natural mineral — red ochre, yellow ochre, green malachite, blue lapis lazuli (expensive, imported from Afghanistan), lamp-black, white kaolin.
  • Subjects: Jataka tales (past lives of Buddha), contemporary court scenes, nature, animals (horses, elephants, geese).

Maharashtra Connection

Core Maharashtra topic. Everything here is Maharashtra:

  • Ajanta Caves are in Aurangabad district, Maharashtra — UNESCO World Heritage Site (1983).
  • Vakataka capital: Nandivardhana (Nagardhan, Nagpur district) — in Maharashtra.
  • Vatsagulma branch capital: Washim, Maharashtra.
  • Prabhavati Gupta's copper plates found at Riddhapur (Amravati district) and Poona — Maharashtra.
  • Ajanta rediscovered 1819 by British officer John Smith.
  • Famous painting: Padmapani Bodhisattva (Cave 1) — among the most reproduced images from ancient India.

Exam Traps

  • Ajanta paintings are NOT true frescoes — they are tempera/dry plaster technique.
  • The famous Ajanta paintings (Phase II) were done under the Vakatakas, NOT the Guptas directly — though the inspiration and artistic tradition are Gupta-style.
  • Ajanta UNESCO WHS: year is 1983 — Ellora is also 1983, same year; remember both.
  • Cave 9 and 10 (Phase I) are Hinayana (Satavahana era) — the famous paintings are Phase II (Vakataka/Mahayana).
