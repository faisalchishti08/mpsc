Buddhist stupas and viharas PREMAINS

Overview

Buddhist stupas and viharas are the earliest surviving monumental architectural forms of India, dating from the 3rd century BCE (Ashokan era). The stupa — a dome-shaped mound housing relics of the Buddha or Buddhist saints — became the defining sacred monument of Buddhism across Asia. MPSC asks about Sanchi, Amravati, and the structural components of stupas in both Prelims and Mains.

In-Depth

Origin and Meaning

Stupa (Pali: thupa) literally means "mound" or "heap." Tradition holds that after the Buddha's death (Mahaparinirvana, 483 BCE), his remains were divided among 8 kings who built stupas. Later, Ashoka (r. 268–232 BCE) is said to have opened 7 of these 8 original stupas, divided the relics further, and built 84,000 stupas across the Maurya Empire.

Sanchi Stupa (the Greatest)

Location: Sanchi, Madhya Pradesh (near Bhopal); on a hill.

  • Great Stupa (Stupa 1): Originally built by Ashoka (3rd c. BCE); enlarged and embellished during the Shunga (2nd c. BCE) and Satavahana (1st c. BCE–1st c. CE) periods.
  • Structural additions: Four elaborately carved Torana gateways — each 10 m high; covered with scenes from the Buddha's life (Jataka tales), episodes from Ashoka's life; among the finest examples of Indian narrative sculpture.
  • The Buddha is represented symbolically (not in human form) in early Buddhist art — footprints (paduka), empty throne (simhasana), Wheel of Law (dharmachakra), Bodhi tree, etc. (Aniconic tradition).
  • UNESCO WHS: Sanchi (1989).

Stupa 2 and 3 at Sanchi also survive — Stupa 3 contains relics of the Buddha's disciples.

Amravati Stupa (Andhra Pradesh)

  • Located at Amravati (Amaravati), Krishna district, Andhra Pradesh.
  • Dating from 3rd c. BCE to 3rd c. CE (Satavahana and later Ikshvaku periods).
  • Famous for the Amravati school of sculpture — narrative panels in white limestone; more dynamic, sinuous style compared to Sanchi's more restrained carving; considered the finest early Buddhist sculpture.
  • Much of the sculpture was removed to museums; significant pieces at the Government Museum Chennai and British Museum London.
  • The stupa was largely dismantled; the site now has a museum.

Sarnath Stupa (Uttar Pradesh)

  • Dhamek Stupa at Sarnath: where the Buddha delivered his first sermon (Dharmachakra Pravartana). The existing Dhamek stupa is cylindrical (Gupta and later construction, 6th–11th c. CE) on the original Ashokan base.
  • Ashokan capital from Sarnath is the Lion Capital — now India's national emblem.

Bharhut Stupa (Madhya Pradesh)

  • 2nd c. BCE (Shunga period); major railing with richly carved medallions (yaksha-yakshini figures, Jataka scenes).
  • Largely dismantled; carvings at Indian Museum, Kolkata.

Vihara (Monastery)

  • A vihara was a Buddhist monastic complex: individual cells for monks around a central courtyard with a shrine at one end.
  • Later developed into universities — Nalanda, Vikramashila, Takshashila (older) are the grandest examples.
  • The word "Bihar" (state) derives from Vihara — the state had so many Buddhist monasteries.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Karla, Bhaja, Nasik, Pitalkhora in Maharashtra have chaitya (prayer hall) caves with miniature stupa replicas carved inside; these are the rock-cut equivalent of the structural stupa.
  • Amravati district in Andhra Pradesh shares the name with Amravati/Amraoti city in Vidarbha (Maharashtra) — a common exam confusion but different places.
  • The Sanchi-style railings (vedika) found at Paithan (Maharashtra) — capital of the Satavahana kings who patronised stupa construction.

Key Facts

  • Buddha's remains divided after death: 8 original stupas.
  • Ashoka built 84,000 stupas (tradition).
  • Sanchi Great Stupa: originally Ashokan (3rd c. BCE); Torana gateways added by Shunga-Satavahana (2nd–1st c. BCE).
  • Toranas (gateways): 4 at Sanchi (cardinal directions).
  • Aniconic representation of Buddha in early Buddhist art (symbols, not human form).
  • Amravati stupa: Andhra Pradesh, 3rd c. BCE–3rd c. CE; finest limestone sculpture.
  • Dhamek Stupa, Sarnath: site of first sermon (Dharmachakra Pravartana).
  • Bharhut: Shunga period (2nd c. BCE); railing carvings at Indian Museum, Kolkata.
  • Bihar state name derived from "Vihara."
  • UNESCO WHS: Sanchi (1989).

Memory Aids

  • Stupa components: "Anda (dome) → Harmika (box) → Yashti (pole) → Chattra (umbrella)".
  • Sanchi Toranas: "4 toranas = North, South, East, West".
  • Early Buddhist art = Aniconic (no human Buddha image) → Later = Iconic (Gandhara, Mathura schools introduce human Buddha).
  • Bihar from Vihara = monastery; Bihar = land of monasteries.

Exam Traps

  • Buddha not depicted in human form in early Buddhist art (before ~1st c. CE) — only at Gandhara school (Greco-Buddhist) was the human form first used.
  • Sanchi is in Madhya Pradesh, not Maharashtra; Amravati (stupa) is in Andhra Pradesh — both often confused with Maharashtra connections.
  • Harmika is not the dome — it is the flat box on top of the dome (anda); confusing harmika with anda is common.
  • Sanchi Toranas were primarily added during Shunga and Satavahana periods — not all Ashokan.
