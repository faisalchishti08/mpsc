Kathakali (Kerala) PREMAINS

Overview

Kathakali is Kerala's elaborate classical dance-drama form — a total theatre of storytelling through mime, elaborate makeup (chutti), spectacular costume, and rigorous physical training. It enacts stories from the Ramayana, Mahabharata and Puranas entirely through gesture and expression — no spoken dialogue. MPSC tests its origin, makeup system, and contrast with other Kerala forms (Mohiniyattam, Kudiyattam).

In-Depth

Origin and History

  • Evolved in 17th century Kerala from earlier forms including Krishnanattam (Zamorin of Calicut's dance-drama) and Ramanattam (Kottayam raja's form); also draws from Kalarippayattu (Kerala martial art) for body training.
  • Kottarakkara Thampuran is credited with initiating the form (~1657).
  • Attakalasam (composed by various poets) are the musical compositions that accompany Kathakali; the Manipravalam language (blend of Sanskrit and Malayalam) used in texts.

Makeup Character Types (Vesham)

The makeup defines the moral character of the figure:

  • Pachcha (green): noble heroes (Rama, Krishna, Arjuna) — green face with white mask features.
  • Kathi (knife/red): villainous characters with a curved white line and red spots — Ravana, Duryodhana.
  • Kari (black): female demons (Surpanakha), hunters.
  • Thaadi (beard): white beard = divine; red beard = fierce demons; black beard = forest dwellers.
  • Minukku: refined characters — women, sages; minimal makeup (yellow/gold face).

Training

Kathakali demands extraordinary physical training — the kalari (training ground) tradition:

  • Training begins as young as 5–8 years.
  • Body flexibility achieved through oil massages (uzhichil) and foot stamping exercises.
  • Eye exercises: chutti training for extreme eye movement control.
  • Kerala Kalamandalam (founded 1930 by Mahakavi Vallathol Narayana Menon): the premier Kathakali training institution; given Deemed University status; in Thrissur.

Maharashtra Connection

No direct origin. Maharashtra has Kathakali schools in Mumbai and Pune due to cultural exchange. The all-night performance tradition of Kathakali parallels Maharashtra's Tamasha in community gathering aspect. Both are elaborate theatrical traditions that outlasted colonial displacement.

Key Facts

  • State: Kerala.
  • Origin: 17th century; from Krishnanattam + Ramanattam + Kalarippayattu.
  • No spoken dialogue — entire story through mudras and navarasas.
  • Green face (Pachcha) = noble hero; Red spots/knife (Kathi) = villain.
  • Music: Chenda and Maddalam drums + singers.
  • Training institute: Kerala Kalamandalam (1930), Thrissur; founded by Vallathol Narayana Menon.
  • Traditionally all-male performers; all-night performance.

Memory Aids

  • Kathakali = Kerala + elaborate makeup (chutti) + mime/no dialogue + all-night.
  • Green face = Pachcha = noble (green = go = good).
  • Chenda = Kathakali's drum; Mridangam = Bharatanatyam/Carnatic.
  • Kerala Kalamandalam = 1930, Thrissur, Vallathol.

Exam Traps

  • Kathakali ≠ Mohiniyattam — both Kerala; Kathakali is theatrical/elaborate/no speech; Mohiniyattam is solo female/gentle.
  • Kalarippayattu is Kerala's martial art, not a dance; but it is an input into Kathakali training.
  • Pachcha (green) = noble — students sometimes think green = demon because it's an unusual colour.
  • Kudiyattam (Kerala) ≠ Kathakali — Kudiyattam is ancient Sanskrit drama (UNESCO ICH 2008); Kathakali is 17th c. and not UNESCO ICH.
