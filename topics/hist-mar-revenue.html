Maratha revenue system — Chauth, Sardeshmukhi PREMAINSMH

Overview

The Maratha revenue system combined Shivaji's direct-collection model with levies on external territories (Chauth and Sardeshmukhi). It was a pragmatic adaptation of Malik Ambar's Ahmednagar model and later the Mughal Zabt, modified for a mobile, fort-based state. The system funded the Maratha military machine and underpinned the confederacy's expansion. MPSC MH tag tests Chauth/Sardeshmukhi fractions, land revenue classification, and the fiscal basis of Maratha power.

In-Depth

Shivaji's Revenue Reforms

Shivaji rejected the Watan (hereditary rights) system of local Deshmukhs and Patils who had extracted wealth for themselves. He:

  • Abolished Watans (in principle): attempted to pay village officials a fixed salary instead of hereditary revenue shares; this was partially reversed after his death.
  • Ryotwari tendency: dealt directly with the cultivating peasant (ryot) rather than through zamindars — a proto-ryotwari approach later systematised by the British in the Bombay Deccan.
  • Kathi measurement: used the Kathi (rod) system of land measurement adapted from Malik Ambar (Ahmednagar Sultanate's innovative chancellor); classified land by quality.

Land Revenue Rates:

  • Fixed at roughly 33–40% of produce (different from Mughal 1/3 = 33%).
  • Assessed in both cash and kind depending on the region.
  • Prant Pati (Subhedar) at the top; below them Havaldars (taluka-level); at the bottom — Patil (village head) and Kulkarni (village accountant).

Chauth

Chauth (literally "one-fourth") = 1/4 (25%) of the assessed land revenue of a territory not under direct Maratha rule, collected from the ruler of that territory in exchange for Maratha "protection" (i.e., from Maratha raids).

  • Begun under Shivaji when he extracted Chauth from the Mughal territories and Bijapur's domains that he was not directly administering.
  • Under Peshwa rule became the primary source of revenue from north India.
  • Not just a tax — it was also a form of political assertion of suzerainty; the ruler paying Chauth acknowledged Maratha power without necessarily being conquered.
  • Distribution of Chauth (under Shivaji):
  • 3/4 of Chauth → King (Shivaji/Chhatrapati).
  • 1/4 of Chauth → Army chief (Senapati) for military expenses.
  • Later, under Peshwas, a portion was assigned to the Chhatrapati as nadgauda.

Sardeshmukhi

Sardeshmukhi (literally "head village headman's share") = 1/10 (10%) of the revenue of a territory — justified on the basis that Shivaji was the Sardeshmukh (chief village headman) of the entire Maharashtra region by hereditary right.

  • A separate 10% levy in addition to Chauth, collected from the same territories.
  • The rationale was Shivaji's claim to be the supreme revenue chief of all Maharashtra — a semi-feudal, historical assertion.
  • Under Peshwas, Sardeshmukhi was collected from the Mughal Deccan provinces as a condition of the 1719 treaty secured by Balaji Vishwanath.

Peshwa Revenue Expansion (18th Century)

Under the Peshwas, the fiscal system expanded geographically:

  • Chauth from Bengal, Bihar, Orissa (Raghuji Bhosale collected from 1740s).
  • Chauth from Rajputana, Malwa (Scindia and Holkar).
  • Peshwa's central treasury (Huzur Daftar) at Pune maintained sophisticated accounts — the Pune Daftar (records) are a major historical archive.
  • Kamavisdar — a Peshwa revenue official who collected taxes in a pargana; distinct from the hereditary Patil/Deshmukh.

Maharashtra Connection

Directly central — the Maratha revenue system is Maharashtra's own administrative history:

  • Mamlatdar (taluka-level revenue officer) remains the term used in Maharashtra's current revenue administration — a direct 300-year continuity.
  • Patil and Kulkarni remain recognised revenue roles in Maharashtra villages.
  • Chauth and Sardeshmukhi were specifically claimed over Maharashtra (Deccan subas of the Mughal empire) — secured in the Treaty of 1719.
  • British reorganisation of Deccan revenue (Ryotwari settlements, Thomas Munro) partly built on the Maratha-era ryot-direct approach.

Key Facts

  • Chauth = 1/4 (25%) of territory's assessed revenue — protection levy.
  • Sardeshmukhi = 1/10 (10%) — Shivaji's hereditary claim.
  • Combined = 35% of external territory revenue.
  • Land measurement: Kathi system (adapted from Malik Ambar).
  • Shivaji's ryot-direct approach: abolished Watans (partially) and Deshmukh intermediaries.
  • Mamlatdar: Maratha taluka revenue officer — still used in Maharashtra.
  • Peshwa's 1719 Treaty (via Balaji Vishwanath) secured Chauth and Sardeshmukhi rights from Mughal Deccan.

Exam Traps

  • Chauth (1/4) ≠ Sardeshmukhi (1/10) — constantly swapped in options; fix: Chauth is the bigger one (25%), Sardeshmukhi is smaller (10%).
  • Sardeshmukhi was specifically Shivaji's hereditary claim as Sardeshmukh — not a general levy like Chauth.
  • These levies were from territories NOT under direct Maratha administration — within directly-held territories, normal land revenue was collected separately.
  • Watan abolition by Shivaji was partial and incomplete — Deshmukhs and Patils survived with modified roles.
