Maratha society, culture and administration PREMAINSMH

Overview

Maratha society, culture and administration under Shivaji and the Peshwas blended Brahminical values, Kshatriya martial culture, and Bhakti movement egalitarianism. It was a complex social order: caste hierarchy coexisted with mobility through military service; women could exercise significant power (Tarabai, Ahilyabai); Maharashtra's rich literary and saint tradition shaped social values. MPSC asks about caste structure, Warkari tradition, Tamasha/Lavani, and administrative terminology.

In-Depth

Social Structure under the Marathas

Maratha society was organised along traditional varna-jati lines but with significant practical modifications:

Shivaji's inclusive policy: Shivaji employed Muslims in his army, navy and administration — Ibrahim Khan commanded his artillery; Siddi Hilal served as a cavalryman; Shivaji is praised for respecting mosques and the Quran in captured territories. Yet the court was defined by Hindu symbolism and Marathi language.

Bhakti-Warkari Tradition

The Warkari (pilgrim) movement centred on Vithoba/Vitthal temple at Pandharpur was the spiritual heart of medieval Maharashtra:

  • Founded by Sant Dnyaneshwar (1271–1296): wrote Dnyaneshwari (1290) — Marathi commentary on the Bhagavad Gita; gave Marathi its first great literary text.
  • Sant Namdev (1270–1350): poet-saint; composed abhangas; bridged Maharashtra and Punjab (spent years in Punjab).
  • Sant Eknath (1533–1599): social reformer; ate with untouchables; wrote Eknathi Bhagavat; simplified religious access.
  • Sant Tukaram (1608–1650): contemporary of Shivaji; greatest Warkari poet; composed thousands of abhangas (devotional verses); challenged caste hierarchy through devotion to Vithoba.
  • Shivaji himself revered Sant Ramdas (Samartha Ramdas, 1608–1681) as his spiritual guide — "Ramdas = Shivaji's Guru" is a widely cited tradition (though the nature of this relationship is debated by historians).

Vari (Pilgrimage): Twice-yearly pilgrimage to Pandharpur — Ashadhi Ekadashi and Kartiki Ekadashi — continues as one of Maharashtra's most important religious traditions; lakhs participate.

Literature and Language

  • Marathi was adopted as the administrative language by Shivaji alongside Sanskrit — the Rajya Vyavahar Kosh (dictionary of administrative terms) was compiled under Shivaji to replace Persian/Arabic terms.
  • Bakhars (chronicles): historical narratives about Maratha kings — key source of Maratha history (e.g., Sabhasad Bakhar about Shivaji).
  • Powadas (ballads): ballad tradition celebrating Maratha warriors — Shahir (folk poet) tradition; Agindas and other early Shahirs composed powadas about Shivaji.
  • Shahir culture: continues in Maharashtra (Annabhau Sathe, Amar Sheikh in the 20th century).

Women in Maratha Society

Despite a patriarchal social structure, several women exercised extraordinary power:

  • Jijabai (Shivaji's mother): chief inspiration of Shivaji's early life; shaped his values and early military training; revered as the mother of Maharashtra.
  • Tarabai: regent and military commander against Aurangzeb (1700–07).
  • Ahilyabai Holkar (1767–1795): ruler of Indore; revered administrator and philanthropist.
  • Chand Bibi: regent of Ahmednagar; defended against Mughals (1595).

However, sati (widow immolation), child marriage, and other patriarchal practices also existed; the reform movements of the 19th century (Phule, Agarkar) were reactions to these Peshwa-era social rigidities.

Peshwa-Era Social Character

The Peshwas (Brahmin ruling class) combined administrative brilliance with increased caste orthodoxy:

  • Strengthened Brahmin cultural authority; Pune became a centre of Sanskrit learning.
  • Dalits (especially Mahars) were socially marginalised and forced to perform degrading customs (in some accounts, required to carry pots around their necks so their spittle didn't pollute the ground in Pune's market).
  • This caste oppression under Peshwa rule is part of why Battle of Koregaon (1818) is remembered as a liberation moment by Dalits — British rule, despite its colonialism, at least removed some Peshwa caste discrimination.
  • Dr. B.R. Ambedkar (from Mahar caste) specifically invoked Peshwa-era oppression in his writings.

Maharashtra Connection

Entirely Maharashtra-rooted:

  • Pandharpur Vari (pilgrimage) — one of Maharashtra's most important living traditions.
  • Saints Dnyaneshwar, Namdev, Eknath, Tukaram, Ramdas — all from Maharashtra; their abhangas are central to Marathi cultural identity.
  • Shahir tradition (powadas) — alive in Maharashtra's folk culture.
  • Koregaon social significance — a site of living contestation in Maharashtra today.
  • Rajya Vyavahar Kosh — promoted Marathi administrative language (basis of modern Maharashtra's Marathi identity in administration).

Exam Traps

  • Shivaji's guru = Samartha Ramdas (the spiritual guide/mentor); Dadoji Konddev was his early childhood guardian/tutor (different person, different role).
  • Dnyaneshwari was written in 1290, not 1296 — 1296 is Dnyaneshwar's samadhi (death) year.
  • Tukaram was NOT Shivaji's court poet — he was an independent saint who refused royal patronage; this distinction matters.
  • Pandharpur is in Solapur district, Maharashtra (not Pune or Satara district).
