Folk music traditions of India PREMAINS

Overview

Folk music traditions of India represent the living musical heritage of communities — rooted in agrarian cycles, festivals, devotion, and social occasions. Unlike classical music, folk music is oral, community-owned, and regionally specific. MPSC (being a Maharashtra exam) heavily tests Maharashtra's folk music — Lavani, Powada, Gondhal, Kirtan, Tamasha (musical component), Lezim — alongside major national folk traditions (Baul, Bhangra, Bihu, Thumri roots). Maharashtra folk music is almost certainly a Mains topic.

Shahir Tradition:

  • Shahir = Urdu-Persian for "poet"; in Marathi tradition = ballad-singer + social commentator.
  • Honaji Bala: 18th c.; court of Peshwas; known for Lavanyo.
  • Annabhau Sathe (1920–1969): Dalit Shahir; Communist; known as "Tukaram of Maharashtra"; Majhi Maina Gavavar Rahili (his famous Lavani); Padma award nominations posthumously.
  • Vithabai Narayangaokar: greatest Tamasha and Lavani performer; "Tamasha Samragni (Queen)".

South India Folk

  • Burra Katha (AP/Telangana): narrative folk performance; three performers; Janapada tradition.
  • Villupattu (Tamil Nadu): bow-song; bow (villu) used as musical instrument.
  • Yakshagana (Karnataka): combination of music, dance, dialogue, costume; folk theatre-song.

Common Instruments in Folk Music

  • Dholak: small barrel drum; used in Lavani, Bhangra, folk songs.
  • Daf/Dafli: frame drum; Maharashtra + North India folk.
  • Ektara: one-string plucked instrument; Baul + Varkari saints.
  • Tuntune: single-string; Maharashtra Shahirs.
  • Morchang (Morsing): jaw harp; Rajasthan + South India.
  • Shehnai (folk context): auspicious occasions.
  • Pepa: bamboo horn; Bihu (Assam).

Maharashtra Connection

Maharashtra's folk music is central to this topic:

  • Lavani: declared the state folk dance/song form; associated with Tamasha (folk theatre); Dholak + Tuntune accompaniment; most popular form.
  • Powada ballads on Shivaji: created during Shivaji's own lifetime by Shahir Agindas; Govind (wrote Agindas Powada); tradition continued.
  • Warkari Kirtan: integral to the Pandharpur pilgrimage tradition; saints like Tukaram used Kirtan to spread bhakti.
  • Tamasha: folk theatre genre; Lavani is its musical backbone; Vithabai Narayangaokar; performed at fairs and night-long shows.
  • Gondhal: specific to Maharashtra; ritual significance; Ekveera Devi, Tulja Bhavani worshipped.
  • Maharashtra Lokshahir: tradition of folk poets/bards as social commentators continues; Annabhau Sathe's Lavani as protest literature.

Key Facts

  • Lavani: Maharashtra's signature folk music-dance; Dholak + Tuntune; Nirguni + Shringari.
  • Powada: Maratha heroic ballad; Shahir tradition.
  • Kirtan: devotional narrative; Varkari + Naradiya.
  • Baul: Bengal mystic minstrels; UNESCO ICH 2008.
  • Bihu: Assam; three types; pepa (horn).
  • Bhangra: Punjab; harvest/Baisakhi; dhol.
  • Garba: Gujarat; Navratri; UNESCO ICH 2023.
  • Annabhau Sathe: Dalit Shahir; "Tukaram of Maharashtra".

Exam Traps

  • Baul = Bengal (UNESCO ICH 2008) — not to be confused with Bhangra (Punjab).
  • Lavani is both music AND dance — in the context of Tamasha it's performed; in standalone concerts it's sung.
  • Garba UNESCO ICH = 2023 (recent; very likely to appear in current-affairs questions).
  • Bihu has three sub-types based on agricultural season — Prelims often asks to identify the correct one.
