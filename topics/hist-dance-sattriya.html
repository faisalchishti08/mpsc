Sattriya (Assam) PREMAINS

Overview

Sattriya is the classical dance form of Assam, the most recently recognised of India's 8 classical dance forms (recognised by Sangeet Natak Akademi in 2000). It originated in the Vaishnavite Sattras (monasteries) of Assam, founded by the reformer-saint Srimanta Sankardeva in the 15th–16th century. Traditionally performed by male monks, it encompasses dance, drama, music, and devotional narrative. MPSC tests its recognition year, founder, and its monastic origin.

In-Depth

Origin and Development

  • Srimanta Sankardeva (1449–1568): polymath saint-reformer of Assam; founded the Ekasarana Naam Dharma (one-god devotional movement); established Sattras (Vaishnavite monasteries) across Assam; composed the Borgeet (devotional songs) and Ankiya Naat (one-act plays) that form the textual basis of Sattriya.
  • The Sattras served as centres of religion, education, music and dance; Sattriya was a means of teaching Hindu mythology and Vaishnavite devotion to illiterate rural people.
  • Historically: performed only by male monks (Bhokots) inside the Sattra precincts; not open to women or public entertainment.
  • Modernisation: 20th century — opened to women and public stages; Maheswar Neog and other scholars documented and codified the tradition.
  • Recognised as classical: 2000 — the last of the 8 to receive recognition.

ThemesStories of Vishnu/Krishna; acts from Ramayana, Mahabharata; Ankiya Naat (one-act plays)
MusicBorgeet (Sankardeva's devotional songs); *Khol* drum (barrel drum of Assam); cymbals (*taal*)
Mask dancesDistinctive mask tradition (*mukhosh nritya*) — papier-mâché masks for characters like Garuda, Ravana, Narasimha

Borgeet: devotional songs composed by Srimanta Sankardeva and his disciple Madhavdeva; sung in Assamese; form the backbone of Sattriya music.

Ankiya Naat: one-act plays in Brajawali (mixed Assamese-Sanskrit) language; composed by Sankardeva; performed in the Sattra; the narrative context within which Sattriya dance was performed.

Contrast with Other Northeast Traditions

  • Bihu (Assam): not a classical dance — a folk dance performed at the Bihu festival; energetic; cane-rings used.
  • Sattriya is the only classical form from northeast India.
  • Manipuri (northeast) was recognised earlier (pre-2000); Sattriya was the final addition.

Exponents

  • Jatin Goswami: leading contemporary Sattriya practitioner; Padma Shri.
  • Anwesa Mahanta: contemporary female practitioner; brought Sattriya to international stages.

Maharashtra Connection

Minimal direct connection. Assam-Maharashtra cultural links are limited. However, Maharashtra's cultural organisations host Sattriya performances in Mumbai and Pune. Sankardeva's social reform message (caste equality through devotion) parallels Maharashtra's Warkari and Bhakti traditions.

Key Facts

  • State: Assam.
  • Founded by: Srimanta Sankardeva (1449–1568).
  • Setting: Vaishnavite Sattras (monasteries).
  • Recognised as classical: 2000 (last of the 8).
  • Music: Borgeet (Sankardeva's songs); Khol drum.
  • Mask dances (mukhosh nritya): unique feature.
  • Literary basis: Ankiya Naat (one-act plays, Brajawali language).
  • Traditional performers: Bhokots (male monks).

Memory Aids

  • Sattriya = Assam + Sattra (monastery) + Sankardeva + 2000 recognition.
  • Last recognised = "Sattriya = 2000" (easiest MPSC fact to test).
  • Mask dances = Sattriya's unique feature (no other classical form has a mask tradition).
  • Borgeet = "Borg = devotional; Sankardeva + Madhavdeva composed".

Exam Traps

  • Sattriya was recognised in 2000 — NOT earlier; it is the 8th and last classical form recognised.
  • Bihu is NOT a classical dance — it is Assam's folk dance; Sattriya is the classical form.
  • Khol drum ≠ Mridangam — Khol is the Assamese/Bengali barrel drum; Mridangam is the Tamil/South Indian form.
