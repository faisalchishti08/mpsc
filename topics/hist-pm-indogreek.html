Indo-Greeks and Shakas PREMAINS

Overview

The Indo-Greeks and Shakas were the first significant groups of foreign rulers who settled permanently in India and became culturally Indian. The Indo-Greeks (Bactrian Greeks) entered northwestern India c. 200–100 BCE; the Shakas (Scythians) displaced them and established the Western Satraps (c. 1st–4th century CE) in western India. Both groups are important for MPSC because of their numismatic legacy (bilingual coins), contributions to astronomy, medicine, and art (Gandhara school), and interactions with Indian politics and Buddhism.

In-Depth

Indo-Greeks (Bactrian Greeks) — Background

After Alexander's death (323 BCE), the Seleucid Empire inherited his eastern territories. The Bactrian Greeks — descendants of Greek settlers in Bactria (modern Afghanistan/Uzbekistan) — declared independence from the Seleucids under Diodotus I (c. 250 BCE). Around 200–190 BCE, Demetrius I of Bactria invaded northwestern India, starting the Indo-Greek kingdom in the Subcontinent.

Menander I (Milinda) — Most Famous Indo-Greek

Menander I (Pali: Milinda) ruled from Sagala (Sialkot, modern Pakistan). He is the best-known Indo-Greek ruler because:

  • He converted to Buddhism (or deeply engaged with it) based on the Milindapanha ("Questions of King Milinda") — a Pali Buddhist text in which the philosopher-monk Nagasena answers Menander's philosophical questions about Buddhism. This text is a major source for Buddhist philosophy.
  • His coins bear a wheel motif (Dhammachakra) suggesting Buddhist leanings.
  • His kingdom stretched from the Oxus to the Ravi, and he may have reached as far east as Pataliputra (Shunga capital) according to Yuga Purana.
  • After his death, different cities claimed his ashes — parallel to the Buddha's relic distribution story, suggesting cult status.

Contributions of Indo-Greeks

  1. Coinage innovation: Indo-Greeks introduced bilingual coins (Greek on one side, Kharoshthi/Brahmi on the reverse), the round coin (earlier Indian coins were punch-marked), and portrait coins (king's face on coins — first in India). They introduced the concept of the coin-legend and die-struck coinage.
  2. Gandhara Art: Indo-Greek cultural interaction generated the Gandhara school of Buddhist art (see hist-pm-kushana for full treatment), which depicted the Buddha in Hellenistic style (Apollo-like features, toga-draped robe, realistic drapery).
  3. Astronomy: Greek astronomical knowledge filtered into India through this period — the Greek concept of horoscope (hora) appears in Sanskrit as hora in Indian astronomy.

Shakas (Scythians) — Origins and Entry

The Shakas (Greek: Scythians; Sanskrit: Shaka) were Central Asian nomadic pastoralists who invaded Bactria, displacing the Greeks, and then pushed into India c. 80–60 BCE through the Bolan Pass. They divided into five branches, occupying:

  • Taxila branch (Punjab, briefly)
  • Mathura branch (ruled until ~4th century CE)
  • Western Satraps (Nasik/Konkan, Nashik, Pune, Gujarat, Rajasthan — most important branch)

Western Satraps (Kshatrapas) — c. 35–415 CE

The Western Satraps ruled western India from Ujjain (primarily) and are the most historically significant Shaka group for India:

  • Founded by Nahapana (c. 105–124 CE) — initially based at Nashik region, Maharashtra.
  • Nahapana was defeated and his coins cancelled/restruck by Gautamiputra Satakarni (Satavahana) c. 120 CE.
  • The Chastana dynasty (later branch) ruled from Ujjain from c. 130 CE onwards.
  • Rudradaman I (c. 150 CE): greatest Western Satrap — his Junagadh Rock Inscription (Girnar, 150 CE) is the first major Sanskrit prose inscription in India. He repaired the Sudarshana Lake (Mauryan tank built by Pushyagupta and repaired by Ashoka's governor Tushaspa). He defeated a Satavahana king twice. His inscription praises his Sanskrit learning — evidence of Shaka cultural integration.

Maharashtra Connection

Moderate-high for Maharashtra. Nahapana, the Western Shaka Satrap, operated extensively in the Nashik–Pune region before being defeated by Gautamiputra Satakarni. The Jogalthambi coin hoard (Nashik) — thousands of Nahapana's silver coins with Gautamiputra's restrike — is a direct Maharashtra link. The Shaka influence appears in Maharashtra's early medieval epigraphy (Nashik cave inscriptions mention Shaka-era donations).

Key Facts

  • Demetrius I: first Indo-Greek king to invade India (~200 BCE).
  • Menander/Milinda: greatest Indo-Greek; associated with Milindapanha; capital Sagala (Sialkot).
  • Bilingual coins: Greek script + Kharoshthi — introduced by Indo-Greeks.
  • Portrait coins in India: introduced by Indo-Greeks.
  • Rudradaman I's inscription: Junagadh (Girnar) Rock Inscription, 150 CE — first major Sanskrit prose.
  • Sudarshana Lake repaired by: Chandragupta's governor → Ashoka's governor → Rudradaman I.
  • Nahapana defeated by: Gautamiputra Satakarni; coins found at Jogalthambi (Nashik).
  • Menander: may have reached Pataliputra (Shunga capital) per Yuga Purana.

Memory Aids

  • "Demetrius → Menander → Hermaeus → Shakas" — succession of NW India.
  • "Milinda = Menander" — same person, Pali vs Greek name.
  • Rudradaman = "Junagadh 150 CE" = first Sanskrit prose inscription.
  • "Shaka Satraps ruled Ujjain" — later defeated by Gupta king Chandragupta II (Vikramaditya).

Exam Traps

  • Do NOT confuse Menander (Indo-Greek king) with Nanda/Maurya kings.
  • The Milindapanha is a Buddhist text, not an Indo-Greek text — written by Indians about Menander.
  • Rudradaman's Junagadh inscription is not the first inscription in India — it's the first major Sanskrit prose inscription (Ashokan inscriptions were in Prakrit).
  • Shakas were finally ended by Gupta king Chandragupta II (c. 395–415 CE), not by the Satavahanas — keep the timeline clear.
