Third Battle of Panipat (1761) PREMAINSMH

Overview

The Third Battle of Panipat (14 January 1761) was one of the most consequential battles in 18th-century Indian history: the Maratha confederacy under Sadashivrao Bhau and the young Viswasrao (Peshwa's son) was decisively defeated by the Afghan ruler Ahmad Shah Abdali (Durrani). The battle ended Maratha ambitions to dominate north India as Mughal successors and led to a decade of Maratha weakness. Very high MPSC MH frequency.

In-Depth

Background and Causes

By the 1750s, the Marathas were the dominant power in north India — protecting the nominal Mughal emperor, collecting chauth across India. However, Afghan ruler Ahmad Shah Abdali invaded India repeatedly from 1748, alarmed by Maratha expansion into Punjab.

Immediate causes:

  • Marathas backed the restoration of Alamgir II (Mughal emperor); Abdali backed his rival.
  • Marathas drove out Abdali's governor of Punjab (Timur Shah) in 1758 — provocative.
  • Abdali returned for his sixth invasion (1759–60).
  • Marathas sent an army under Sadashivrao Bhau (cousin of Peshwa Balaji Bajirao) to confront him.

Critical failure: Marathas were unable to secure the alliance of Suraj Mal (Jats) and Shuja-ud-Daulah (Awadh). These potential allies withdrew or sided with Abdali, leaving the Marathas isolated. The Marathas also carried a vast civilian camp (pesha) with women and families — reducing mobility.

The Battle (14 January 1761)

The battle lasted all day in bitterly cold weather on the Panipat plain (Haryana):

  • The Marathas adopted an aggressive formation but were outflanked.
  • Abdali held back his reserve cavalry under his son Timur Shah and general Jahan Khan for the decisive moment.
  • Viswasrao (Vishwasrao) was killed early in battle — his death broke Maratha morale.
  • Sadashivrao Bhau rushed into the melee personally and was killed (his body never officially identified; some accounts say he survived and became a sadhu — persistent legend).
  • By evening, the Maratha army collapsed; tens of thousands were killed in battle and pursuit.

Casualties: estimates vary widely; 20,000–40,000 Maratha soldiers killed; possibly equal numbers of camp followers killed in the rout. Many nobles, including Jankov and Ibrahim Khan Gardi (Maratha's Deccan artillerist), died.

Consequences

  • Peshwa Balaji Bajirao died of shock/grief 23 June 1761 — his health never recovered from the news.
  • The battle decisively ended Maratha dominance in north India; Abdali withdrew to Afghanistan but had demonstrated Maratha military limitations.
  • Power vacuum: neither Marathas nor Mughals could dominate; this directly benefited the British East India Company which expanded rapidly in the 1760s (Battle of Buxar 1764).
  • Madhavrao I (1761–1772) revived Maratha power within a decade — reasserted control over Rajputs, Mysore, Bengal.
  • The Maratha north Indian sphere (Scindia in Gwalior, Holkar in Indore) was reorganised after 1761.

Why the Marathas Lost — Analytical Reasons

  1. Failure to secure alliances: Suraj Mal and Awadh remained neutral or hostile; internal Maratha rivalries prevented unity.
  2. Supply crisis: Marathas were besieged around Panipat for months; starvation weakened them before the battle.
  3. Artillery inferiority: Abdali's artillery (under Shuja-ud-Daulah's contribution) outmatched; Maratha artillery (Ibrahim Khan Gardi's corps) was good but insufficient.
  4. Strategic overextension: Marathas far from home base; Afghan forces better supplied locally.
  5. Leadership errors: Bhau's decision to fight on an open plain disadvantaged fast-moving Maratha cavalry.

Maharashtra Connection

Deeply impactful on Maharashtra:

  • Sadashivrao Bhau from Pune's Peshwa family — the loss devastated nearly every aristocratic family in Pune.
  • Contemporary accounts say almost no family in Pune was spared a death; widows' lamentations in Pune gave rise to the phrase "asked God not to show again such a Panipat".
  • Balaji Bajirao (Nana Saheb Peshwa) died of grief — succession then went to Madhavrao I, whose ability saved Maharashtra from permanent decline.
  • The battle is memorialised in Maharashtra's cultural consciousness — multiple folk songs (powadas) lament the Panipat.

Key Facts

  • Date: 14 January 1761 (Makar Sankranti).
  • Maratha commanders: Sadashivrao Bhau + Viswasrao.
  • Afghan commander: Ahmad Shah Abdali (Durrani).
  • Abdali's key allies: Najib-ud-Daulah + Shuja-ud-Daulah (Awadh).
  • Marathas' key ally who withdrew: Suraj Mal (Jat ruler of Bharatpur).
  • Viswasrao killed during battle; Bhau's fate uncertain (officially killed).
  • Peshwa Balaji Bajirao died of grief: 23 June 1761.
  • Immediate beneficiary of the battle: British EIC (Buxar 1764 followed).
  • Maratha revival: Madhavrao I (1761–1772).

Exam Traps

  • Third Panipat (1761) is Ahmad Shah Abdali vs Marathas, NOT Afghans vs Mughals.
  • Suraj Mal (Jat) ≠ Shuja-ud-Daulah (Awadh) — Suraj Mal withdrew from Maratha alliance; Shuja sided with Abdali.
  • Viswasrao died in battle; Bhau's death is historically stated but body unrecovered — don't say "both survived."
  • The battle is 14 January 1761, not 1760 (campaigns started 1759–60 but battle was Jan 1761).
