Ranade, Agarkar, Gokhale, Tilak PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

Mahadev Govind Ranade (1842–1901), Gopal Ganesh Agarkar (1856–1895), Gopal Krishna Gokhale (1866–1915), and Bal Gangadhar Tilak (1856–1920) represent the mainstream reform and nationalist tradition of 19th–early 20th century Maharashtra. Ranade founded the Prarthana Samaj and advocated rational social reform; Tilak championed aggressive nationalism; Gokhale was Gandhi's political mentor. MPSC tests their organisations, key works, differences, and dates.

In-Depth

Mahadev Govind Ranade (1842–1901) — The "Father of Maharashtra's Reform Movement"

AspectDetail
Born18 January 1842, Niphad, Nashik district
CareerJudge of the Bombay High Court; also social reformer and economist
OrganisationsPrarthana Samaj (1867, Bombay) — Hindu reformist; Social Conference (National Social Conference, 1887)
Key work"Rise of the Maratha Power" (1900); essays on Indian economic history and widow remarriage
Social reformsChampioned widow remarriage, women's education, abolition of child marriage, caste discrimination
ApproachModerate, gradualist; reform within Hindu tradition; influence of Western liberalism

Prarthana Samaj (1867): Founded by Atmaram Pandurang; Ranade became its leading figure. Similar to Brahmo Samaj (Bengal) — monotheistic, rationalist, opposed idol worship and caste. Distinguished from Phule's Satyashodhak by being Brahmin-led and more moderate.

Gopal Ganesh Agarkar (1856–1895) — Rationalist Radical

AspectDetail
Born14 July 1856, Tembhu (Satara)
Key publication"Sudharak" (Reformer) — Marathi journal he founded and edited; rationalist, anti-superstition
Early workCo-founded Deccan Education Society (1884) with Tilak, Vishnu Shastri Chiplunkar; also "New English School" Pune (1880)
ViewsBelieved social reform must PRECEDE political freedom (contrast with Tilak who said political freedom first)
Died17 June 1895, age 38 — tuberculosis

Agarkar-Tilak debate: The famous intellectual divide — Agarkar argued social reform is the foundation of political freedom; Tilak argued political freedom (Swarajya) is the first necessity. This debate shaped Maharashtra's and India's nationalist discourse.

Tilak vs. Agarkar (and Tilak vs. Gokhale): The "Lal-Bal-Pal" triad (Lala Lajpat Rai, Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Bipin Chandra Pal) = Extremists of Congress. Tilak championed mass mobilisation through religion/culture (Ganesh Utsav, Shivaji Jayanti) and rejected the Moderate's approach of petitioning the British.

Maharashtra Connection

Fergusson College (Pune, 1885) — founded by Tilak, Agarkar and colleagues — remains Pune's most prestigious college. Lokmanya Tilak's birth anniversary (23 July) = Bal Gangadhar Tilak Jayanti (state holiday). Tilak died on 1 August 1920 — "Lokmanya Tilak Smriti Din." Ratnagiri (Tilak's and Gokhale's birthplace) is proud of its reformer heritage. Gokhale's Servants of India Society (Pune) influenced Gandhi's early career in India.

Exam Traps

  • Agarkar and Tilak both born 1856 — different people, same birth year; Agarkar died at 38 (1895), Tilak lived to 64 (1920).
  • Prarthana Samaj (Maharashtra) ≠ Brahmo Samaj (Bengal) — both rationalist reform movements but separate.
  • Tilak started Ganesh Chaturthi as PUBLIC festival in 1893 (not 1857 or 1900).
