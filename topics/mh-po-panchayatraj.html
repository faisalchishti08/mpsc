Panchayati Raj in Maharashtra (Bombay Village Panchayat Act) PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

Maharashtra's Panchayati Raj system is governed by two distinct Acts: the Maharashtra Zilla Parishads, Panchayat Samitis, Gram Panchayats and Nyaya Panchayats Act, 1961 (for ZPs, PS, GPs — covered separately in mh-ad-localbodies) and the older Bombay Village Panchayats Act, 1958 (which predated the ZP-PS structure). This entry focuses on the evolution, key features, 73rd Amendment compliance, and Maharashtra-specific innovations in Panchayati Raj. The statutory framework, election procedures, reservations, and Gram Sabha provisions are frequent MPSC Mains topics.

Maharashtra's PR — Distinctive Features

1. Three-Tier Structure (covered in mh-ad-localbodies) — Brief recap:

  • Zilla Parishad (ZP) — District level
  • Panchayat Samiti (PS) — Taluka/Block level
  • Gram Panchayat (GP) — Village level

2. Direct Election of Sarpanch (2017):

  • Maharashtra was among the first states to switch to direct election of Gram Panchayat Sarpanch by all voters of the GP area — rather than election by GP members.
  • This change was introduced in 2017 to strengthen local accountability and reduce "horse-trading" in GP elections.
  • Note: Several states have reverted to indirect election due to practical issues; Maharashtra maintained direct election.

3. 50% Women's Reservation:

  • Maharashtra provides 50% reservation for women across all three tiers (GP, PS, ZP) — exceeding the constitutional minimum of 33%.
  • Also reserves seats for SC/ST in proportion to their population; OBC reservation up to 27% as permitted.

4. Gram Sabha — Powers in Maharashtra:

  • Gram Sabha consists of all registered voters in the GP area.
  • Must meet at least twice a year (official requirement; 4 meetings recommended).
  • Gram Sabha functions: Approval of GP annual accounts and audit; approval of GP development plan; social audit of MGNREGS works; election of beneficiaries for welfare schemes.
  • Maharashtra has also experimented with Gram Sabha approval for minor works (roads, wells) — giving villages direct say.

5. State Election Commission (SEC):

  • Established under Article 243K (73rd Amendment).
  • Maharashtra State Election Commission (MSEC) conducts all PR and ULB elections.
  • SEC Commissioner appointed by Governor; removed only by similar process as High Court Judge.

6. State Finance Commission (SFC):

  • Established under Article 243I.
  • Maharashtra SFCs have been constituted (several rounds) to recommend devolution of funds from state to PRIs and ULBs.
  • 15th Finance Commission (central) also provides grants directly to GPs (tied + untied grants).

7. Eleventh Schedule (29 Functions): The 73rd Amendment lists 29 subjects that may be transferred to Panchayati Raj Institutions (the state may or may not transfer all). Maharashtra has gradually transferred several:

  • Agriculture including agricultural extension
  • Minor irrigation, water management
  • Social forestry and farm forestry
  • Small-scale industries
  • Rural housing, Drinking water
  • Roads, culverts
  • Non-conventional energy sources
  • Primary and secondary education
  • Markets and fairs
  • Public distribution system
  • Maintenance of community assets
  • etc.

The Gram Nyayalaya vs. Nyaya Panchayat

  • Maharashtra's 1961 Act has provisions for Nyaya Panchayats — village-level dispute resolution for minor civil/petty criminal matters.
  • The Gram Nyayalayas Act, 2008 (central) created a separate mobile courts system; Maharashtra has had limited implementation of Gram Nyayalayas as formal courts — Nyaya Panchayats under the 1961 Act continue to be the primary forum.

Maharashtra Connection

Maharashtra's 1961 Act established Panchayati Raj 31 years before the 73rd Amendment — making it a model for other states. The ZP CEO model (an IAS officer controlling ZP administration) is distinctive. Maharashtra's Panchayat Raj Training Institutes (PRTI) and Yashada (Yashwantrao Chavan Academy of Development Administration, Pune) train elected PR representatives and government officials in PR governance.

Key Facts

  • Primary Act: Maharashtra Zilla Parishads, Panchayat Samitis, GPs & Nyaya Panchayats Act, 1961
  • Pre-existing Act: Bombay Village Panchayats Act, 1958
  • 73rd Amendment: came into force 24 April 1993
  • Sarpanch direct election: Maharashtra switched to direct election in 2017
  • Women's reservation: 50% (enhanced from 33% in 2011)
  • Gram Sabha: 2 meetings/year minimum; key functions: social audit, beneficiary selection
  • SEC: Article 243K; MSEC conducts all PR elections; Commissioner removed like HC judge
  • SFC: Article 243I; recommends fund devolution
  • 11th Schedule: 29 functions listed for potential transfer to PRIs
