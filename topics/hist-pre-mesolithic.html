Mesolithic age — microliths, rock art PREMAINS

Overview

The Mesolithic Age (Middle Stone Age, ~10,000–4,000 BCE in India) bridges the Paleolithic and Neolithic. Its defining features are microlithic tools (tiny, geometric stone blades), more sophisticated rock art, and the beginning of semi-settled or semi-nomadic life, possibly including early animal domestication. Bhimbetka spans both Paleolithic and Mesolithic. MPSC tests on microlithic tool characteristics, key sites (Bagor, Adamgarh), and the transition to food production.

In-Depth

Defining Characteristics of Mesolithic

  1. Microlithic tools: Very small (1–5 cm) geometric stone tools — blades, lunates (crescents), triangles, trapezoids. Made from flint, chert, chalcedony. Used as composite tools — inserted into bone or wood handles.
  2. Rock art: Continued and expanded from Upper Paleolithic; paintings at Bhimbetka, Adamgarh (MP), Pachmarhi (MP) depict hunting, dancing, community life in vivid polychrome.
  3. Semi-nomadic/semi-settled: Groups stayed longer near rivers and water bodies; seasonal camps near food resources.
  4. Early domestication: Evidence of early dog domestication at Bagor (Rajasthan). Some sites show early cattle/sheep/goat bones — debate on whether herded or hunted.
  5. Fishing and gathering: Coastal and riverine Mesolithic communities relied on shellfish, fish, and gathered plant foods (tubers, fruits).

Mesolithic Rock Art

Mesolithic rock paintings at Bhimbetka and Adamgarh are the richest visual record of prehistoric life in India:

  • Subjects: Animals (bison, deer, rhinoceros, elephant), hunting scenes, dancing and ritual scenes, battle scenes between communities.
  • Colours: Red (iron oxide/haematite), white (limestone/gypsum), green (copper ore), yellow (ochre).
  • Technique: Painted with fingers, twigs, or hair brushes.
  • Paintings continued to be created over centuries — some are superimposed on earlier paintings, creating stratigraphic layers.

Transition to Neolithic

The Mesolithic-Neolithic boundary is gradual and varied across India:

  • In the Ganga Plain (~7th–6th millennium BCE), microlithic industries persisted even as Neolithic settlements emerged.
  • In the Deccan/South India, Neolithic cultures appear ~3000–2500 BCE; microlithic traditions continued in coastal and forested regions much longer.
  • The key shift: Mesolithic people did not produce food (they hunted and gathered); Neolithic people began producing food (agriculture, herding). Some Mesolithic communities may have had proto-agricultural practices.

Physical Characteristics of Mesolithic People

Skeletal evidence (Sarai Nahar Rai, Mahadaha, Damdama — all UP):

  • Tall stature; long-headed (dolichocephalic); robust; life expectancy ~35 years.
  • Lived in open-air camps near rivers.
  • Buried their dead in extended position (supine); grave goods (microliths) placed with the dead.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Inamgaon and Nevasa have evidence of Mesolithic occupation layers before their Chalcolithic phase.
  • Maharashtra's forest zones (Satpuda, Sahyadri) had Mesolithic hunter-gatherer communities that persisted into historical times — some scholars link traits of Mesolithic culture to ancestral practices of modern tribal communities like Katkari and Warli.
  • The Deccan's microlithic sites are studied primarily by Deccan College (Pune) — Maharashtra is the centre of prehistoric research for the region.

Key Facts

  • Mesolithic tools: Microliths — tiny geometric blades (lunate, triangle, trapezoid).
  • Best rock art site: Bhimbetka (Mesolithic phase: ~10,000–8,000 BCE paintings onward).
  • First evidence of dog domestication in India: Adamgarh, MP.
  • Largest Mesolithic site: Bagor, Rajasthan.
  • Mesolithic burials found at: Sarai Nahar Rai, Mahadaha, Damdama (all UP).
  • Rock painting colours: red (haematite), white (limestone), green (copper ore).
  • Period: ~10,000–4,000 BCE in India (varies regionally).

Memory Aids

  • "Micro tools, Mega art" — Mesolithic = tiny tools + giant rock painting legacy.
  • Sites: "B-A-B-S" — Bhimbetka, Adamgarh, Bagor, Sarai Nahar Rai.
  • Tool shapes: "LTT" — Lunate, Triangle, Trapezoid.
  • Adamgarh = Animal domestication evidence in India (earliest).

Exam Traps

  • Bhimbetka is famous for BOTH Paleolithic and Mesolithic — don't assign it exclusively to one period.
  • Microliths are Mesolithic tools; do NOT call them Paleolithic. Paleolithic tools are large (handaxe, cleaver).
  • Adamgarh is in Madhya Pradesh (Hoshangabad), NOT in Rajasthan — easy confusion with Bagor.
  • Mesolithic people were NOT farmers — agriculture is Neolithic. Some had proto-herding, but full food production came only in Neolithic.
