Samarth Ramdas — Dasbodh PREMAINSMH

Overview

Samarth Ramdas Swami (1608–1681 CE) is the fifth foundational saint of Maharashtra's Bhakti tradition and a unique figure — as much a social organiser and national awakener as a devotional poet. Unlike the Warkari saints centred on Vitthal, Ramdas was a Rama devotee and is associated with Shivaji Maharaj (who reportedly considered him his guru or spiritual guide). His Dasbodh — a prose-poetry treatise in 20 chapters — is a practical guide to life, governance, and spirituality. MPSC tests Ramdas extensively because of his connection to Shivaji, his works (Dasbodh, Manache Shlok), and his tradition (Math establishment).

In-Depth

Life

  • Born: 1608 CE, Jamb village, Jalna district, Maharashtra.
  • Birth name: Narayan Suryajipant Thosar (Narayan).
  • Caste: Deshastha Brahmin (Parashar Gotra).
  • Family: At his own wedding (~age 12), he heard the marriage ceremony mantra "Savdhana" (Be Alert!) and took it as a divine call — ran away from the wedding and left the householder life forever.
  • Pilgrimages: Undertook 12 years of strict asceticism and pilgrimage across India; visited Nasik, Kashi, Ayodhya, South India.
  • Nashik: Settled at Nasik (Takli) for 12 years on the banks of the Godavari — intense austerity; counted 30 crore Rama-names; worshipped Surya (Sun) each morning.
  • Shivaji connection: Ramdas met Shivaji (accounts vary on exact date and nature of relationship). Traditional account: Shivaji offered his kingdom to Ramdas; Ramdas gave the saffron flag (bhagwa dhwaj) and said it was God's kingdom, not Shivaji's — symbolic moment of spiritual nationalism.
  • Sajjangad (near Satara): Ramdas's primary residence in later life; the fort of Sajjangad was given by Shivaji to Ramdas; his samadhi is there.
  • Death: 1681 CE, Sajjangad, Satara district.

Dasbodh — Key Features

  • Full name: Dasbodh = Das (servant) + bodh (teaching/awakening) = "Teaching of the Servant [of Rama]."
  • Structure: 20 Dashaks (chapters) × 10 Samasas (sub-chapters) × 10 Ovis (verses) ideally; in practice varies.
  • Unique character: Unlike devotional literature, Dasbodh addresses practical life — how to lead a disciplined life, select good disciples, manage resources, govern, recognise enemies, control the mind.
  • Dictated to disciple Kalyanswami at Shivthar Ghal (a cave near Mahad, Raigad district).
  • Rama devotion at the core; but the text is as much practical ethics as devotional literature.

Manache Shlok

  • 205 Ovis addressed to the mind (mana) with imperatives: "O mind, do this..." "O mind, don't do that..."
  • Extremely practical; covers qualities of ideal character, avoidance of arrogance, service to others.
  • Among the most widely memorised texts in Marathi; recited in schools and homes.
  • Opening verse: "Mana apule sadhi sadhi jagate, mana je karo te karo changa" — "O mind, discipline yourself gently; whatever you do, do it well."

Ramdas and Shivaji: The Relationship

  • The exact nature of the Ramdas-Shivaji relationship is historically debated:
  • Traditional account: Ramdas was Shivaji's guru (spiritual teacher); Shivaji offered his kingdom; Ramdas returned it with the bhagwa dhwaj (saffron flag).
  • Scholarly view: More likely Ramdas was a preceptor (not formal guru in initiation sense) and spiritual inspiration; Shivaji's formal religious guru was Kondadev/family deity tradition.
  • Shivaji reportedly wrote letters (Patra) to Ramdas.
  • Sajjangad Fort (Satara): Given by Shivaji to Ramdas; Ramdas lived and died there.
  • The Maratha saffron flag (bhagwa dhwaj) — associated with this tradition.

Eleven Maths (Temples-Centres)

  • Ramdas established 1,100 maths (monasteries/centres) across Maharashtra — an unprecedented organisational effort to spread Rama devotion and social awakening.
  • These centres served as points of spiritual and political-social mobilisation.
  • Hanuman temples: Ramdas was also known for installing Hanuman idols across Maharashtra to inspire the warrior spirit.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Born in Jamb, Jalna: district has Ramdas centres.
  • Nashik: 12-year austerity at Takli on Godavari; pilgrimage site.
  • Sajjangad, Satara: samadhi; major pilgrimage; largest festival at Ramdas Navami.
  • Shivthar Ghal, Raigad: where Dasbodh was dictated; cave heritage site.
  • Shivaji connection: integral to Maharashtra's political-spiritual identity; Ramdas as inspiration for the Maratha cause.
  • 1,100 maths: physical presence across Maharashtra; Ramdas Sampradaya (Dasnavami) is an active monastic tradition.
  • Manache Shlok: still widely recited in Maharashtra's schools and homes.

Exam Traps

  • Ramdas is NOT a Warkari saint — he worshipped Rama (not Vitthal); he belongs to the Dasnavami/Ramdasi tradition, not the Pandharpur Warkari tradition.
  • Dasbodh ≠ Dnyaneshwari — Dnyaneshwari is Bhagavad Gita commentary (Dnyaneshwar); Dasbodh is Ramdas's own practical treatise.
  • Ramdas's guru (in formal initiation sense) is disputed — not Eknath or Tukaram; he was self-initiated through Rama's grace.
  • Shivaji's formal guru ≠ Ramdas necessarily — historically more nuanced; questions that say "Ramdas was Shivaji's spiritual guide" are safer than "formal guru."
