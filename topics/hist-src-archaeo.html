Archaeological sources — inscriptions, coins, monuments PREMAINS

Overview

Archaeological sources are material remains of the past — inscriptions, coins, monuments, pottery, tools, and objects recovered from excavations. They provide direct, often contemporaneous evidence independent of literary bias. For MPSC, this topic is foundational to Ancient and Medieval History; examiners ask about specific inscriptions (Ashokan edicts, Allahabad Pillar Inscription), coins (punch-marked, gold, copper), and excavation sites — both as MCQ facts and as Mains short-answer on source limitations.

In-Depth

Inscriptions

Inscriptions are texts engraved on stone, metal, or clay. They are among the most reliable historical sources because they are contemporary documents.

Key classes:

  • Ashokan Inscriptions (3rd c. BCE): Mauryan Emperor Ashoka's edicts on rocks and pillars; written in Brahmi (deciphered by James Prinsep, 1837), Kharosthi (NW India), and Greek/Aramaic (Afghanistan). Contain policy statements on Dhamma, welfare, and governance. Major sites: Girnar (Gujarat), Dhauli (Odisha), Sanchi, Kalsi (Uttarakhand), Shahbazgarhi, Mansehra (Pakistan).
  • Allahabad Pillar Inscription (4th c. CE): Written by Harisena, court poet; praises Samudragupta's military campaigns (Prayag prashasti).
  • Aihole Inscription (634 CE): Written by Ravikirti (court poet of Pulakesi II of Chalukya); records Pulakesi II's defeat of Harsha; references the Mahabharata war date.
  • Hathigumpha Inscription (Udayagiri, Odisha): Of Kharavela of Kalinga (1st c. BCE); records his campaigns and conquests.
  • Junagarh Rock Inscription: Of Rudradaman I (Kshatrapa, 2nd c. CE); oldest Sanskrit prose inscription; mentions Sudarshana Lake.

Coins

Coins provide evidence on rulers' names, dates, religious affiliations, trade, and territorial extent.

  • Punch-marked coins (Janapada period, ~6th–4th c. BCE): Silver; earliest coins in India; symbols not rulers' names; found in Taxila, Pataliputra hoards.
  • Indo-Greek coins (Menander, Demetrius — 2nd c. BCE): First bilingual, first portrait coins in India; Greek on one side, Kharosthi on the other.
  • Gupta gold coins (Dinaras): Most aesthetically refined; show rulers with bow, horse, lute; give names and titles.
  • Kushana coins: Show both Greek/Iranian and Hindu/Buddhist imagery; bilingual; key source for Kanishka's chronology.
  • Roman coins: Found in South India (Arikamedu, Karur) — evidence of Indo-Roman trade in Early Common Era.

Monuments and Excavated Sites

  • Harappan sites (Harappa, Mohenjo-daro, Dholavira, Lothal): Provide evidence of urban planning, trade, and religion.
  • Sanchi Stupa (Madhya Pradesh): Mauryan-origin Buddhist monument; inscribed gateways (toranas); dated ~3rd c. BCE–1st c. CE.
  • Nalanda, Taxila: Ruins of ancient universities.
  • Megalithic sites (Southern India, Deccan): Large stone burial structures (dolmens, cists) — evidence of Iron Age cultures (1st millennium BCE).

Limitations of Archaeological Sources

  1. Incomplete survival: Only durable materials (stone, metal, fired clay) survive; organic materials decay.
  2. Interpretation bias: Without textual corroboration, meaning can be contested (e.g., Harappan script undeciphered).
  3. Geographical gaps: Excavation is expensive and uneven; large areas remain unexcavated.
  4. Elite bias: Monuments and large structures built by rulers/elites; common people's lives poorly documented.
  5. Lack of dates: Many objects cannot be precisely dated; relative chronology only.

Maharashtra Connection

Maharashtra has significant archaeological finds:

  • Inamgaon (Pune district): Major Chalcolithic site (Jorwe culture, ~1400–700 BCE); excavated by Deccan College. Evidence of house structures, burials, crop remains.
  • Daimabad (Ahmednagar): Important Chalcolithic-Bronze Age site; famous Daimabad bronzes (chariot, rhinoceros, elephant, buffalo) — largest Bronze Age castings in Deccan.
  • Ajanta, Ellora, Elephanta: Rock-cut architectural and epigraphic heritage (covered under Tourism too).
  • Nashik Caves (Pandu Lena): Hinayana Buddhist cave inscriptions (Satavahana period, ~1st–3rd c. CE).

Key Facts

  • Brahmi script deciphered by: James Prinsep (1837).
  • Oldest Sanskrit prose inscription: Junagarh Rock (Rudradaman I, ~150 CE).
  • Allahabad Pillar Inscription author: Harisena (Gupta period).
  • First portrait coins in India: Indo-Greek (Menander era).
  • Inamgaon/Daimabad: Maharashtra's major Chalcolithic sites.
  • Roman coins in India found at: Arikamedu (Pondicherry), Karur (Tamil Nadu).
  • Ashokan edicts use Brahmi (most of India), Kharosthi (NW), Greek/Aramaic (Afghanistan).

Memory Aids

  • "Prinsep reads Brahmi in 1837" → Ashoka's edicts decoded → whole Mauryan history opens.
  • Inscriptions mnemonic: "A-H-A-J" = Ashokan edicts, Hathigumpha (Kharavela), Allahabad Pillar (Samudragupta), Junagarh (Rudradaman).
  • Coins evolution: Punch-marked → Indo-Greek portrait → Gupta gold → Kushana bilingual.

Exam Traps

  • Brahmi (reads left-to-right) ≠ Kharosthi (reads right-to-left, like Arabic/Persian) — direction matters in MCQs.
  • Allahabad Pillar = Samudragupta (Gupta), NOT Ashoka — the pillar is Mauryan but the famous inscription on it is Gupta.
  • Junagarh Rock — Rudradaman's Sanskrit prose inscription; Ashoka also has a separate Junagarh (Girnar) inscription — they're different texts on the same rock face.
  • Daimabad is in Ahmednagar, not Pune — district matters in MH-GK.
