Mahmud of Ghazni and Muhammad Ghori PREMAINS

Overview

Mahmud of Ghazni (r. 997–1030 CE) and Muhammad Ghori (r. 1173–1206 CE) are the two pivotal figures of the early Turko-Afghan invasions of India. Mahmud conducted 17 raids for plunder without intent to settle; Ghori conquered to stay and establish a permanent empire, founding the Delhi Sultanate. MPSC tests their raids, motivations, battles, and the contrast between "raider" and "conqueror" — a classic Prelims and Mains distinction.

In-Depth

Mahmud of Ghazni — Background

Mahmud was the ruler of the Ghaznavid sultanate centred at Ghazni (Afghanistan). He was the first ruler to bear the title Sultan in the Islamic world (proclaimed 999 CE). His court was one of the most brilliant in the Islamic world: scholars Alberuni (Abu Rayhan al-Biruni) and poet Firdausi (Shahnameh) were at his court.

Mahmud's 17 Raids on India (1000–1027 CE)

Mahmud conducted approximately 17 raids into India, primarily for:

  1. Plunder: enormous wealth from temple treasuries.
  2. Religious motivation: framed as jihad (iconoclasm); breaking "idols."
  3. Political: funding his Afghan state and Central Asian campaigns.

Key raids:

  • 1001 CE: Battle of Peshawar — defeated Jaipal (Shahi dynasty) of Punjab; Jaipal humiliated himself and committed suicide.
  • 1008 CE: Battle of Waihind — defeated Anandapal (Jaipal's son); Rajput coalition including Pratiharas, Chandellas, Kalachuris, also defeated.
  • 1025 CE — Somanath (Somnath): Most famous raid — sacked and plundered the Somnath Temple (Gujarat, on the Saurashtra coast). Took enormous wealth; destroyed the famous Shivalinga. The Somnath raid became a symbol of temple desecration and later Hindu-Muslim political memory — heavily politicised in 19th–20th century colonial and post-colonial discourse.
  • 1027 CE: Last raid — against Jats of the Indus region.

Alberuni accompanied Mahmud's campaigns and wrote Kitab-ul-Hind (c. 1030 CE) — the most comprehensive early Islamic account of India's geography, society, religion, and science. It remains a primary source for early 11th-century India.

Muhammad Ghori (Muizzuddin Muhammad bin Sam)

Muhammad Ghori was the ruler of the Ghurid sultanate (Ghur, Afghanistan). Unlike Mahmud (plunderer), Ghori's goal was permanent conquest and empire:

  • Left trusted generals — Qutb-ud-din Aibak and Bakhtiyar Khilji — to administer conquered territories; this is the organisational act that created the Delhi Sultanate.

Key campaigns:

  • 1175–1178 CE: First Indian campaign — Gujarat; defeated by Chaulukya (Solanki) king Mularaja II at the Battle of Kayadara — Ghori's only significant defeat.
  • 1191 CE: First Battle of Tarain — defeated by Prithviraj Chauhan (Chahamana of Ajmer-Delhi).
  • 1192 CE: Second Battle of Tarain — decisive victory; Prithviraj captured and killed. This battle opened the Gangetic plain.
  • 1194 CE: Battle of Chandawar — defeated Jayachandra (Gahadavala) of Varanasi; Bihar and eastern UP conquered.
  • Ghori's general Bakhtiyar Khilji (1197–1205 CE) conquered Bihar (destroyed Nalanda and Vikramashila monasteries, killing monks) and Bengal.

Alberuni — Key Source

Al-Biruni (Abu Rayhan al-Biruni, 973–1048 CE) accompanied Mahmud of Ghazni to India. His Kitab-ul-Hind ("Book of India"):

  • Written in Arabic; describes Indian philosophy, mathematics, astronomy, geography, religion, and social customs.
  • Noted Indian intellectual insularity: "the Hindus believe there is no country but theirs, no nation like theirs, no kings like theirs, no religion like theirs."
  • Invaluable for understanding early 11th-century Indian intellectual and social conditions.
  • Al-Biruni was the first person to discuss the specific gravity of metals and gemstones accurately.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. Mahmud's raids did not reach Maharashtra. However, the chain of events: Mahmud's raids weakened the Gurjara-Pratiharas → Rajput fragmentation → Ghori's conquest → Delhi Sultanate → Alauddin Khilji's Deccan raids (1296 CE) against the Yadavas of Devagiri (Maharashtra) is the direct causal chain linking this topic to Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • Mahmud's raids: 17 (1000–1027 CE).
  • Mahmud's title: Sultan — first bearer of this title in the Islamic world.
  • Somnath raid: 1025 CE — most famous.
  • Alberuni: wrote Kitab-ul-Hind (~1030 CE).
  • Firdausi: wrote Shahnameh (Epic of Kings) — at Mahmud's court.
  • First Battle of Tarain: 1191 CE — Ghori defeated by Prithviraj.
  • Second Battle of Tarain: 1192 CE — Prithviraj defeated; Delhi Sultanate era begins.
  • Bakhtiyar Khilji: destroyed Nalanda and Vikramashila monasteries (c. 1197–1203 CE).

Exam Traps

  • Mahmud never established a permanent empire in India — he raided and left; do NOT say he founded the Delhi Sultanate.
  • Ghori founded the basis of the Delhi Sultanate through his generals — but Ghori himself never ruled from Delhi; it was his successor Aibak who became the first Sultan of Delhi.
  • Alberuni was at Mahmud's court, not Ghori's.
  • Nalanda was destroyed by Bakhtiyar Khilji (Ghori's general) — NOT by Mahmud.
