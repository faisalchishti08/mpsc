Varna system and society PREMAINS

Overview

The Varna system is the fourfold social classification of ancient Indian society into Brahman (priests/scholars), Kshatriya (warriors/rulers), Vaishya (traders/farmers), and Shudra (servants). Originally a functional/occupational division in the Rigveda's Purusha Sukta (Book 10), it gradually became hereditary and rigid by the Later Vedic period. The varna system is foundational to understanding Indian social history, caste, and the social reform movements of Maharashtra. MPSC asks on its origin, evolution, the Purusha Sukta, the Ashrama system, and its critique.

In-Depth

Origin — Purusha Sukta (Rigveda 10.90)

The Purusha Sukta hymn describes the cosmic sacrifice of the Purusha (Primeval Being) from whose body the universe was created:

  • Brahman emerged from the mouth.
  • Kshatriya from the arms.
  • Vaishya from the thighs.
  • Shudra from the feet.

This is the first literary reference to the four-varna system. Historians note that Rigveda Book 10 is considered a later addition (~1000–800 BCE), so this passage belongs to the transitional Early–Later Vedic phase, not the oldest stratum of the Rigveda.

"Twice-born" (Dvija): The three upper varnas (Brahman, Kshatriya, Vaishya) were called Dvija ("twice-born") — born once biologically, and "reborn" spiritually through the Upanayana (sacred thread ceremony). Shudras are once-born only.

The Ashrama System (Four Stages of Life)

The varna system was complemented by the Ashrama system — four stages of an ideal Dvija life:

  1. Brahmacharya (student life): Study under a guru; celibacy; ~12–24 years.
  2. Grihastha (householder): Marriage, family, occupation; the most important ashrama.
  3. Vanaprastha (forest retirement): Gradual withdrawal from worldly life; begins when grandchildren are born.
  4. Sannyasa (renunciation): Complete detachment; wandering ascetic; seeking moksha.

The combined Varnashrama Dharma (duty according to one's varna and stage of life) became the central ethical framework of classical Hinduism.

Jati vs Varna

A crucial distinction often confused in MCQs:

  • Varna: The four-fold theoretical classification (Brahman, Kshatriya, Vaishya, Shudra) — a pan-Indian concept.
  • Jati (sub-caste): Thousands of hereditary occupational/endogamous groups within varnas — the real operative unit of caste in practice.
  • The British census (1871 onwards) tried to map jati onto varna — often inaccurately.

Criticisms and Social Reform

  • Buddhism and Jainism (~6th–5th c. BCE) rejected varna hierarchy — both Mahavira and Buddha were Kshatriyas who challenged Brahman supremacy.
  • Bhakti movement (medieval): Saints like Kabir, Ramananda, Tukaram, Chokhamela challenged caste.
  • Maharashtra reformers: Jyotiba Phule (Mahatma Phule) — exposed Brahmanical oppression through Gulamgiri (1873); founded Satyashodhak Samaj (1873). Dr B.R. Ambedkar — led Dalits; burned Manusmriti (1927, Mahad); led conversion to Buddhism (1956). Shahu Maharaj — implemented reservations in Kolhapur state (1902, the first anywhere in India).

Maharashtra Connection

Maharashtra is the epicentre of India's social reform history against caste:

  • Mahatma Phule (1827–1890): Satyashodhak Samaj; Gulamgiri; opened first school for girls and Dalits; challenged Brahman monopoly.
  • B.R. Ambedkar (1891–1956): Born Mahar (Dalit); drafted Indian Constitution; led Buddhist conversion movement; championed abolition of caste.
  • Mahad Satyagraha (1927): Ambedkar led Dalits to drink from the Chavdar Lake (Mahad, Raigad) — first major Dalit assertion in colonial India; burned copies of Manusmriti.
  • Chokhamela (~14th c.): Dalit Varkari sant-poet who challenged caste through devotion to Vitthal; one of the first Dalit literary voices in Marathi.
  • Shahu Maharaj of Kolhapur: Implemented 50% reservation for backward classes in 1902 — decades before the Constitution.

Key Facts

  • Purusha Sukta: Rigveda 10.90 — first reference to four varnas.
  • Dvija: Brahman + Kshatriya + Vaishya (three "twice-born" varnas).
  • Upanayana (sacred thread): denied to Shudras (and women in Later Vedic).
  • Varna ≠ Jati: Varna = 4 theoretical groups; Jati = thousands of sub-castes.
  • Maharashtra reformers: Phule (1873 Satyashodhak Samaj), Ambedkar (1956 Buddhist conversion), Shahu (1902 reservations).
  • Ashrama stages: Brahmacharya, Grihastha, Vanaprastha, Sannyasa.

Exam Traps

  • Purusha Sukta is in Rigveda Book 10, which is a LATER addition — not the oldest part of Rigveda.

  • Dvija = three upper varnas (Brahman, Kshatriya, Vaishya); NOT all four.
  • Shahu Maharaj's 1902 reservations predate the Indian Constitution's affirmative action — often asked in MH-GK context.
