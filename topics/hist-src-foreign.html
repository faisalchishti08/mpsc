Foreign accounts — Greek, Chinese, Arab travellers PREMAINS

Overview

Foreign accounts — by Greek, Chinese, and Arab travellers, diplomats, and scholars — provide an external perspective on Indian history, society, and trade. They often record what Indian texts omit: administrative details, social customs, prices, urban life, and relations with the outside world. MPSC regularly asks on specific travellers, their sponsors, their period, and what they reported.

Megasthenes is the most important Greek source. His Indika (though lost) is preserved in fragments by later writers (Arrian, Strabo, Diodorus). He describes Pataliputra as a city on the Ganges with a palace, a wall with 570 towers, 64 gates, and a system of officers overseeing sanitation, trade, and foreigners. He mentions seven castes (a misunderstanding of Indian varnas/jatis) and a large army.

Limitations of Megasthenes: He never left Pataliputra; reported hearsay; his "seven castes" is a simplification; some statements (e.g., no famine in India) appear exaggerated.

Xuanzang's account is the most historically valuable. He spent ~15 years in India, visited Nalanda (where Silabhadra was the head), Kanauj, Prayag, and South India. He describes Harsha's territory, administration, and the famous Prayag Assembly (Kumbh) where Harsha distributed all his wealth every five years. His account of Maharashtra (Maharastra/Maharashtra) mentions the Chalukyas — one of the few Chinese references to the Deccan.

Al-Biruni is exceptional — a Muslim scholar who accompanied Mahmud of Ghazni but critiqued the destruction objectively; learned Sanskrit to read Vedic and scientific texts directly. His Kitab-ul-Hind is an encyclopaedic study of Indian society, science, and religion — unique for its empirical method.

Ibn Battuta (Moroccan) is notable for his eyewitness account of the Tughluq Sultanate — he served as a qazi (judge) in Delhi under Muhammad bin Tughluq and gives first-hand observations of the Deccan campaigns, forced transfer of the capital (Daulatabad), and the chaotic administration.

Limitations of Foreign Accounts

  1. Language barrier: Most did not know local languages well (except Al-Biruni).
  2. Limited geography: Most accounts cover only the region visited (Megasthenes only knew Pataliputra well).
  3. Cultural misunderstanding: Greek "seven castes," Chinese shock at Hindu practices — observers filtered India through their own cultural lens.
  4. Diplomatic bias: Court visitors reported what rulers wanted heard.
  5. Time gap: Some accounts written years after the visit from memory.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Xuanzang specifically mentions Maharastra (Maharashtra) and the Chalukyan ruler Pulakesi II; he met Pulakesi and described him as ruler of "Maharashtra" — one of the earliest uses of the name.
  • Al-Masudi documents the Rashtrakuta kingdom (Deccan, 8th–10th c. CE) and their capital Manyakheta (Malkhed, Karnataka/Gulbarga region) — directly relevant to Maharashtra's medieval history.

Key Facts

  • Megasthenes: Seleucid ambassador to Chandragupta Maurya's court (~302–298 BCE).
  • Faxian: Visited during Chandragupta II (Gupta), 399–414 CE.
  • Xuanzang: Visited during Harsha, 629–644 CE; account called Da Tang Xiyu Ji.
  • Al-Biruni: 1030 CE; learned Sanskrit; wrote Kitab-ul-Hind.
  • Ibn Battuta: 1333–1342 CE; served under Muhammad bin Tughluq; wrote Rihla.
  • Periplus of the Erythraean Sea (~1st c. CE): Key source for Indo-Roman trade; mentions Barygaza (Bharuch).

Memory Aids

  • Greeks: "He–Ne–Mega–Str–Pli" (Herodotus, Nearchus, Megasthenes, Strabo, Pliny).
  • Chinese: "F–X–Y" = Faxian (Gupta), Xuanzang (Harsha), Yijing (post-Harsha).
  • Arabs: "S–M–Al–B" = Sulaiman, Al-Masudi, Al-Biruni (1030), Ibn Battuta (1333).
  • Xuanzang = "X for Harsha" (both 7th century CE).

Exam Traps

  • Faxian visited during Chandragupta II, not Ashoka or Chandragupta I — don't confuse the Guptas.
  • Al-Biruni came with Mahmud of Ghazni but is NOT a conqueror — he was a scholar; his observations are objective.
  • Periplus of the Erythraean Sea is anonymous — many MCQs try to assign it to Ptolemy or Pliny.
  • Marco Polo was Italian, not Arab — often grouped with Arab travellers in MCQs; correct category is "European".

Mains (descriptive):

  1. [Expected] Critically assess the value and limitations of foreign accounts as sources for ancient and medieval Indian history.
  2. [Expected] Compare the accounts of Faxian and Xuanzang to assess the state of Buddhism in India between the 5th and 7th centuries CE.
  3. [Expected] How does Al-Biruni's 'Kitab-ul-Hind' differ from other medieval accounts of India? What makes it uniquely valuable?
