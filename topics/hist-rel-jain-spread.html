Jain sects (Digambara/Svetambara), literature, art PREMAINS

Overview

After Mahavira's death (~527 BCE), Jainism spread across India through merchant communities and royal patronage. The most consequential development was the split into Digambara and Svetambara sects (~300 BCE) over the question of nudity, canonical texts, and the status of women in achieving moksha. Jainism left a rich legacy of literature, art, and architecture from Gujarat to Karnataka. MPSC asks on the sect differences, Jain contributions to literature (Apabhramsha, Kannada), and art (Dilwara temples, Gomateshwara).

In-Depth

The Great Split — Digambara vs Svetambara

The schism originated at the time of the 1st Jain Council (~300 BCE) in Pataliputra. A severe famine (~12 years) in the Ganges plain caused a group of Jain monks under Bhadrabahu to migrate south to Karnataka. The monks who remained (under Sthulabhadra) are said to have relaxed some rules. When Bhadrabahu's group returned, they could not agree on:

  1. Whether monks should wear white robes (Svetambara = "white-clad") or remain naked (Digambara = "sky-clad/space-clad").
  2. Whether women can attain moksha (Digambara say no; Svetambara say yes — including the 19th Tirthankara Mallinatha being female in Svetambara tradition).
  3. Whether the canonical texts compiled at Pataliputra were authentic (Digambara reject them; Svetambara accept them).

Jain Literature

Jainism made enormous contributions to Indian literature, especially in regional languages:

Sanskrit and Prakrit:

  • Agamas/Angas (Jain canonical texts, Ardhamagadhi Prakrit): 12 Angas, 12 Upangas, etc.
  • Parishishtaparvan (Hemachandra, 12th c.): Jain biography; covers Chandragupta Maurya's conversion.
  • Hemachandra (~1089–1172 CE): Gujarat; polymath; wrote Kumarapalacharita; grammar treatises (Siddha-Hema grammar); worked under Chalukya king Kumarapala. Called "the last scholar of the classical tradition."
  • Apabhramsha literature: Jain scholars were pioneers of Apabhramsha (the transitional language between Prakrits and modern Indian languages). Svayambhu and Pushpadanta wrote in Apabhramsha — considered precursors to Hindi and Marathi literary traditions.

Kannada: Jainism was the dominant religion in early medieval Karnataka; Jain scholars wrote the earliest extant works in Kannada:

  • Pampa (~941 CE): Vikramarjuna Vijaya (Pampa Bharata) — earliest major Kannada epic; Jain monk.
  • Ponna (~950 CE): Shantipurana — Jain Kannada epic.
  • Ranna (~993 CE): Gadayuddha — Kannada epic.

These three are called the "Three Gems" (Ratnatraya) of Kannada literature.

Tamil:

  • Silappadikaram and Manimekalai — Tamil epics with strong Jain and Buddhist influences.
  • Thirukkural (Tiruvalluvar) — widely considered to have Jain ethical influences.

Marathi: Jain scholars contributed to early Apabhramsha which influenced early Marathi. Mukundaraj (~13th c., considered a Jain influence) and Jain merchants' patronage of temple literature influenced Maharashtra's religious culture.

Chandragupta Maurya and Jainism

According to Jain tradition (Hemachandra's Parishishtaparvan), Chandragupta Maurya abdicated his throne (~297 BCE), accepted Jainism under the guidance of Bhadrabahu, migrated south to Shravanabelagola (Karnataka), and died there by Sallekhana (ritual fasting unto death). This is accepted by Jain tradition but not conclusively established in mainstream historical records.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Jain Ellora Caves (30–34): Finest in the Digambara tradition; Cave 32 (Indra Sabha) has a two-storey structure with elaborate Tirthankara images.
  • Jain merchant communities (Sheth, Oswal, Porwal) historically dominated trade in Pune, Mumbai, and Deccan towns.
  • Sallekhana (ritual fasting) controversy reached Maharashtra courts — a 2015 Rajasthan High Court ruling (later reversed) affected Jain communities across India including Maharashtra.

  • Svetambara = white-robed; Gujarat/Rajasthan; Sthulabhadra; women can attain moksha.

  • Hemachandra: Gujarat; 12th c.; Jain polymath; grammar + biographies.

Exam Traps

  • Gomateshwara is in Karnataka (Shravanabelagola) — NOT in Maharashtra, despite Jain connections there.
  • Dilwara is at Mount Abu (Rajasthan) — NOT Gujarat (though Jains built it while linked to Gujarat rulers).
  • Svetambara say 19th Tirthankara Mallinatha was female — Digambara disagree; a common MCQ trap on Jain sect differences.
  • The Mahamastakabhisheka at Shravanabelagola happens every 12 years (not annually).
