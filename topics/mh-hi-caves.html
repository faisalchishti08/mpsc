Rock-cut caves — Ajanta, Ellora, Elephanta, Karla, Bhaja PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

Maharashtra has India's greatest concentration of rock-cut cave architecture: Ajanta (UNESCO WH, Buddhist paintings), Ellora (UNESCO WH, multi-religious), Elephanta (UNESCO WH, Shiva sculptures), and early Hinayana caves at Karla and Bhaja (Pune). Together, these represent over 1,500 years of continuous cave-cutting tradition and are among the world's finest examples of rock-cut art. All are perennial MPSC targets for facts on period, religion, district, and UNESCO status.

Ajanta Caves — Detailed

  • Location: Sahyadri ravine on the Waghora river, Aurangabad district; ~100 km from Aurangabad city.
  • Total caves: 29 caves (numbered 1–29; some erroneously not numbered); hewn into a horseshoe-shaped gorge.
  • Two phases:
  • Phase I (Hinayana, c. 2nd century BCE – 1st century CE): Simpler chaityas (prayer halls) and viharas (monasteries); no figurative paintings; Caves 9, 10, 12, 13, 15A.
  • Phase II (Mahayana, c. 5th–6th century CE, mainly Vakataka period): Elaborate paintings and sculptures; famous jataka tales paintings; Caves 1, 2, 16, 17, 19, 26 are most celebrated.
  • Rediscovery: The caves were forgotten for centuries; "rediscovered" by British officer John Smith during a tiger hunt in April 1819.
  • Paintings: Tempera-like technique (not fresco — the paint is applied on dry surface); pigments from mineral, plant, and animal sources; subjects include Bodhisattvas, Jataka stories, court scenes — a window into ancient Indian life.
  • Cave 1: Bodhisattva Padmapani — considered the greatest painting in Ajanta.
  • Cave 26: Giant Mahaparinirvana (reclining Buddha, ~9m long) sculpture.

Ellora Caves — Detailed

  • Location: 30 km from Aurangabad city; cut into the Charanandri hills (Deccan Trap basalt).
  • Total caves: 34 caves — Cave 1–12 Buddhist (6th–8th century), Cave 13–29 Hindu (7th–11th century), Cave 30–34 Jain (8th–12th century).
  • Kailasa Temple (Cave 16):
  • Carved under Rashtrakuta king Krishna I (c. 756–773 CE).
  • Single monolithic rock carved top-down; larger than the Parthenon in Athens; dedicated to Shiva.
  • Entire temple carved by removing 200,000 tonnes of rock — an engineering feat without parallel.
  • Area: ~84m long × 47m wide; tower (shikhara) ~30m high.
  • Cave 10 (Buddhist): Vishwakarma Cave — largest Buddhist chaitya at Ellora; 7th century.
  • Cave 32 (Jain): Indra Sabha — finest Jain cave; elaborate carvings.

Elephanta Caves — Detailed

  • Location: Gharapuri Island in Mumbai harbour; 10 km from Mumbai Gateway of India; accessible only by boat.
  • 7 caves + 2 smaller; main cave (Cave 1 = "Great Cave") is the most important.
  • Trimurti of Shiva (Maheshmurti): The iconic 6-metre-tall triple-headed (Trimurti) sculpture of Shiva at the centre of Cave 1 — one of the greatest sculptures in world art. The three faces represent Shiva as creator (Brahma-like, benign), preserver (serene), and destroyer (Aghori/Bhairava, fierce).
  • Period: Mainly 5th–8th century CE; attributed to Kalachuri dynasty or Chalukya-era patronage.
  • UNESCO WH: 1987.
  • Portuguese damage: Portuguese colonists (16th–17th century) used Elephanta for target practice; some sculptures bear damage.
  • The island's name "Elephanta" is Portuguese — from a large stone elephant statue found here (now at Dr. Bhau Daji Lad Museum, Mumbai).

Karla and Bhaja Caves (Non-UNESCO but important)

  • Karla Caves (Pune district, Lonavala area): Great Chaitya (Cave 8) — largest surviving Hinayana chaitya griha in India; c. 1st–2nd century BCE; wooden ribs still survive inside the arched roof. Near the Bhor Ghat.
  • Bhaja Caves (Pune district, near Lonavala): 22 caves; 2nd century BCE; notable for early Buddhist rock-cut viharas and Hinayana art; some of the earliest such caves in India.
  • Also: Pitalkhora Caves (Aurangabad, c. 3rd–2nd century BCE) — among the earliest rock-cut caves in the Deccan.

Maharashtra Connection

Ajanta and Ellora are Maharashtra's top two UNESCO World Heritage Sites and the state's most important heritage tourism assets (UNESCO 1983). The Maharashtra Tourism Development Corporation (MTDC) manages tours to Aurangabad's heritage cluster. Elephanta is Mumbai's only UNESCO WH site. The Ajanta-Ellora region anchors Aurangabad (Chhatrapati Sambhajinagar) as the "Tourism Capital of Maharashtra".

Key Facts

  • Ajanta: 29 caves, Aurangabad; UNESCO 1983; Buddhist; rediscovered by John Smith, April 1819
  • Greatest painting: Bodhisattva Padmapani (Cave 1); largest sculpture: Mahaparinirvana (Cave 26)
  • Ellora: 34 caves (Buddhist 12 + Hindu 17 + Jain 5 = 34); UNESCO 1983
  • Kailasa Temple (Ellora Cave 16): Rashtrakuta King Krishna I (~756 CE); largest monolithic rock structure
  • Elephanta: Gharapuri Island, Mumbai harbour; Trimurti/Maheshmurti Shiva (6m); UNESCO 1987
  • Karla Caves (Pune): Largest surviving Hinayana Chaitya (c. 1st–2nd BCE)
  • Bhaja Caves (Pune): 2nd century BCE; near Karla

Exam Traps

  • Ajanta UNESCO: 1983 (same year as Ellora); Elephanta: 1987 — do NOT say all three were designated together.
  • Kailasa Temple is at Ellora (Cave 16), NOT Ajanta — people confuse the two sites.
  • Ajanta paintings are NOT frescoes — they are tempera/distemper applied on dry surface (not wet plaster).
