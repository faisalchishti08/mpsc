Mauryan art and architecture PREMAINS

Overview

Mauryan art and architecture (322–185 BCE) represents India's first great phase of state-sponsored, durable stone construction. It encompasses rock-cut caves, monolithic pillars, stupas, and palace architecture, with Ashoka (268–232 BCE) as the principal patron. Mauryan art is studied in MPSC for its stylistic features (polish, naturalism), specific monuments, and its link to the spread of Buddhism — a perennial Prelims MCQ source.

In-Depth

The "Mauryan Polish" and Royal Patronage

The defining technical hallmark of Mauryan sculpture is the extraordinary mirror-like polish on sandstone, achieved by a lost technique involving mineral polishes. This polish was applied to pillars, capitals, and cave walls (Barabar Caves). It signals royal workshops with centralised craft skills. The material is predominantly Chunar sandstone (near Varanasi), transported across the empire.

Ashokan Pillars

Ashoka erected ~30 free-standing monolithic sandstone pillars (stambhas) inscribed with edicts across the empire — from Topra (Haryana) to Rampurva (Bihar) to Lumbini (Nepal). Key features:

  • Shafts are slightly tapering, highly polished, un-fluted monoliths (unlike Greek columns).
  • Each capital sits atop an abacus (circular/square) with animal/lotus motifs, then a bell/lotus capital, and the topmost animal capital.
  • Sarnath Pillar capital (near Varanasi): four back-to-back lions on a circular abacus bearing the Dhammachakra wheel, standing on a lotus bell. Adopted as National Emblem of India (1950). The abacus shows an elephant, horse, bull, and lion separated by chakras.
  • Vaishali Pillar (Bihar): single lion capital, marks the site of Licchavi kingdom.
  • Rampurva Bull capital and Rampurva Lion capital are in Rashtrapati Bhavan today.
  • Lauriya Nandangarh pillar (Bihar): best preserved single-shaft example.

Rock-Cut Architecture — Barabar and Nagarjuni Caves

Mauryan rock-cut caves are the earliest in India:

  • Barabar Caves (Gaya district, Bihar): four caves — Sudama, Lomas Rishi, Vishwakarma (Karan Chaupar), and Visvakarman — donated by Ashoka to the Ajivika sect (~250 BCE).
  • Lomas Rishi Cave has the oldest known chaitya-arch facade in stone, carved in imitation of wooden architecture (horse-shoe arch, beam cross-pieces).
  • Nagarjuni Caves (nearby): donated by Ashoka's grandson Dasharatha to Ajivikas.
  • Interior walls have the typical Mauryan polish.

Stupas

Ashoka is credited with building 84,000 stupas across the empire (traditional count). Key surviving examples:

  • Sanchi Stupa 1 (Madhya Pradesh): original core is Ashokan brick stupa, later enlarged in Shunga period with stone facing and the famous four toranas (gateways).
  • Bharhut (MP): Ashokan in origin; known for narrative relief panels.
  • Piprahwa stupa (UP): excavated reliquary with Buddha's relics.
  • Taxila stupas (Pakistan): northwestern extent.

Structure: hemispherical anda (mound) on a circular drum (medhi), with a harmika (square railing) at top, yashti (mast), chatravali (umbrella tier), vedika (railing), and torana (gateway).

Sculpture — Yaksha/Yakshi Tradition

Free-standing sculpture predates the pillars and shows a different tradition:

  • Parkham Yaksha (near Mathura) — 2.59 m, massive frontal pose.
  • Didarganj Yakshi (Patna Museum) — woman holding a fly-whisk (chamara); brilliant polish; one of the finest Mauryan sculptures.
  • These yakshas/yakshis were local fertility deities absorbed into Mauryan art.

Palace Architecture — Pataliputra

Megasthenes described Pataliputra's royal palace as rivalling Persepolis and Susa. Excavations at Kumhrar (Patna) revealed an 80-pillar assembly hall (hypostyle hall, 10×8 columns) with polished sandstone columns — one of the few structural remnants. Also found: a wooden palisade mentioned by Megasthenes, and a monastic complex.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. The nearest Mauryan administrative presence in the Deccan is attested by Ashoka's Rock Edict XIII and minor edicts at Sopara (Nala Sopara, Palghar district, Maharashtra) — one of the southernmost Ashokan inscriptions. The Barabar Cave model of rock-cut architecture directly inspired the Western Ghats cave tradition (Bhaja, Karla, Ajanta), which flourished under the Satavahanas in Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • National Emblem source: Sarnath Pillar Capital (Ashokan).
  • Barabar Caves donated to: Ajivikas by Ashoka (~250 BCE).
  • Nagarjuni Caves: donated by Dasharatha (Ashoka's grandson).
  • Material: Chunar sandstone; technique: mirror-like Mauryan polish.
  • Kumhrar: 80-pillar assembly hall excavated.
  • Didarganj Yakshi: now in Patna Museum.
  • Sopara (Maharashtra): has an Ashokan rock edict.
  • Sanchi Stupa 1 core: Ashokan; later enlargement: Shunga period.

Exam Traps

  • The four toranas at Sanchi were built during the Shunga period, not by Ashoka himself — the stupa core is Ashokan, the gateways are not.
  • Lomas Rishi Cave is not a Buddhist cave — it was given to Ajivikas.
  • The Sarnath capital has four lions, not three — the fourth is hidden at the back; the flag/emblem shows only three from the front.
  • Pillars were erected by Ashoka; stambha inscriptions on some pillars are by Ashoka, but the pillars at Lauriya, Vaishali etc. existed as territorial markers.
