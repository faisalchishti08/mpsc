Biosphere and conservation areas (Western Ghats) PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

Maharashtra contains portions of two UNESCO-recognised Biosphere Reserves: the Sahyadri-Konkan Cluster (part of the Western Ghats UNESCO World Heritage Site, 2012) and the Deccan Plateau fringes. The Western Ghats as a whole is both a Biosphere Reserve network and one of 36 global biodiversity hotspots. MPSC tests biosphere reserve names, their districts, UNESCO status, and the Kaas Plateau special inclusion.

Western Ghats — Maharashtra's Biodiversity Hotspot

The Western Ghats (Sahyadri) is one of the world's 8 biodiversity hotspots (as designated by Conservation International). Of 39 sub-clusters nominated for UNESCO World Heritage in 2012, 7 are in Maharashtra:

  1. Kaas Plateau (Satara) — "Valley of Flowers of Maharashtra"; UNESCO WH 2012; annual wildflower bloom September; endemic orchids, Ceropegia species.
  2. Koyna Wildlife Sanctuary (Satara) — core of Sahyadri Tiger Reserve; high Western Ghats biodiversity.
  3. Chandoli National Park (Sangli-Satara-Kolhapur) — Warna river; raptor diversity.
  4. Radhanagari Wildlife Sanctuary (Kolhapur) — Gaur sanctuary; moist deciduous forests.
  5. Bhimashankar Wildlife Sanctuary (Pune) — Shekru habitat; Bhima river source.
  6. Phansad Wildlife Sanctuary (Raigad) — coastal Sahyadri.
  7. Tamhini (Tamini) Ghat area (Pune) — not formally a NP but part of WH cluster.

Kaas Plateau — Focus

  • Location: Satara district, ~25 km from Satara city; altitude ~1,200 m.
  • UNESCO WH: Part of the Western Ghats serial nomination (2012).
  • Wildflower bloom: Blooms spectacularly during September monsoon withdrawal — thousands of wildflower species including endemic Karvi (Strobilanthes callosus, blooms once every 7–8 years), Smithia, Ceropegia, and rare orchids.
  • Management: Maharashtra Forest Department restricts visitor numbers to prevent trampling; best time September.
  • Nearby: Vajrai and Thoseghar waterfalls (Satara) — part of the same highland circuit.

Melghat Biosphere Reserve

  • Declared: 1985 (India's 2nd biosphere reserve, after Nilgiri 1986 — some sources say India's first is Nilgiri 1986; Melghat 1985 is often cited as one of the earliest).
  • Actually: India's biosphere reserves were established from 1986 onwards following UNESCO's MAB programme. The correct sequence: Nilgiri (1986) is India's first; subsequent ones include Nanda Devi, Nokrek, etc. Melghat as a biosphere reserve designation — check exact year; it is included in the national biosphere reserve network.
  • Scope: Covers Melghat Tiger Reserve + buffer zone; Tapi river; Korku tribal population.
  • Ecosystem services: Carbon sequestration, Tapi river watershed, biodiversity corridor.

Conservation Challenges in Western Ghats

  • Infrastructure: Dams (Koyna, Warna, Radhanagari), roads, and railways cut through the WH zone.
  • Habitat fragmentation: Plantations (eucalyptus, teak) replacing native species; invasive Lantana camara spread.
  • Climate change: Shifts in monsoon patterns affecting wildflower bloom timing at Kaas.
  • Ecotourism pressure: Kaas Plateau visitor surge post-2012 UNESCO designation — Maharashtra restricted visitors to protect flora.

Maharashtra Connection

Maharashtra is home to the largest share of the Western Ghats UNESCO World Heritage sites (7 of 39 sub-clusters). The Kaas Plateau is a direct MH ecology icon for MPSC — its annual wildflower bloom, UNESCO status, and location in Satara district are tested together. The Gadchiroli forest (eastern Vidarbha) is not a biosphere reserve but is a proposed biodiversity conservation area under the Forest Rights Act (FRA 2006) — linking tribal rights to conservation.
