Buddhist literature and spread PREMAINS

Overview

Buddhist literature spans Pali canonical texts (Tripitaka), Sanskrit Mahayana sutras, Chinese and Tibetan translations, and Indian regional literature. It is not only religious but also a primary historical source for ancient Indian social life, trade, politics, and philosophy. MPSC asks on specific texts — their category (Pitaka), language, content, and historical value — and on the spread of Buddhism to Asia.

Therigatha — Women's Voices

The Therigatha ("Verses of the Elder Nuns") is a remarkable collection of Pali poetry by bhikkunis (Buddhist nuns) — some of the oldest surviving women's literature in the world. Written by the first Buddhist nuns (ordained by the Buddha himself), it includes voices of women from all social backgrounds (courtesans, queens, farmers' wives) who found liberation in the Buddhist path. Significant for women's history.

Buddhism in India — Rise and Decline

Rise: Royal patronage (Ashoka most decisive); merchant community support; egalitarian social message.

Decline factors in India:

  1. Brahmanical revival (Gupta period onwards) — Hindu bhakti, Shaivism, Vaishnavism absorbed Buddhist ideas and popular support.
  2. Loss of royal patronage — Gupta kings were Vaishnavas; later dynasties (Pratiharas, Rashtrakutas) were Hindu.
  3. Corruption within monasteries — Vajrayana became esoteric and divorced from lay society.
  4. Destruction of monasteries — Nalanda, Vikramashila burned by Bakhtiyar Khilji (~1193 CE); monks fled to Nepal and Tibet.
  5. Absorption into Hinduism — Buddha accepted as the 9th avatar of Vishnu; Buddhist practices absorbed.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Ajanta murals (UNESCO WHS) depict Jataka tales extensively — the largest visual Jataka narrative in the world, painted during 5th–6th c. CE (Vakataka period).
  • Karla Chaitya (~2nd c. BCE): Largest extant rock-cut Buddhist prayer hall; merchants' inscriptions inside show Buddhist patronage by traders.
  • Kanheri Caves (Mumbai): Rich Buddhist manuscript/inscriptions; major library and study centre in ancient times.
  • Ambedkar's Navayana (neo-Buddhism, 1956): Based on Pali texts directly; emphasised the Dhammapada and early suttas; explicitly rejected Brahmanical distortions; Maharashtra has the largest Buddhist population in India (~5.8%, mostly Ambedkarite).

Key Facts

  • Tripitaka: Vinaya + Sutta + Abhidhamma Pitakas (Theravada Pali canon).
  • Dhammapada: 423 verses; Khuddaka Nikaya; most quoted Buddhist text.
  • Jatakas: 547 birth stories; historical source for early Indian society.
  • Mahinda: Ashoka's son; took Buddhism to Sri Lanka (~250 BCE).
  • Buddhacharita: Ashvaghosha (~1st c. CE); Sanskrit Buddhist poetry.
  • Nalanda destroyed: Bakhtiyar Khilji (~1193 CE).
  • Therigatha: Oldest surviving women's poetry collection in the world.

Exam Traps

  • Jatakas are in the Sutta Pitaka (Khuddaka Nikaya), NOT the Vinaya or Abhidhamma.
  • Ashvaghosha (Buddhacharita) was a Kushana-era Sanskrit poet, NOT a Pali writer.
  • Buddhism declined in India but DID NOT disappear globally — it flourished abroad.
  • Mahinda took Theravada to Sri Lanka; Xuanzang brought Mahayana texts from India to China (direction matters).
