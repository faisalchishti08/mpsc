Decline of the Mughal empire PREMAINS

Overview

The decline of the Mughal Empire (1707–1858) was a complex process with multiple overlapping causes — weak successors, Maratha rise, jagir crisis, regional revolts, Afghan invasions, and eventually European dominance. After Aurangzeb's death (1707), twelve emperors ruled in 50 years; by 1803 the Emperor was a British pensioner. MPSC asks the causes, the role of Marathas/Nadir Shah, and the British takeover context.

In-Depth

Immediate Causes — Aurangzeb's Legacy

The process began in Aurangzeb's own reign:

  1. Deccan blunder: 25 years + massive army pinned in the Deccan; treasury drained; Delhi neglected.
  2. Jagir crisis: number of mansabdars exceeded available jagirs; officials resorted to oppression to extract revenue.
  3. Rajput alienation: Aurangzeb's Marwar annexation attempt and Mewar conflict estranged key military allies.
  4. Religious polarisation: jizya reinstatement, temple demolitions → Sikh, Jat, Rajput, Maratha revolts.
  5. Succession wars: each emperor's death triggered violent succession wars that weakened central authority.

The Sayyid Brothers (1713–1720)

Sayyid Abdullah Khan and Sayyid Husain Ali Khan — two brothers who became "king-makers", installing and deposing emperors at will (1713–1720). They deposed and blinded Farrukhsiyar (1719) with Maratha support; installed three puppet emperors in one year (1719). Finally overthrown by Muhammad Shah with Nizam-ul-Mulk's help (1720).

Nadir Shah's Invasion (1739)

Nadir Shah (Afsharid Persian ruler) invaded India and defeated Muhammad Shah at the Battle of Karnal (1739), then massacred 20,000–30,000 Delhiites in the Qatl-i-Aam of Delhi. He looted:

  • Peacock Throne (Takht-e-Taus)
  • Koh-i-Noor diamond
  • Daria-i-Noor diamond
  • Shah of Persia diamond

Total loot estimated at ₹70 crore (contemporary value). The massacre and looting exposed Mughal military collapse. Muhammad Shah was humiliated but retained his throne after paying tribute.

Ahmad Shah Abdali (Durrani) Invasions (1748–1767)

Ahmad Shah Abdali invaded India seven times between 1748 and 1767, looting Punjab, Delhi and other regions. The decisive blow: Third Battle of Panipat (1761) — Abdali's Afghan forces defeated the Maratha confederacy under Viswasrao and Bhau Saheb (Peshwa Vishwasrao and Sadashivrao Bhau) — dealing a mortal blow to Maratha attempts to control north India. (See hist-mar-panipat.)

Rise of Regional Powers

Mughal governors asserted independence:

  • Nizam-ul-Mulk founded the Hyderabad state (1724) — Asaf Jahi dynasty; acknowledged Mughal suzerainty nominally.
  • Sa'adat Khan (Burhan-ul-Mulk) founded the Awadh Nawabate (1722).
  • Murshid Quli Khan in Bengal (1717) — made Bengal virtually independent.
  • Marathas under the Peshwas expanded across north India (1740s–1760s) — controlled Delhi, collected chauth from much of India.

Role of the British East India Company

  • Farrukhsiyar Farman (1717): gave British EIC duty-free trade in Bengal — a pivotal commercial concession.
  • Battle of Plassey (1757): British defeated Nawab Siraj-ud-Daulah (Bengal) — beginning territorial empire.
  • Battle of Buxar (1764): British defeated combined forces of Nawab Mir Qasim, Awadh Nawab Shuja-ud-Daulah, and Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II — Treaty of Allahabad (1765) followed: Shah Alam granted British the Diwani (revenue rights) of Bengal.
  • By 1803, British general Lake captured Delhi; Shah Alam II became a British pensioner.

Structural/Long-Term Causes

  • Lack of primogeniture: no fixed succession rule → perpetual civil wars.
  • Decentralisation of mansabdari: mansabs became increasingly hereditary; central discipline collapsed.
  • Economic decline: Deccan wars drained wealth; trade disrupted; currency debasement.
  • No naval power: allowed European maritime dominance.

Maharashtra Connection

The Maratha role in the decline is central to Maharashtra history:

  • Marathas collected chauth from Mughal territories across India.
  • Peshwa Bajirao I raided Delhi (1737) — stunning demonstration of Mughal impotence.
  • The Third Battle of Panipat (1761) ended Maratha hopes of succeeding the Mughals as pan-India rulers; Abdali's victory benefited eventually only the British.
  • Nizam-ul-Mulk's Hyderabad had extended authority into Maharashtra (Aurangabad, Marathwada region).

Key Facts

  • Nadir Shah's invasion: 1739; Battle of Karnal; looted Peacock Throne and Koh-i-Noor.
  • Sayyid Brothers overthrown by Muhammad Shah (1720).
  • Third Battle of Panipat: 1761 — Abdali vs Marathas.
  • Farrukhsiyar Farman: 1717 — British EIC duty-free trade in Bengal.
  • Battle of Buxar: 1764 — Shah Alam II signed Treaty of Allahabad (1765).
  • Hyderabad Nizam founded: 1724, Nizam-ul-Mulk.
  • Last Mughal emperor: Bahadur Shah Zafar II (1837–1858), exiled to Rangoon.

Exam Traps

  • Nadir Shah (1739) ≠ Ahmad Shah Abdali/Durrani — different invaders; Nadir Shah came from Persia, Abdali from Afghanistan.
  • Farrukhsiyar Farman (1717) gave British trading concessions — not the Charter Acts which are later (British Parliament legislation).
  • Third Panipat (1761) was Marathas vs Abdali, NOT Marathas vs Mughals; the Mughal emperor was irrelevant by then.
  • Koh-i-Noor looted by Nadir Shah (1739), not Abdali — a common exam confusion.
