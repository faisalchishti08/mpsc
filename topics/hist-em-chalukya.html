Chalukyas of Badami, Kalyani PREMAINS

Overview

The Chalukyas were a major Deccan dynasty in two distinct periods: Chalukyas of Badami (Vatapi) (c. 543–753 CE) who ruled from Badami (Karnataka) and dominated the Deccan, and their successors the Later Chalukyas of Kalyani (Western Chalukyas) (973–1189 CE) who revived Chalukya power after overthrowing the Rashtrakutas. The Badami Chalukyas are remembered for defeating Harsha at the Narmada (Pulakesi II), creating the Aihole and Pattadakal temple complexes, and their rivalry with the Pallavas. MPSC tests both branches for their rulers, monuments, and administrative features.

Pulakesi II — the Greatest Badami Chalukya

Pulakesi II (c. 609–642 CE) is the most celebrated early Chalukya:

  • Stopped Harsha at the Narmada (c. 618–619 CE) — commemorated in the Aihole Inscription composed by court poet Ravikirti (a prasasti that is one of the finest early Sanskrit-Kannada literary documents).
  • Received the embassy of the Sassanid Persian king Khusrau II — an extraordinary diplomatic contact; commemorated in Ajanta Cave 1's "embassy scene" painting (a Persian ambassador with characteristic dress).
  • Sent an Indian embassy to Persia — noted in Sassanid court records.
  • Fought the Pallavas — initially victorious, but Pallava king Narasimhavarman I (Mamalla) counterattacked, sacked Badami (Vatapi) in c. 642 CE and killed Pulakesi II. Hence the Pallava title Vatapikonda ("Conqueror of Vatapi").
  • Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang visited the Chalukya kingdom during Pulakesi II's reign and left a description.

Art and Architecture — Badami, Aihole, Pattadakal

The early Chalukyas created a remarkable architectural tradition in the Malaprabha river valley (northern Karnataka, near Maharashtra border):

Badami Caves (4 rock-cut caves, c. 570–578 CE):

  • Cave 1: Shiva; Cave 2: Vishnu; Cave 3: Vishnu (largest, dated 578 CE with inscription); Cave 4: Jain.
  • Cave 3 has the famous image of Vishnu Trivikrama (three strides) and Narayana reclining on Shesha — early Chalukya sculptural style, distinct from Pallava.

Aihole (~125 temples, 6th–8th century CE) — described as the "cradle of Indian temple architecture":

  • Ladkhan Temple: earliest Chalukya temple; unusual flat-roofed style.
  • Durga Temple: apsidal plan (semi-circular end) — unusual hybrid of Buddhist chaitya arch and Hindu temple.
  • Meguti Jain Temple (634 CE): has a Sanskrit-Kannada inscription (the Aihole inscription of Ravikirti).

Pattadakal — UNESCO World Heritage Site (1987):

  • 10 major temples from the 7th–8th century; experiments with both Nagara and Dravidian styles.
  • Virupaksha Temple (c. 740 CE): built by Lokamahadevi (queen of Vikramaditya II) to celebrate his victories over the Pallavas; modelled on the Kanchipuram Kailasanatha Temple.
  • Papanatha Temple: Nagara style with Dravidian elements.
  • Pattadakal is called the "playground for Chalukya architectural experiments."

Later Chalukyas of Kalyani (Western Chalukyas, 973–1189 CE)

Tailapa II (Vikramaditya/Taila II) overthrew the last Rashtrakuta (Indra IV) in 973 CE and revived the Chalukya line:

  • Capital: Kalyani (Basavakalyan, Karnataka) — near Maharashtra.
  • Greatest ruler: Vikramaditya VI (Vikramanka) (r. 1076–1126 CE) — patronised Bilhana (wrote Vikramankadevacharita) and Vijnaneshvara (wrote Mitakshara — authoritative digest of Hindu law on inheritance, still cited in Indian courts).
  • Mitakshara system of inheritance (joint family, survivorship rights) prevails in most of India today (vs. Dayabhaga system in Bengal/Assam).

Maharashtra Connection

Moderate-high for Maharashtra. The Chalukya territory covered northern Karnataka and portions of present-day Maharashtra (Marathwada, Vidarbha). The Aihole/Pattadakal/Badami triangle is in Karnataka but very close to the Maharashtra border. The Later Chalukyas of Kalyani with their capital at Basavakalyan (Bidar district) directly bordered Maharashtra. The Mitakshara legal system (Vijnaneshvara's codification under Vikramaditya VI) governs Hindu succession across Maharashtra and most of India today.

Key Facts

  • Pulakesi II: stopped Harsha; Aihole inscription by Ravikirti.
  • Badami Caves: 4 caves (3 Vaishnava/Shaiva + 1 Jain); Cave 3 dated 578 CE.
  • Pattadakal: UNESCO WHS 1987; 10 temples; Virupaksha Temple c. 740 CE.
  • Pallavas sacked Badami: Narasimhavarman I (Vatapikonda, c. 642 CE).
  • Later Chalukya of Kalyani founded: Tailapa II (973 CE).
  • Vikramaditya VI: patronised Vijnaneshvara (Mitakshara) and Bilhana.
  • Mitakshara: Hindu inheritance law system; prevails across most of India.

Exam Traps

  • Pulakesi II stopped Harsha — do NOT say Pulakesi I.
  • The Aihole Inscription praising the Narmada victory was written by Ravikirti (Chalukya poet), NOT by Bana (who wrote Harsha's biography for the Harsha side).
  • Pattadakal is UNESCO WHS 1987; Ellora and Ajanta are 1983 — different years.
  • Mitakshara (inheritance/succession law) was codified under Later Chalukyas of Kalyani (Vikramaditya VI), not the Badami Chalukyas.
