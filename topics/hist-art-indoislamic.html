Indo-Islamic and colonial architecture PREMAINS

Overview

Indo-Islamic architecture (covered in its Sultanate phase under hist-ds-art) continued evolving through Mughal, Deccan Sultanate, and Provincial styles before the colonial (Indo-Saracenic/Victorian) phase introduced European idioms adapted to Indian conditions. This file covers the later phases — Mughal zenith, Deccan Sultanate distinctives, and British colonial architecture — completing the arc from 1526 to 1947. MPSC asks about Mughal monuments, Deccan Sultanate buildings, and colonial-era landmarks.

In-Depth

Mughal Architecture (1526–1707) — Recapitulation and Depth

Mughal architecture evolved through three clear phases:

PhaseRulerCharacteristicKey Monument
Early — Red StoneAkbar (1556–1605)Red sandstone; Hindu-Islamic blend; massiveFatehpur Sikri, Agra Fort, Humayun's Tomb
TransitionalJahangir (1605–27)Persian gardens; naturalism in decorationShalimar Bagh (Kashmir), Nur Jahan's tomb (Lahore)
Zenith — White MarbleShah Jahan (1628–58)White marble; pietra dura; charbaghTaj Mahal, Red Fort Delhi, Jama Masjid
DeclineAurangzeb (1658–1707)Smaller, less ornate; Deccan-influencedBibi Ka Maqbara (Aurangabad), Badshahi Mosque (Lahore)

Charbagh Garden Design: Persian four-part garden divided by water channels; water = life; design used at Humayun's Tomb, Taj Mahal, Shalimar Bagh. The Taj's charbagh garden is the most celebrated example.

Pietra Dura (Parchin-kari): Semi-precious stone inlay in white marble — lapis lazuli, cornelian, turquoise, jade. Perfected at the Taj Mahal under Shah Jahan.

Deccan Sultanate Architecture (1490–1687)

After the Bahmani breakup, five Deccan Sultanates developed distinct regional styles:

Bijapur (Adil Shahi):

  • Gol Gumbaz (1656): Tomb of Muhammad Adil Shah; largest pre-modern dome in India — diameter 44 m; the "Whispering Gallery" runs along the inner base of the dome (a whisper travels 37 m across). Architect: Yaqut of Dabul.
  • Ibrahim Rauza: tomb of Ibrahim Adil Shah II; elegant proportions; minarets that influenced Taj Mahal design (debated).
  • Jama Masjid, Bijapur: one of the finest mosques of the Deccan.

Golkonda (Qutb Shahi, Hyderabad):

  • Qutb Shahi Tombs (Hyderabad): cluster of 7 tombs with Persian-Deccani style; bulbous domes; recently restored.
  • Golkonda Fort: massive fortification with acoustic warning system (hand-claps at the gate can be heard at the top).

Bidar (Barid Shahi):

  • Madrasa of Mahmud Gawan (Bidar, 1472): Persian tile-work (kashi tilework); three-storey facade; one minaret still stands.

Colonial Architecture in India (1757–1947)

British rule introduced European architectural idioms, eventually blending with Indian traditions:

Gothic Revival: Adopted for Bombay (Mumbai) colonial buildings in the 19th century:

  • Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Terminus (CST, formerly Victoria Terminus, 1887): Designed by Frederick William Stevens; High Victorian Gothic; UNESCO WHS (2004). The most ornate colonial building in India.
  • Bombay High Court (1878): Gothic; figure of Justice (without blindfold — she can "see").
  • Rajabai Clock Tower (1878): 85 m; modelled on Big Ben; part of Mumbai University campus.
  • Bombay Municipal Corporation (BMC) building (1893): Gothic-Baroque dome.

Indo-Saracenic / Mughal-Gothic Synthesis:

  • Gateway of India (1924, Mumbai): Arch in the Indo-Saracenic style (blending Mughal/Gujarati Gothic); built for King George V's visit (1911); constructed 1924.
  • Secretariat Building, Mumbai: Indo-Saracenic.
  • Mysore Palace (1912): Indo-Saracenic; architect Henry Irwin; three domes; lights up for Dasara.
  • Victoria Memorial (Kolkata, 1921): Italian Renaissance + Mughal elements; white Makrana marble.
  • India Gate (New Delhi, 1931): Triumphal arch; War Memorial; designed by Edwin Lutyens.
  • Rashtrapati Bhavan (New Delhi, 1929): designed by Edwin Lutyens with Herbert Baker; blends classical European with Mughal/Indian motifs; Jaipur Column in forecourt.

New Delhi Planning (Lutyens' Delhi): Laid out by Edwin Lutyens and Herbert Baker (1912–1931) as the new capital of British India; radial avenues, bungalow zones, Parliament building, Rashtrapati Bhavan, Secretariat blocks.

Maharashtra Connection

Colonial architecture is centred on Mumbai (Bombay):

  • CST (formerly VT) — UNESCO WHS (2004) — the greatest Victorian Gothic building in Asia; a daily-used Mumbai landmark.
  • High Court, Rajabai Tower, BMC Building, University of Mumbai — the Fort area ensemble of Gothic buildings is a UNESCO nomination (Victorian Gothic and Art Deco Ensembles of Mumbai, WHS 2018).
  • Gateway of India — the most iconic Mumbai landmark.
  • Bibi Ka Maqbara (Aurangabad, 1660) — Deccan Mughal in Maharashtra.
  • Gol Gumbaz (Bijapur) — technically Karnataka but historically part of the Deccan Sultanate sphere that dominated Maharashtra.

Key Facts

  • Gol Gumbaz, Bijapur (1656): dome diameter 44 m; Whispering Gallery; architect Yaqut of Dabul.
  • CST (Victoria Terminus, 1887): architect F.W. Stevens; Gothic; UNESCO WHS 2004.

  • Bibi Ka Maqbara (1660): Aurangabad (Maharashtra); built by Azam Shah for his mother Dilras Banu Begum.

Exam Traps

  • Gol Gumbaz (Bijapur) ≠ Bibi Ka Maqbara (Aurangabad) — both are Deccan/Mughal domes but different locations; Gol Gumbaz is in Karnataka (Bijapur), Bibi Ka Maqbara is in Maharashtra.
  • Gateway of India was built for George V's visit in 1911 but completed and inaugurated in 1924 — both years appear in questions.
  • CST (formerly VT) became UNESCO WHS in 2004 as part of "Victorian and Art Deco Ensembles of Bombay" (2018 designation covers the broader ensemble).
  • Victoria Memorial (Kolkata) ≠ Victoria Terminus (Mumbai) — two very different Victorian-era buildings.
