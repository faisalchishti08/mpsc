Folk arts — Lavani, Tamasha, Powada, Koli dance PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

Maharashtra has a rich tradition of folk and performing arts — Lavani (erotic-devotional dance-song), Tamasha (folk theatre), Powada (ballad-epic), Kirtan (devotional narration), Gondhal (goddess worship performance), Koli Dance, and Lezim (group exercise-dance). These forms are deeply linked to Maharashtra's caste communities, historical memory, and social life. Several have received Sangeet Natak Akademi recognition and GI tag consideration. MPSC tests the community of origin, performing context, and key performers.

Shahir Tradition — Social Reform Through Folk Art

The Shahir (poet-singer, from Persian "sha'ir" = poet) tradition in Maharashtra links folk arts to social movements:

  • Shahir Annabhau Sathe (1920–1969): Mang community; revolutionary folk poet-singer; Loknatya (people's theatre); wrote Powadas on Shivaji, social protest; Padma Shri posthumously (2020 announcement); associated with Communist Party; "Jagtachi Navtee" (world won't remain as it is).
  • Shahir Amar Sheikh (1916–1969): Pasi community; revolutionary shahir; Lal Bawta (Red Flag) cultural front.
  • Shahir Sable: Famous for Samyukta Maharashtra movement Powadas; "Ek Marathi" songs during agitation.

Warli Painting — Tribal Art from Maharashtra

  • Warli painting: Traditional art of the Warli tribe (Palghar-Nashik region, Western Maharashtra); geometric forms — triangles (human body), circles, squares; depicts community life, nature, wedding rituals (Chauk motif); white pigment on mud walls; Jivya Soma Mase popularized Warli internationally from 1970s; now commercialised on fabric, paper.
  • Padma Shri Jivya Soma Mase (1945–2018): Warli artist; brought Warli to global recognition.

Maharashtra Connection

Lavani and Tamasha are closely linked to Maharashtra's caste politics — artists are often from Dalit/nomadic communities who historically had no land rights or temple access. The Shahir tradition was central to the Samyukta Maharashtra movement (1956) and Dalit movement. Tilak's use of Lezim in Ganesh Utsav is a case of folk arts for political mobilization.
