Jahangir and Shah Jahan PREMAINS

Overview

Jahangir (1569–1627, r. 1605–1627) and Shah Jahan (1592–1666, r. 1628–1658) represent the middle phase of the Mughal Empire — Jahangir continuing Akbar's consolidation with an emphasis on justice and art, Shah Jahan marking the architectural zenith with the Taj Mahal and Red Fort. MPSC asks about Jahangir's chain of justice, Shah Jahan's buildings, and the Peacock Throne.

In-Depth

Jahangir (Salim) — Reign and Character

Born as Prince Salim in 1569, Jahangir ascended in 1605 after Akbar's death. He twice rebelled against his father (1599–1600 revolt; Prince Khusrau's subsequent rebellion). Key events:

Chain of Justice (Zanjir-i-Adl): Jahangir hung a golden chain with 60 bells at Agra Fort — any subject could ring it to appeal directly to the Emperor for justice. Famous for its symbolism even if rarely used in practice.

Political issues:

  • Khusrau's rebellion (1606): Jahangir's eldest son rebelled; Khusrau was blinded and kept under arrest.
  • Prince Khurram (later Shah Jahan) and the powerful queen Nur Jahan (née Mihr-un-Nisa, married 1611) dominated court politics; Nur Jahan was the de facto ruler in Jahangir's later years — she issued farmans and had coins struck in her name (extremely rare for a Mughal woman).
  • Sir Thomas Roe — English ambassador (1615–1618) who secured trade privileges for the East India Company.
  • Deccan: failed to capture Ahmednagar; Malik Ambar (Abyssinian regent of Ahmednagar) repulsed Mughal forces repeatedly.

Mewar settlement: Jahangir concluded a settlement with Rana Amar Singh of Mewar (1615) — Mewar recognised Mughal suzerainty; long Rajput–Mughal conflict ended.

Jahangir and Art

Jahangir was a passionate lover of nature and art:

  • Patronised the Mughal miniature painting school to its finest; portraits of birds and animals were his speciality.
  • Famous painters: Ustad Mansur (plants and animals), Abul Hasan (portraits), Bishandas, Manohar.
  • Jahangir's memoirs: Tuzuk-i-Jahangiri (Jahangirnama) — written in Persian; rich in art and natural observations.
  • Shalimar Bagh, Kashmir and Nur Jahan's tomb (Lahore) are Jahangir's architectural works.

Shah Jahan (Khurram) — Reign

Became emperor in 1628 after a rebellion against Jahangir's successor (briefly, Shahryar). Reign notable for:

  • Deccan conquests: Ahmednagar finally annexed (1636); Bijapur and Golkonda made tributary states; Aurangzeb appointed Deccan governor.
  • Balkh/Kandahar campaigns: costly, inconclusive campaigns in Central Asia.
  • Mughal Rebellion (1657): Shah Jahan fell ill; his four sons (Dara Shikoh, Shah Shuja, Aurangzeb, Murad Bakhsh) fought a war of succession; Aurangzeb won, imprisoned Shah Jahan at Agra Fort (1658) where he died in 1666.

Taj Mahal details: Built as a mausoleum for Mumtaz Mahal (died 1631 in childbirth at Burhanpur — now Madhya Pradesh). Chief architect: Ustad Ahmad Lahori. Construction: 1632–1653 (21 years); ~20,000 workers; white marble from Makrana (Rajasthan). Inlaid with pietra dura (parchin-kari) — precious stone inlay in floral patterns. UNESCO World Heritage Site (1983).

Peacock Throne (Takht-e-Taus): Built under Shah Jahan; set with the Koh-i-Noor and Timur Ruby diamonds; looted by Nadir Shah in 1739.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Mumtaz Mahal died at Burhanpur (now Madhya Pradesh, bordering Maharashtra) in 1631 — her body was first buried there before being moved to Agra.
  • Shah Jahan appointed Aurangzeb as governor of the Deccan (Gujarat 1645, Deccan 1652) — this set the stage for Aurangzeb's extensive Maharashtra campaigns later.
  • Malik Ambar (Abyssinian regent of Ahmednagar Sultanate, died 1626) successfully resisted both Jahangir and Shah Jahan's early forces — a major figure in Maharashtra's history; he also promoted Marathi administrative language.

Key Facts

  • Jahangir's Chain of Justice (Zanjir-i-Adl): at Agra Fort.
  • Nur Jahan: issued farmans, had coins struck in her name — unique in Mughal history.
  • Mewar settled under Jahangir: Rana Amar Singh (1615).
  • English ambassador under Jahangir: Sir Thomas Roe (1615–18).
  • Jahangir's memoirs: Tuzuk-i-Jahangiri.
  • Taj Mahal: 1632–1653, for Mumtaz Mahal, architect Ustad Ahmad Lahori.
  • Marble from Makrana (Rajasthan).
  • Peacock Throne looted by Nadir Shah (1739).
  • Shah Jahan imprisoned at Agra Fort (1658) by Aurangzeb.
  • Jama Masjid (Delhi): largest mosque in India.

Exam Traps

  • Nur Jahan issued coins in her own name — extremely rare; don't say "coins were never issued in a queen's name."
  • Taj Mahal architect was Ustad Ahmad Lahori — not Isa Khan or Ustad Isa (popular misconceptions persist).
  • Mumtaz Mahal died in Burhanpur (not Agra) — the body was transferred.
  • Jahangir's Tuzuk-i-Jahangiri ≠ Babur's Baburnama — separate memoirs by separate rulers.
