Odissi (Odisha) PREMAINS

Overview

Odissi is the classical dance form of Odisha, one of India's oldest — with sculptural evidence in Odisha's temple friezes dating from the 2nd century BCE. Its defining postural element is the tribhangi (three-body-bend), creating a sinuous, lyrical quality that contrasts with Bharatanatyam's angular geometry. Rooted in the Jagannath temple tradition of Puri. MPSC tests state, tribhangi, and revival context.

In-Depth

Origin and History

  • Sculptural evidence of Odissi-like poses at Udayagiri caves (Bhubaneswar, ~2nd c. BCE) and Konark Sun Temple (13th c.) friezes.
  • Temple tradition: Maharis (female temple dancers dedicated to Jagannath) performed within the temple precincts.
  • Later, Gotipua tradition: young boys (gotipua) trained to perform devotional dances; they performed acrobatic movements dressed as girls — this tradition co-existed with Maharis.
  • Decline under colonial rule and social stigma (Devadasi abolition movement) — Maharis were legally barred from temple service.
  • Revival (1950s): scholars and gurus including Guru Pankaj Charan Das, Guru Kelucharan Mohapatra, Guru Mayadhar Raut worked to reconstruct Odissi using temple sculptures, palm-leaf manuscripts (Abhinaya Chandrika), and Gotipua traditions.

ThemesKrishna devotion (*Gita Govinda* of Jayadeva); Jagannath

Gita Govinda (by Jayadeva, 12th c. CE): the most important text for Odissi; Sanskrit poem describing Krishna and Radha's love; composed in Odisha for the Jagannath temple; the emotional core of Odissi's abhinaya repertoire.

Key Exponents

  • Guru Kelucharan Mohapatra (1924–2004): the single most important figure in Odissi's modern form; the "father of modern Odissi"; Padma Vibhushan; trained most living Odissi exponents.
  • Sanjukta Panigrahi (1944–1997): disciple of Kelucharan Mohapatra; brought Odissi to international fame; Sangeet Natak Akademi award.
  • Sonal Mansingh: also trained in Odissi; Bharatanatyam also; Padma Vibhushan.
  • Madhavi Mudgal: contemporary; Delhi-based; leading Odissi dancer.

Maharashtra Connection

No direct origin. Odissi schools are present in Maharashtra's major cities. Odisha's connections with Maharashtra historically include Maratha rule over Odisha (under Raghuji Bhosale, 1740s) — though this is political, not cultural dance history.

Key Facts

  • State: Odisha.
  • Evidence: Udayagiri caves (2nd c. BCE) and Konark temple friezes.
  • Defining posture: Tribhangi (three-body-bend).
  • Second stance: Chauka (square, masculine).
  • Temple tradition: Mahari (female) + Gotipua (young boys).
  • Key literary text: Gita Govinda (Jayadeva, 12th c.).
  • Revival hero: Guru Kelucharan Mohapatra (Padma Vibhushan).
  • Music: Odissi Sangeet; Mardala drum.

Memory Aids

  • Odissi = Odisha + Tribhangi + Gita Govinda + Kelucharan Mohapatra.
  • Tribhangi = Three bends (Tri = three, bhangi = bend/break).
  • Gita Govinda = Jayadeva + 12th century + Jagannath temple, Puri.

Exam Traps

  • Tribhangi is Odissi's posture, not Bharatanatyam (which uses araimandi) or Manipuri (which uses Ras Lila postures).
  • Gita Govinda is Odissi's primary literary text — don't confuse with Gitagovinda meaning Bhagavad Gita.
  • Gotipua tradition = young boys dancing (not adult men as in Kathakali).
