Cooperative movement — sugar, dairy, credit PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

Maharashtra has one of India's strongest cooperative movements, particularly in sugar, dairy, cotton, banking, and fisheries. The cooperative sector is deeply entwined with the state's political economy — sugar cooperative chairmen hold enormous political influence. The movement traces its roots to the post-Independence policies of Y.B. Chavan and the philosophical legacy of the Phule-Shahu-Ambedkar tradition emphasizing collective self-reliance. MPSC tests the legislative framework, major cooperative institutions, and their role in rural development.

Sugar Cooperatives — Maharashtra's Political Economy

  • Maharashtra has ~200+ cooperative sugar mills — highest in India; the cooperative sugar sector is Maharashtra's most politically influential institution.
  • Key belt: Kolhapur, Satara, Sangli, Ahmednagar, Solapur, Pune — western Maharashtra.
  • Maharashtra State Cooperative Sugar Federation Ltd (MSCSSF): Apex body for cooperative sugar mills; coordinates procurement, marketing, ethanol production.
  • Cooperative sugar mill chairmen are de facto political kingmakers in western Maharashtra.
  • Vasantdada Sugar Institute (VSI), Pune: Premier research institution for sugar technology, breeding; India's largest sugar research body.
  • Fair and Remunerative Price (FRP): Set by central government (Commission for Agricultural Costs and Prices) for sugarcane; mills must pay FRP to farmers within 14 days of cane crushing season — a major political issue.
  • State Advisory Price (SAP): Some states (not Maharashtra) set higher SAP above FRP; Maharashtra uses FRP with potential top-up.

Dairy Cooperatives

  • Godavari Milk Producers' and Processors' Association Ltd (Nashik region).
  • Gokul Milk (Kolhapur District Cooperative Milk Producers' Union): One of India's largest milk cooperatives; comparable to Amul (Gujarat) model.
  • Mahanand Dairy (Maharashtra Rajya Sahakari Doodh Mahasangh — MRSSDM): State-level dairy federation; "Mahanand" brand; distributed via cooperative retail.
  • Maharashtra is not as dominant in dairy as Gujarat (Amul) or UP, but the cooperative dairy model in Kolhapur and Nashik is significant.

Credit Cooperatives

  • District Central Cooperative Banks (DCCB): ~31 DCCBs in Maharashtra — provide short-term credit to farmers through Primary Agricultural Credit Societies (PACS).
  • Maharashtra State Cooperative Bank (MSCB, "Rajya Bank"): Apex cooperative bank; provides refinance to DCCBs.
  • Maharashtra State Cooperative Agriculture & Rural Development Bank (MSCARDBL, "Land Development Bank"): Long-term agricultural credit (for land development, irrigation, farm mechanisation).
  • NABARD supervises cooperative credit structure; short-term credit flow: NABARD → MSCB → DCCB → PACS → Farmer.

Weavers' and Other Cooperatives

  • Maharashtra Rajya Sahakari Maga-Pudhe Soot Girni Federation (Cotton Ginning Cooperatives): Vidarbha cotton ginning.
  • Handloom Cooperatives: Nanded, Solapur (Solapur Chaddar and Terry Towel GI tag), Chandrapur.
  • Fishermen Cooperatives: Konkan coast; Maharashtra Matsyodya Sahakari Federation.
  • Consumer Cooperatives: Nashik, Pune, Mumbai — through Maharashtra State Cooperative Consumers' Federation.

Cooperative Sector — Issues

  1. Politicisation: Elections to sugar mill boards are proxy for state politics; management often not professionally driven.
  2. Accumulation of losses: Many old urban cooperative banks collapsed (2001 Madhavpura Mercantile Cooperative Bank crisis; 2019–2020 Punjab and Maharashtra Cooperative Bank/PMC Bank fraud — ₹6,500 crore).
  3. Delayed payments to farmers: Sugar mills often delay FRP payment despite legal obligation.
  4. 97th Amendment challenges: Supreme Court in Rajendra N. Shah v. Union of India (2021) partially struck down multi-state cooperative provisions inserted by 97th Amendment; states' cooperative laws remain primary.

Maharashtra Connection

The cooperative sector employs millions in rural Maharashtra — sugar mills are the largest rural employer after agriculture. The political economy of sugar cooperatives explains Maharashtra's electoral politics: Maratha community dominance of cooperative institutions ↔ political power. Cooperative sugar belt (KSS — Kolhapur-Sangli-Satara) is the hinterland that produces Maharashtra's most powerful politicians.
