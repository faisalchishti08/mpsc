Ashtapradhan and Maratha administration PREMAINSMH

Overview

The Ashtapradhan (Council of Eight Ministers) was the central administrative council of the Maratha kingdom under Shivaji Maharaj and his successors. It was a functional cabinet distributing governance across eight portfolios — not a collective decision-making body but a set of individual ministerial responsibilities to the king. Very high MPSC frequency (MH tag): Prelims (names, roles) and Mains (comparison with Mughal administration, Maratha statecraft).

First Peshwa under Shivaji: Moropant Trimbak Pingle — appointed c. 1674 (at coronation). He was a ministerial Peshwa, not the hereditary Brahmin Peshwa of the later Bhat lineage.

Key Features

  • Ministers were individually responsible to the king, not collectively to a council — no cabinet system.
  • All ministers accompanied the king in military campaigns (unlike the later Peshwa-dominated period when ministers could be absent from field operations).
  • Positions were not hereditary under Shivaji — merit and king's trust determined appointments; however, in practice the later Maratha polity saw hereditary Peshwa dominance.
  • Shivaji also had a separate Nyayadhish (chief justice) making the system more complete than just military/revenue.

Peshwa Evolution (Post-Shivaji)

After Shivaji's death and especially from Balaji Vishwanath Bhat (first Bhat Peshwa, 1713) onwards, the Peshwa became the de facto ruler — the Chhatrapati reduced to a figurehead:

  • Balaji Vishwanath (1713–1720): first hereditary Peshwa of Pune; consolidated Maratha power after Mughal pressure.
  • Bajirao I (1720–1740): greatest Peshwa; expanded Maratha confederacy; raided Delhi (1737).
  • Balaji Bajirao (Nana Saheb, 1740–1761): reign ended in disaster at Third Battle of Panipat (1761).
  • Madhavrao I (1761–1772): revived Maratha power after Panipat.

Maratha Revenue Administration

Alongside the Ashtapradhan, the Maratha state had a structured revenue system:

  • Prant (Province) headed by Subhedar.
  • Sarkar and Pargana levels below (adapted from Mughal/Sultanate model).
  • Revenue officials: Mamlatdar (revenue + law & order at taluka level) — still used in Maharashtra today.
  • Patil — village headman; Kulkarni — village accountant/record-keeper.
  • Revenue collected in cash (rokhda) and kind; Chauth and Sardeshmukhi from external territories.

Maharashtra Connection

Central to MPSC MH syllabus. The Ashtapradhan is a defining institution of Maharashtra's historical governance:

  • Mamlatdar revenue system still exists in Maharashtra administration today — direct legacy.
  • Peshwa Bajirao I's capital Pune became the effective Maratha capital; Pune's importance to Maharashtra's identity directly traces to the Peshwa period.
  • The Peshwa's residence (Shaniwarwada, Pune, built 1732) is a major heritage monument.
  • Revenue terms like Patil, Kulkarni remain in use in Maharashtra's rural governance vocabulary.

Key Facts

  • Eight ministers: Peshwa, Amatya, Sachiv, Mantri, Senapati, Sumant, Nyayadhish, Panditrao.
  • First Peshwa: Moropant Trimbak Pingle (under Shivaji).
  • First hereditary Peshwa: Balaji Vishwanath Bhat (1713).
  • Greatest Peshwa: Bajirao I (1720–1740).
  • Mamlatdar (revenue officer at taluka level): Maratha-origin term, still used in Maharashtra.
  • Patil = village headman; Kulkarni = village accountant (both Maratha administrative terms).
  • Chauth = 1/4 tax; Sardeshmukhi = 1/10 additional tax — collected from external territories.
  • Shaniwarwada (Pune): Peshwa's palace, built 1732.

Exam Traps

  • Ashtapradhan ≠ a cabinet — it was eight individual ministers each responsible separately to the king; collective decision-making was not the principle.
  • The Peshwa under Shivaji was a minister (Moropant); the hereditary Bhat Peshwa (ruling power) came only from 1713 — don't conflate.
  • Senapati (Sarnobat) = Commander-in-Chief, NOT the Mantri — Mantri was an intelligence/chronicle officer.
  • Panditrao dealt with religion/charity, not justice — justice was Nyayadhish's domain.
