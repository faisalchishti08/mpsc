Economy, trade and crafts PREMAINS

Overview

The Harappan economy was a surplus-producing, craft-specialised, long-distance trading system that sustained urban populations across ~1.3 million sq km. Key features: standardised weights and measures, specialised crafts (bead-making, pottery, metallurgy, textile), and documented trade with Mesopotamia (Sumeria) via sea and overland routes. This topic tests knowledge of specific trade goods, trading partners, ports, and the evidence for economic organisation.

In-Depth

Agriculture — The Base

  • Crops: Wheat (Triticum compactum and T. sphaercocum), barley, peas, sesame, cotton (earliest cotton textiles in the world), mustard, ragi, jowar (in later phases).
  • Animals: Cattle (humped zebu), buffalo, sheep, goat, pig, elephant (depicted, unclear if domesticated or hunted), dog, cat.
  • Irrigation: Limited evidence — Kalibangan has furrows (ploughed fields); Dholavira had water channels.
  • Grain storage: Large granaries at Harappa and Mohenjo-daro suggest state-controlled grain distribution.

Weights and Measures

One of the most remarkable Harappan achievements was a standardised system of weights and measures:

  • Cubical stone weights (chert/agate) following a binary system (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64…) and then decimal (160, 320, 640, 1600, 3200, 6400).
  • Uniform weight standards across all major Harappan sites — strong evidence of centralised economic authority or pan-IVC commercial conventions.
  • Bronze graduated scales (measuring rods) found at Lothal and Mohenjo-daro.
  • The unit of length was approximately 1.32 inches (a "Harappan inch").

Trade Networks

Internal trade: Movement of raw materials and finished goods within the IVC:

  • Copper: From Khetri (Rajasthan) and Baluchistan.
  • Tin: From Afghanistan (possibly); needed for bronze alloy.
  • Gold: From Karnataka (Kolar?), Rajasthan.
  • Silver: From Iran, Afghanistan.
  • Lapis lazuli: From Badakhshan (Afghanistan) — found even in Mohenjo-daro, Lothal.
  • Carnelian and agate: From Saurashtra (Gujarat).
  • Shells: From Gujarat coast.

External (overseas) trade with Mesopotamia:

  • Harappan seals found at Ur, Kish, Lagash (Mesopotamia).
  • Mesopotamian texts refer to trade with "Meluhha" (identified with IVC), "Dilmun" (Bahrain — intermediary), and "Magan" (Oman).
  • Goods exported from Harappa to Mesopotamia: Cotton textiles, carnelian beads, ivory, timber, copper.
  • Goods imported: Tin, silver, woolens (possibly).
  • Trade route: Mostly maritime (through Persian Gulf); some overland (Iran, Baluchistan).

Lothal — The Trade Hub

Lothal (Gujarat) was IVC's most important trading port:

  • Possibly a dockyard (~214×36 m basin) — debated.
  • A bead-maker's workshop; copper tools; Persian Gulf connections.
  • Persian Gulf seals found at Lothal.
  • A terracotta model of a ship found.
  • Evidence of fire altars (minor).

Evidence for Specialisation and Stratification

  • Separate craft quarters (Chanhu-daro bead-making; Lothal warehouse/dock).
  • Uniform weights suggest merchant class or trade guild system.
  • Granaries near workers' barracks at Harappa suggest a managed labour system — possibly state-organised.
  • No evidence of extreme social inequality in burials (unlike Egypt/Mesopotamia with royal tombs) — but some differentiation exists (quality of houses, goods).

Maharashtra Connection

  • Daimabad had contact with Harappan trade networks — copper and carnelian artefacts showing stylistic links.
  • The Harappan cotton textile trade may have used the Deccan overland route to southern India — Maharashtra would have been part of this network though not a core urban zone.

Key Facts

  • Cotton textiles: Earliest in the world from Harappan sites.
  • Weights: Binary + decimal system; cubical chert weights; standardised pan-IVC.
  • Copper source: Khetri (Rajasthan).
  • Lapis lazuli from: Badakhshan (Afghanistan).
  • Mesopotamia knew Harappans as: "Meluhha"; intermediary = Dilmun (Bahrain).
  • Harappan seals found in: Ur, Kish, Lagash (Mesopotamia).
  • Specialised craft site: Chanhu-daro (bead-making), Lothal (maritime trade).
  • No iron; copper and bronze only.

Memory Aids

  • "Khetri copper, Afghan tin/lapis, Gujarat carnelian" — raw material supply chain.
  • Trade abroad: "H → Meluhha, D → Dilmun, M → Magan" in Mesopotamian texts.
  • Weights: "Binary doubles: 1-2-4-8-16-32-64" — simple doubling system.
  • Lothal = L for Lapis + Logistics (port + bead workshop).

Exam Traps

  • Cotton is the crop for which Harappan are best known in trade — NOT spices (spices = later South India).
  • Mesopotamia called Harappans "Meluhha", not "Sindhu" or "India."
  • Chanhu-daro (not Mohenjo-daro or Harappa) is the dedicated bead-making craft centre.
  • Harappans had no iron — iron appears only with the Later Vedic/Gangetic Iron Age (~1000 BCE).
