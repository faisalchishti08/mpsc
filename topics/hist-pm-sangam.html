Sangam age — Cholas, Cheras, Pandyas PREMAINS

Overview

The Sangam Age (c. 300 BCE – 300 CE) refers to the early historical period in South India when three Tamil kingdoms — the Cheras, Cholas, and Pandyas — flourished. The period is named after the Sangam (literary academies) that produced a rich body of classical Tamil literature. MPSC tests this topic for the three kingdoms' political geography, trade, social structure (caste-ecology overlap), and as the backdrop to understanding South Indian history before the medieval era.

Polity and Administration

The kings were called by Tamil titles: Vanavar (Cheras), Kovalar (Cholas), Minavar (Pandyas). Real political power was often local — the vel (chieftains) and kizhar (village headmen) were important intermediaries. The state levied irai (tribute/tax) and controlled trade routes.

The vel (regional chieftains) hosted poets and warriors, making patron–poet relationships central to political legitimacy. A king who did not patronise poets was stigmatised in Tamil poetry.

Economy — Trade with Rome

The Sangam period coincides with the peak of Rome–India maritime trade (1st–3rd centuries CE):

  • Periplus of the Erythraean Sea (c. 60 CE) and Pliny the Elder (Natural History, c. 77 CE) describe South Indian ports and the pepper trade. Pliny complained Rome spent 50 million sesterces per year on India and Arabia.
  • Musiri/Muchiri (Chera port): Roman merchant settlement; Roman amphora fragments found. The Muziris Papyrus (Vienna, 2nd century CE) records a loan for a spice cargo from Muzuris.
  • Arikamedu (near Puducherry): excavated Roman trading post (Indo-Roman Trading Station) with Roman pottery (arretine ware), glass, and coins.
  • Kaveripattinam/Puhar (Chola): thriving port; described in the Cilappatikaram epic.
  • Exports: pepper, spices, cotton textiles, pearls (Korkai/Pandya), gems, ivory.
  • Imports: gold, wine (Roman amphorae found), olive oil, copper, lead.

Society and the Five Eco-regions (Tinai System)

Sangam literature uses a systematic tinai (ecological region) framework linking landscape, flower, bird, season, and human mood:

  1. Kurinji (mountain): jasmine; male hero in union with heroine (love meeting).
  2. Mullai (pastoral forest): jasmine creeper; patient waiting.
  3. Marutam (agricultural plain): marutam flower; infidelity.
  4. Neithal (coastal): water-lily; anxiety of separation.
  5. Palai (wasteland/arid): cadamba; hardship of separation.

This eco-code permeates Sangam poetry and is unique in world literature.

Caste and Society

Sangam society was not yet rigidly Brahmanical. Varṇa categories existed but were less rigid. There were:

  • Brahmins (antanar) — ritual specialists, respected but not dominant.
  • Vel/Vellalar — agricultural elite.
  • Paraiyar — lower-caste drummers; their presence was essential at royal events.
  • Women in Sangam poetry have voice and agency — many Sangam poets were women (e.g., Avvaiyar, Kaccipettu Nannakkaiyar, Nakkirar).
  • Slavery existed but was limited in scope.

Key Rulers (Sangam period)

  • Chera: Nedunjeral Adan — fought the Cholas and Pandyas; great patron; cremated on his own funeral pyre after his defeat (legend).
  • Chola: Karikala — dug the Grand Anicut (Kallanai dam) across the Kaveri river (still functional); defeated the Cheras and Pandyas at Venni.
  • Pandya: Mudukudumi Peruvaludhi — great warrior and Vedic sacrificer.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. The Sangam period trade network connected the Deccan (including early Maharashtra) to the Tamil ports — Roman goods and coins have been found at sites across Maharashtra and Andhra, suggesting Maharashtra participated in the same Roman–Indian trade circuits. The Satavahanas of Maharashtra were contemporaries of the Sangam kingdoms and traded through the same Western and Eastern Ghats-to-coast routes.

Exam Traps

  • Cheras (Sangam period) are NOT the same as the later Chera/Kerala kings — the medieval Cheras (Kulashekhara dynasty) are a separate, later dynasty.
  • Arikamedu (near Pondicherry) is the Roman trading post — do NOT say Taxila or Barygaza.
  • Karikala built the Kallanai dam — not the Pandya or Chera kings.
  • The Sangam academies (sangam) were literary institutions, not political bodies — they didn't govern.
