Varkari saints — Dnyaneshwar, Tukaram, Namdev, Eknath PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

The Varkari Sampraday is Maharashtra's most distinctive spiritual tradition — a devotional movement centred on the worship of Vitthal (Vithoba) at Pandharpur (Solapur district). Its saints — Dnyaneshwar (Jnandev), Namdev, Eknath, Tukaram, Chokhamela — produced a body of Marathi devotional poetry (abhangas) that shaped the language, caste reform thought, and cultural identity of Maharashtra. The Wari (pilgrimage) to Pandharpur twice a year is among India's largest religious gatherings. MPSC tests saint birth-death years, works, birthplaces, and the Wari calendar.

Pandharpur and Wari

  • Pandharpur (Solapur district): Spiritual capital of Maharashtra; Vitthal (Vithoba) temple on the banks of Bhima River (Chandrabhaga); one of Maharashtra's most important pilgrimages.
  • Wari (Vari): Pilgrimage; done twice yearly:
  • Ashadhi Ekadashi (Ashadh Shukla 11th): Major Wari — July (approximately); lakhs of Varkaris walk from different directions to Pandharpur.
  • Kartiki Ekadashi (Kartik Shukla 11th): Second Wari — October-November; also large.
  • Palkhi procession: Palanquin of Tukaram (from Dehu) and Jnandev (from Alandi) carried to Pandharpur on foot by Varkaris; 21-day walk; one of India's largest annual religious processions.
  • Alandi (Pune district): Samadhi of Jnandev; major pilgrimage site; Palkhi begins here (Jnandev's Palkhi).
  • Dehu (Pune district): Birthplace of Tukaram; second major Palkhi origin; Tukaram's Gatha manuscripts at Dehu are on UNESCO Memory of the World Register (2005).

Dnyaneshwari — Special Significance

  • Written 1290 CE by Dnyaneshwar at age ~15–16.
  • Marathi commentary on Bhagavad Gita; made Gita's philosophy accessible to common people in Marathi.
  • 9,032 ovis (verses) in Ovi metre.
  • Pasaydan (at the end of Dnyaneshwari): Universal prayer for all beings; recited at end of all religious gatherings in Maharashtra.
  • Maharashtra declared 2014 as the year of 1 May 2014 Marathi Classical Language — Dnyaneshwari (1290) is one of the key texts used to establish Marathi's antiquity.
  • Marathi Classical Language: Marathi granted Classical Language status by Central Government in October 2024.

Other Notable Saints

  • Ramdas Swami (1608–1681): Shivaji's spiritual guru; founded Samarth Sampraday; "Dasbodh" (1659–1681, spiritual guide in Marathi); established Hanuman temples (Marbat tradition); Sajjangad Fort (Satara) = samadhi; "Jai Jai Raghuveer Samarth."
  • Gadge Baba (Debuji Zhingaraji Janorkar, 1876–1956): Social reformer-saint; promoted cleanliness, anti-superstition; Khanjira plate; inspired Swacha Bharat Abhiyan concept; "Sant Gadge Baba Gram Swachata Abhiyan" in MH is named after him.

Maharashtra Connection

The Varkari movement was Maharashtra's democratic spiritual revolution — Brahmins, Kunbis, Mahars, Mangs all participated on equal footing in the Wari. Chokhamela (Mahar/Dalit) in the 14th century challenged untouchability through devotion six centuries before legal reform. This tradition directly influenced B.R. Ambedkar's understanding of caste (he famously rejected Hinduism while acknowledging the Varkaris' partial critique of caste). Tukaram's Gatha on UNESCO Memory of the World register (2005) is a source of state pride.

Exam Traps

  • Tukaram's Gatha manuscripts (at Dehu) are on UNESCO Memory of the World (2005) — NOT UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage.
  • Ramdas Swami was Shivaji's spiritual guru; Samarth Ramdas is the full name; Dasbodh is the work.
