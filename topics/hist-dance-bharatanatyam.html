Bharatanatyam (Tamil Nadu) PREMAINS

Overview

Bharatanatyam is the oldest and most widely performed classical dance form of India, originating from Tamil Nadu's temple devadasi tradition. It was revived, codified and formalised in the 20th century and is now practised globally. Its name derives from Bharata (the Natyashastra's author or the acronym BHAva-RAga-TAla-NAtyam) + natyam (dance). MPSC tests its origin, revival, and key features.

In-Depth

Origin and History

  • Rooted in the devadasi (temple dancer) tradition of Tamil Nadu temples; specifically, the sadir tradition performed in the inner sanctum.
  • Texts: Natyashastra (Bharata Muni) + Abhinaya Darpana (Nandikeshwara) — the primary technical texts.
  • Nattuvanars (hereditary masters) choreographed and taught; female dancers performed at Chidambaram, Thanjavur, and other major temple complexes.
  • Devadasi system gradually degraded into exploitation; Anti-Devadasi Act (Madras, 1947) abolished the system.
  • Revival: Rukmini Devi Arundale (1904–1986) — a Brahmin woman who learned and revived Bharatanatyam, stripped it of its "disreputable" association, and founded Kalakshetra (Chennai, 1936) — premier institution for classical arts. She is credited with bringing it to concert stages.
  • E. Krishna Iyer: lawyer and reformer who also promoted and performed Bharatanatyam dressed as a woman to destigmatise it.

Structure of a Bharatanatyam Recital (Margam)

Fixed sequence: Alarippu → Jatiswaram → Shabdam → Varnam (centrepiece) → Padam → Tillana → Mangalam.

  • Varnam: the longest, most complex piece; combines nritta (pure dance) and abhinaya (expression).
  • Tillana: fast, rhythmic; climax of the recital.

Notable Exponents

  • Rukmini Devi Arundale (reviver; Kalakshetra).
  • Balasaraswati (T. Balasaraswati, 1918–1984): devadasi lineage; considered the greatest Bharatanatyam exponent of the 20th c.; Ramon Magsaysay Award.
  • Mrinalini Sarabhai (Gujarat; trained in Bharatanatyam; Padma Vibhushan).
  • Yamini Krishnamurthy (Delhi-based; Padma Vibhushan).
  • Alarmel Valli (contemporary; Padma Bhushan).

Maharashtra Connection

No direct origin in Maharashtra. However:

  • Mumbai and Pune have major Bharatanatyam institutions and annual festivals.
  • Natyashastra is the common foundation for all classical dance including Maharashtra's folk forms (Lavani shares shringara rasa aesthetics with Bharatanatyam abhinaya).
  • Kalakshetra graduates teach in Maharashtra extensively.

Key Facts

  • Origin: Tamil Nadu temple devadasi tradition.
  • Revival: Rukmini Devi Arundale; Kalakshetra (Chennai, 1936).
  • Primary posture: araimandi (half-seated, bent-knee stance).
  • Music: Carnatic.
  • Recital sequence: Alarippu → … → Varnam (centrepiece) → … → Tillana → Mangalam.
  • Greatest 20th-c. exponent: T. Balasaraswati (Ramon Magsaysay Award).
  • Anti-Devadasi Act: Madras, 1947.

Memory Aids

  • Bharatanatyam = Tamil Nadu + araimandi + Carnatic music + Rukmini Devi.
  • Kalakshetra = Chennai, 1936 (K = classical; 1936 pre-Independence).
  • Recital: "A-J-Sh-V-P-T-M" (Alarippu to Mangalam).

Exam Traps

  • Rukmini Devi was NOT from a devadasi family — she was a Brahmin who chose to learn and revive the form despite social disapproval.
  • Balasaraswati (T. Bala) ≠ Rukmini Devi — two different legends; different philosophies (Bala represented the traditional devadasi lineage; Rukmini Devi the brahmanised revival).
  • Bharatanatyam is named from Natyashastra author Bharata (or the BhAva-RAga-TAla acronym) — NOT from the country "Bharat."
