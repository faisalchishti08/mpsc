Power generation (Koyna, thermal, Tarapur) PREMAINSINTMH

Overview

Maharashtra is one of India's largest consumers of electricity but also a significant producer. The state's power sector is governed by the Maharashtra State Electricity Distribution Company Ltd (MSEDCL, "Mahavitaran") for distribution and Maharashtra State Power Generation Company Ltd (MAHAGENCO) for generation. Key sources include hydroelectric (Koyna dominates), thermal (Vidarbha coal belt), nuclear (Tarapur — India's first nuclear plant, 1969), and growing solar/wind. Power shortages, agricultural subsidy burden, and transmission losses are persistent challenges. MPSC tests plant names, installed capacity, fuel types, and regulatory bodies.

Key Power Plants — Details

1. Koyna Hydro Power Project (Satara)

  • India's largest completed hydro project in western India; ~1,960 MW installed (4 stages).
  • Shivasagar Reservoir (Koyna Dam); underground powerhouse.
  • 1967 Koyna earthquake (magnitude ~6.3) — reservoir-triggered seismic activity (RIS — Reservoir Induced Seismicity).
  • Maharashtra's hydro backbone; also used for pumped storage (during off-peak → pump water up; during peak → release).

2. Tarapur Atomic Power Station (TAPS), Palghar

  • India's first nuclear power plant — commissioned 1969 (Unit 1 and 2 were BWR from US-GE; Units 3 and 4 are PHWR, commissioned 2006).
  • Located at Tarapur, Palghar district (earlier part of Thane).
  • Operated by NPCIL (Nuclear Power Corporation of India Ltd).
  • Total capacity: ~440 MW (Units 1+2: 210 MW total; Units 3+4: 540 MW total; net ~440 MW active).
  • Historical significance: Tarapur was built under US-India Agreement (1963); after India's 1974 nuclear test, US stopped fuel supply — India used CIRUS reactor at Trombay for fuel fabrication.

3. Chandrapur Super Thermal Power Station (CSTPS)

  • Maharashtra's largest thermal plant — ~2,340 MW installed; Chandrapur district, Vidarbha.
  • Coal sourced from Western Coalfields Ltd (WCL); air pollution concerns in Chandrapur (classified "critical" polluted area by CPCB).

4. Dabhol/Ratnagiri Gas and Power Project (RGPPL)

  • Formerly Enron Dabhol Power Project — controversial 1993–2001 project.
  • Enron of USA signed controversial PPA (Power Purchase Agreement) with Maharashtra; project cancelled after Maharashtra Govt defaulted; Enron collapsed 2001 (US scandal).
  • Ratnagiri Gas and Power Private Ltd (RGPPL): Joint venture of NTPC, GAIL, others; took over Dabhol plant; running at partial capacity due to lack of gas supply.
  • RGPPL Dabhol is a standard MPSC case study in power sector governance failure.

Power Regulatory Framework

  • Maharashtra Electricity Regulatory Commission (MERC): Regulates tariff for MSEDCL; fixes electricity rates; quasi-judicial; set up under Electricity Regulatory Commissions Act 1998 and Electricity Act 2003.
  • MSEDCL (Mahavitaran): Distribution to ~3 crore consumers in Maharashtra.
  • MAHAGENCO: State generation company; owns Koyna, thermal plants.
  • MSETCL (Mahapareshan): Transmission.
  • Tata Power, Adani Electricity: Private distributors in Mumbai area.

Challenges

  • Agricultural subsidy: Farmers get electricity at highly subsidised or free rates; revenue burden on MSEDCL.
  • Transmission losses: ~15–18% (AT&C losses); above CERC targets.
  • Cross-subsidy: Industrial/commercial consumers pay higher tariff to subsidize agriculture.
  • Renewable energy targets: Maharashtra RE policy 2020 targets for wind, solar under MNRE National targets.

Maharashtra Connection

Tarapur (1969) makes Maharashtra historically significant in India's nuclear programme. The Koyna earthquake (1967) sparked global debate on RIS. The Enron-Dabhol scandal was studied by IIMs and international business schools as a case study in power sector privatisation gone wrong.

Exam Traps

  • Tarapur Units 1+2 were from US (General Electric) but after 1974, relations soured — units 3+4 are domestic PHWR (Pressurised Heavy Water Reactor) technology.
  • MERC regulates tariff — NOT MSEDCL. MSEDCL is the distribution company.
  • Koyna earthquake (1967) was Reservoir Induced Seismicity — NOT a natural tectonic earthquake; RIS is a studied phenomenon linked to dam-filling.
