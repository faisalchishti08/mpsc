Classical languages of India (incl. Marathi) PREMAINSMH

Overview

Classical language status in India is granted by the Government of India to languages meeting specific criteria of antiquity, large body of original literature, and distinct character from modern languages. Currently 6 languages have classical status: Sanskrit, Tamil, Kannada, Telugu, Malayalam, and Marathi (2024). This topic is extremely high-priority for MPSC — Marathi's recognition as a classical language is a direct Maharashtra-specific current affairs fact. The topic also covers India's 22 Scheduled Languages (8th Schedule) and the three-language formula.

In-Depth

Classical Languages of India

Criteria for Classical Language Status (as revised):

  1. High antiquity of its early texts/recorded history over a period of 1,500–2,000 years.
  2. A body of ancient literature/texts, which is considered a valuable heritage.
  3. The literary tradition should be original and not borrowed from another speech community.
  4. The classical language and literature being distinct from its modern form — with possible discontinuity between the classical language and its later forms/offshoots.

8th Schedule Languages (Scheduled Languages)

The Eighth Schedule of the Indian Constitution lists the official languages of India (not all are in 8th Schedule; Classical ≠ 8th Schedule):

  • Original 14 (1950): Assamese, Bengali, Gujarati, Hindi, Kannada, Kashmiri, Malayalam, Marathi, Oriya (now Odia), Punjabi, Sanskrit, Tamil, Telugu, Urdu.
  • 21st Amendment (1967): Added Sindhi (15th).
  • 71st Amendment (1992): Added Konkani, Manipuri, Nepali (18th total).
  • 92nd Amendment (2003): Added Bodo, Dogri, Maithili, Santhali (22nd total).
  • Current total: 22 languages in the 8th Schedule.

Marathi is in the 8th Schedule since original 1950.

Official Language of India: Hindi and English

  • Article 343: Hindi in Devanagari script = official language of the Union.
  • Article 344/351: Official Language Commission; promotion of Hindi.
  • Official Languages Act 1963: English to continue as associate official language.
  • Three-Language Formula: recommended by Kothari Commission; teach mother tongue, Hindi, and English (or one other South Indian language in Hindi-speaking states).

Sanskrit's Special Status

  • In the 8th Schedule; no native state; Uttarakhand declared Sanskrit as second official language (2010).
  • Only language that is both in 8th Schedule AND is a Classical Language AND has no native majority speech community.
  • Used in court oaths, national anthem partial phrases, national motto (Satyameva Jayate — from Mundaka Upanishad, Sanskrit).

Classical Tamil — Specific Details

  • Tamil was the first to receive Classical status (2004) — under PM Vajpayee (NDA).
  • Sangam literature: oldest surviving Indian secular literature; ~3rd c. BCE–3rd c. CE; collections: Ettutogai (8 anthologies), Pattupattu (10 idylls).
  • Tolkappiyam: oldest Tamil grammar (~2nd–1st c. BCE); grammar + poetics.
  • Thirukkural (Thiruvalluvar): 1330 couplets; ethics, politics, love; UNESCO advisory panel cited it; translated into 80+ languages.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Marathi received Classical Language status in October 2024 — this is the single most important Maharashtra-specific fact in this topic.
  • The Cabinet decision was made on October 3, 2024 (ahead of Maharashtra assembly elections).
  • Basis for Marathi's classical status:
  • Shilahara copperplate inscriptions (11th c.) contain early Marathi.
  • Dnyaneshwari (1290 CE): Dnyaneshwar's Marathi commentary on Bhagavad Gita — foundational literary text.
  • Lilavati (Hemadpant, Yadava period): administrative text.
  • Long literary tradition through Namdev, Eknath, Tukaram, Ramdas.
  • Marathi is in the 8th Schedule since 1950 and is the official language of Maharashtra.
  • Marathi classical status demand was a long-standing political-cultural demand; granted October 2024.

Exam Traps

  • Classical language ≠ 8th Schedule language — they are independent categories.
  • Tamil was first (2004), not Sanskrit — common incorrect assumption.
  • Marathi 2024 — very recent; check if question is pre/post this date.
  • 22 Scheduled Languages (not 18 or 14) — current count after 92nd Amendment.
  • Odia (formerly Oriya) is in 8th Schedule; correct spelling is "Odia" since Odisha Renaming Act 2011.
