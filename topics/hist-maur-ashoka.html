Ashoka — Kalinga war, Dhamma, edicts PREMAINS

Overview

Ashoka the Great (~268–232 BCE), the third Mauryan emperor, is one of the most remarkable rulers in world history — a conqueror who renounced war after witnessing its horror and transformed into a champion of non-violence, moral governance, and religious tolerance. The Kalinga War (~261 BCE), his subsequent conversion to Buddhism, and his propagation of Dhamma through edicts engraved across the empire are central themes. His legacy lives in India's national emblem (Ashoka Chakra) and the idea of the state's moral responsibility. MPSC makes Ashoka a top-priority topic.

In-Depth

Ashoka Before the Kalinga War

  • Born ~304 BCE; son of Bindusara (2nd Mauryan emperor); mother Subhadrangi (or Dharma — some accounts).
  • Ashoka means "without sorrow" — the name acquired irony after Kalinga.
  • Served as viceroy of Taxila and Ujjain before becoming emperor.
  • Succession struggle after Bindusara's death (~273 BCE): traditional accounts say Ashoka eliminated 99 brothers (one surviving, Tissa/Vitashoka) — almost certainly exaggerated, but power struggle was real.
  • Crowned ~268 BCE (coronation four years after assuming power).

The Kalinga War (~261 BCE)

  • Kalinga = modern Odisha (roughly).
  • Ashoka launched a massive military campaign to conquer this eastern kingdom.
  • Kalinga Rock Edict XIII (most moving of all his edicts) records the war's aftermath: 1,50,000 carried off captive, 1,00,000 killed, many times that number perished — these are the numbers Ashoka himself recorded.
  • The sight of the carnage — "bodies of slain, dying, and dragged away" — caused Ashoka profound remorse.
  • He embraced Buddhism (already had contact, now became a devoted lay practitioner and later a monk).
  • He renounced war of conquest (Digvijaya) and replaced it with Dhammavijaya (conquest through Dhamma/righteousness).

Dhamma — Ashoka's Philosophy

Ashoka's Dhamma (Pali/Prakrit for Sanskrit Dharma) was NOT sectarian Buddhism — it was an eclectic moral code intended for all:

  • Respect for elders; compassion toward servants, Brahmans, ascetics, and the poor.
  • Non-violence (Ahimsa): reduction of animal slaughter; banned certain animal sacrifices; reduced meat in royal kitchen.
  • Tolerance: Respect for all religious sects (Sangha Vivekena = "avoiding conflict between sects").
  • Social welfare: Planting of trees (along roads), digging wells, building rest houses (Dharmashalas), hospitals for humans and animals.
  • Dhamma Mahamattas (ministers of Dhamma): New officials appointed to spread and implement Dhamma.

The Maski Edict: The Maski (Raichur, Karnataka) minor rock edict was significant because it first mentioned the name "Devanampiya Piyadassi Ashoka" — confirming that the edicts attributed to "Devanampiya Piyadassi" (Beloved of Gods, Gracious to All) were indeed by Ashoka (previously the king's identity was uncertain).

Buddhist Missions (after 3rd Council, ~250 BCE)

  • Mahinda (son) → Sri Lanka (Theravada established).
  • Sanghamitta (daughter) → Sri Lanka (brought Bodhi tree sapling).
  • Dharmarakshita → Western regions (Greek territories/Yavana regions).
  • Mahadharmarakshita → Maharashtra (Maharattha) — one of the few direct Maharashtra-Ashokan connections in texts.
  • Missions to: Egypt, Macedonia, Epirus, Cyrene, Syria — evidence of India's first diplomatic-religious outreach to the West.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Sopara/Nalasopara (Palghar district): Fragment of Ashokan rock edict found — evidence that Mauryan administration reached the Konkan coast.
  • Mahadharmarakshita's mission to Maharattha (Maharashtra): Buddhist texts record Ashoka sending a Buddhist mission to Maharashtra — suggesting early Buddhism in the region (which connects to Ajanta, Karla caves in subsequent centuries).
  • The Ashokan Dhamma concept of compassion and non-sectarianism resonates with Maharashtra's Varkari and Bhakti traditions.

Key Facts

  • Ashoka: reigned ~268–232 BCE; 3rd Mauryan emperor.
  • Kalinga War: ~261 BCE; MRE XIII records: 1,50,000 captives, 1,00,000 killed.
  • Ashokan script: Brahmi (most regions); Kharosthi (NW India); Greek/Aramaic (Afghanistan).
  • Brahmi deciphered: James Prinsep, 1837.
  • Maski edict (Karnataka): First edict to mention Ashoka by name.
  • Lumbini Pillar: Identifies Lumbini as Buddha's birthplace.
  • Dhamma Mahamattas: New officers appointed in 5th regnal year (per MRE V).
  • Sopara edict fragment: Palghar district, Maharashtra.

Exam Traps

  • Ashoka's Dhamma is NOT the same as Buddhism — it is a universal moral code tolerant of all religions; calling it "Buddhist evangelism" oversimplifies it.
  • The Kalinga war is recorded in MRE XIII (Major Rock Edict 13) — not in a "Kalinga edict" (Kalinga had separate Separate Edicts at Dhauli/Jaugada, but MRE XIII is where the remorse is expressed, found at Girnar etc.).
  • Brahmi decipherment = Prinsep (1837) — it was not the Maski edict itself that was first; decipherment enabled reading all edicts.
  • Ashoka still gave cave donations to Ajivikas (Barabar Caves) after becoming Buddhist — evidence of religious tolerance.
