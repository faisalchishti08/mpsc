Nyaya PREMAINS

Overview

Nyaya is one of India's six orthodox (astika) schools of philosophy, founded by Gautama (Akshapada) and systematised in the Nyayasutras (~2nd c. BCE–2nd c. CE). It is primarily a school of logic and epistemology — developing India's most sophisticated system of valid inference (anumana) and knowledge theory (pramana). MPSC tests the six schools collectively (the Shad Darshanas) and Nyaya's specific contribution.

Nyaya — Core Philosophy

Founder: Gautama (also called Akshapada). Primary text: Nyayasutras (16 padarthas — categories of logic); commentaries by Vatsyayana (Nyayabhasya) and later Udyotakara, Vacaspati Misra, Gangesa Upadhyaya.

Four Sources of Valid Knowledge (Pramanas):

  1. Pratyaksha: direct perception (through the senses).
  2. Anumana: inference — the most developed concept in Nyaya; three forms: purvavat (from cause to effect), sheshavat (from effect to cause), samanyatodrishta (by analogy).
  3. Upamana: comparison/analogy.
  4. Shabda: verbal testimony (authoritative word).

Theory of Inference (Anumana): The Nyaya syllogism has five members (avayavas):

  1. Pratijna (proposition): "There is fire on the hill."
  2. Hetu (reason): "Because there is smoke."
  3. Udaharana (example): "Whatever has smoke has fire, like the kitchen."
  4. Upanaya (application): "This hill has smoke."
  5. Nigamana (conclusion): "Therefore the hill has fire."

This five-member syllogism is more elaborate than Aristotle's three-member syllogism and predates formal Western logic independently.

God in Nyaya: Nyaya is theistic — argues for the existence of God (Ishvara) as a logical necessity for creation and cosmic order; uses the cosmological argument (inferring a maker from the creation).

Liberation (Apavarga): Freedom from pain through correct knowledge; Nyaya holds that ignorance (avidya) is the root of suffering; liberation = cessation of all pain.

Navya-Nyaya: A refined logical school developed by Gangesa Upadhyaya (13th c.) in his Tattvachintamani — the most technically rigorous school of Indian logic; influenced modern logic studies.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. However:

  • The Nyaya logical tradition was part of the curriculum at ancient Indian educational centres like Nalanda (Bihar) that attracted scholars from across the subcontinent.
  • Maharashtra's scholarly tradition (Pune as a centre of Sanskrit learning under the Peshwas) engaged with all six darshanas including Nyaya.
  • The Bhandarkar Oriental Research Institute (BORI), Pune — India's premier Sanskrit/Indology research institute — houses critical editions of Nyaya texts among others.

Key Facts

  • Nyaya founder: Gautama (Akshapada).
  • Primary text: Nyayasutras.
  • Focus: Logic and epistemology.
  • Four pramanas: Pratyaksha, Anumana, Upamana, Shabda.
  • Five-member syllogism: Pratijna, Hetu, Udaharana, Upanaya, Nigamana.
  • Nyaya is theistic (accepts God as a logical inference).
  • Paired with: Vaisheshika (both collectively called Nyaya-Vaisheshika).
  • Navya-Nyaya: Gangesa Upadhyaya's Tattvachintamani (13th c.).

Memory Aids

  • Six schools pairs: "N-V, S-Y, M-Ve" (Nyaya-Vaisheshika, Samkhya-Yoga, Mimamsa-Vedanta).
  • Four pramanas: "P-A-U-S" (Pratyaksha, Anumana, Upamana, Shabda).
  • Five syllogism steps: "P-H-U-U-N" (Proposition, Hetu, Example, Application, Nigamana).
  • Nyaya = Logic (N = kNowledge through logic).

Exam Traps

  • Nyaya is theistic — accepts God; don't confuse with Samkhya (which is atheistic/agnostic on God) or Mimamsa (which ignores God).
  • Anumana = inference (the core Nyaya contribution); Pratyaksha = perception — these two are the most tested pramanas.
  • Navya-Nyaya ≠ original Nyaya — Navya-Nyaya is a 13th-century refined logical system, not ancient Nyaya.
