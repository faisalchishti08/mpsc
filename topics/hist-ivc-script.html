Script and society PREMAINS

Overview

The Harappan script (Indus script) is one of the few ancient writing systems that remains undeciphered, making it a unique intellectual puzzle and a key limiting factor in understanding Harappan society. About 400–600 distinct signs have been identified from ~4,000 inscribed objects (seals, tablets, pottery). The script is pictographic/logosyllabic, written right to left, and appears to encode a language that has left no known descendant. The Harappan script directly connects to questions on society — we cannot read their laws, their religious texts, or their history in their own words.

Why Is It Not Deciphered?

  1. No bilingual text: Unlike the Rosetta Stone (Egyptian-Greek-Demotic), no Harappan inscription is found alongside a known language. This prevents the substitution approach used to crack Egyptian hieroglyphics.
  2. Short inscriptions: Average 5–6 signs per seal — too short for statistical pattern analysis.
  3. Unknown language: Even if sign values were determined, the underlying language may be extinct with no descendants.
  4. No clear ideographic "key": Some signs look like objects, but most are abstract or too stylised to identify.

The Dravidian Hypothesis

The mainstream scholarly view (Parpola, Mahadevan) is that the Harappan language was proto-Dravidian:

  • Tamil Dravidian vocabulary has words for fish (min) which phonetically match certain common Harappan signs.
  • The "fish sign" is one of the most common and appears in many combinatory forms — interpreted as a phonetic element in Dravidian.
  • Statistical analysis of sign frequency patterns resembles agglutinative languages (Dravidian is agglutinative; Vedic Sanskrit is not).
  • This hypothesis implies that before the Indo-Aryan migration (~1500 BCE), the IVC spoke a Dravidian language — and the modern Dravidian languages (Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam) are distant descendants.

What the Script Tells Us (Despite Not Being Deciphered)

  • Seals were used as trade and ownership markers — seal impressions on clay indicate goods were sealed and stamped.
  • The script appears on prestige objects (seals, not ordinary pottery) — suggesting it was used by a merchant/elite class, not universally.
  • Wide distribution of similar sign combinations across geographically distant Harappan sites suggests a shared writing system — evidence for a unified cultural/administrative network.
  • Absence of monumental inscriptions (unlike Ashoka's pillars or Egyptian temple walls) suggests the script was for commercial/administrative use, not public proclamation.

Harappan Society Inferences

Since the script is undeciphered, our knowledge of Harappan social organisation is limited:

  • No identified kings, priests, or dynasties.
  • No laws, religious texts, or historical narratives recoverable.
  • No personal names or divine names known.
  • Governance model unknown — "theocracy? merchant republic? city-state? priestly oligarchy?" — all speculative.

Maharashtra Connection

Not directly Maharashtra-specific. The Dravidian hypothesis, if correct, would connect Harappan linguistic heritage to Marathi's linguistic neighbourhood — Marathi is Indo-Aryan but surrounded by Dravidian languages (Telugu, Kannada), and parts of Maharashtra's tribal population speak Dravidian-adjacent languages (Gondi is Dravidian).

Exam Traps

  • The script is NOT deciphered — never say it has been decoded or that it is "proto-Sanskrit".
  • The Dravidian hypothesis is a hypothesis, not a proven fact — MPSC may ask whether it's deciphered (No) or the leading theory (Dravidian).
  • Harappan script is written right to left, not left to right (like English or modern Devanagari).
  • Steve Farmer's claim that it is NOT writing at all is a minority view — most scholars consider it a writing system.
