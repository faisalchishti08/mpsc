Mauryan administration and economy PREMAINS

Overview

The Mauryan Empire's administrative system (~321–185 BCE) was the most sophisticated governmental structure in ancient Indian history — a centralised bureaucratic monarchy with a vast spy network, standardised revenue, and provincial/district administration extending over ~5 million sq km. The primary sources are the Arthashastra (Kautilya) and Megasthenes' Indica (Greek ambassador). MPSC tests on the Arthashastra's organisation, provincial structure, the spy system, revenue, economy, and the role of specific officers.

In-Depth

The Arthashastra — Blueprint of Mauryan Government

Kautilya's Arthashastra (15 books, ~6,000 shlokas) covers:

  • Saptanga Theory: The seven elements of a state — Svamin (king), Amatya (minister), Janapada (territory), Durga (fort), Kosha (treasury), Danda (army), Mitra (ally).
  • All seven must be strong for the state to be secure.
  • The Mandala Theory: Foreign policy — a ruler's immediate neighbour is an enemy; the neighbour's neighbour is a friend (concentric circles of friend/enemy). Classified relations into 6 types: Sandhi (peace treaty), Vigraha (war), Asana (neutrality), Yana (marching), Samshraya (alliance), Dvaidhibhava (double policy).

Megasthenes on Pataliputra Administration (City Government)

Megasthenes describes a municipal board of 30 members divided into 6 committees of 5 each:

  1. Industrial arts and crafts.
  2. Foreign visitors (hospitality, surveillance).
  3. Registration of births and deaths.
  4. Trade and commerce.
  5. Manufacture and supervision of goods sold.
  6. Collection of taxes on goods sold (1/10th of sales price).

This is one of the earliest descriptions of urban administration in the world.

The four major provinces with their capitals were: Uttarapatha (Taxila), Avantiratha (Ujjain), Prachya (Tosali/Dhauli = Kalinga), Dakshinapatha (Suvarnagiri, possibly near modern Karnataka). Pataliputra was the imperial capital.

Espionage System

Kautilya's Arthashastra devotes extensive attention to spies:

  • Guptacharas (secret agents): Multiple types — Sattrin (student spies in guise of students), Likhita (spies using documents), Tikshna (fierce assassins), Rasa-rada (poison users).
  • Agents provocateurs: Some spies tasked with testing officials' loyalty by offering bribes.
  • Women spies: Vishakanya (poison girl) and female agents deployed at court and in enemy states.
  • Spies reported directly to the king or chief spy-master; formed a parallel intelligence network alongside the official bureaucracy.

Revenue System

  • Bhaga (share of crop): The standard land tax — 1/6 of produce was the theoretical standard; Arthashastra mentions various rates.
  • Bali: Additional royal tribute.
  • Kara: A fixed tax on artisans and merchants.
  • Land measurement: Standardised; royal officials surveyed and classified land.
  • State monopolies: Salt, mining (including gold mines), forests — all under state control.
  • Sita lands: Crown lands directly cultivated under state supervision using hired and slave labour.

Economy

  • Agriculture dominated; state irrigation works (tanks, canals) — Sudarshana Lake in Girnar (Gujarat) built by Pushyagupta under Chandragupta, repaired by Tushaspa under Ashoka (recorded in Junagarh Rock Inscription of Rudradaman, ~150 CE).
  • Trade: Extensive internal and external; standardised weights and measures; road network (the Uttarapath / Rajmarg — highway from Taxila to Pataliputra to Tamralipti).
  • Coinage: Punch-marked silver coins (Karshapanas) — state-issued; earliest Indian coins with royal use.
  • Artisans and guilds (Srenis): Organised production; guilds acted as banks, courts, and regulatory bodies.

Maharashtra Connection

  • Suvarnagiri (Golden Mountain — possibly in Karnataka/Maharashtra border region) was the provincial capital for the southern Mauryan province (Dakshinapatha) — bringing Mauryan administration directly into the Deccan/Maharashtra region.
  • Ashokan edict fragment at Sopara (Nalasopara, Palghar) attests to Mauryan administrative presence in Maharashtra.
  • Karla and Nashik caves funded by Satavahana-era merchants whose trade networks were built on Mauryan-era road infrastructure.

Key Facts

  • Saptanga: 7 elements — Svamin, Amatya, Janapada, Durga, Kosha, Danda, Mitra.
  • Land tax: 1/6 of produce (Bhaga).
  • Sudarshana Lake: Built under Chandragupta (by Pushyagupta) in Girnar — earliest major irrigation work.
  • 6 municipal committees (Megasthenes): Pataliputra's city administration.
  • Punch-marked coins: Karshapana — silver; Mauryan era.
  • Uttarapath highway: Taxila → Pataliputra → Tamralipti.
  • Provincial capitals: Taxila, Ujjain, Tosali (Kalinga), Suvarnagiri (south).

Memory Aids

  • Saptanga: "S-A-J-D-K-D-M" = Svamin, Amatya, Janapada, Durga, Kosha, Danda, Mitra.
  • Land tax: "1/6 = Bhaga."
  • Mandala Theory: "Neighbour = enemy; neighbour's neighbour = friend."
  • Municipal committees: "6 committees × 5 = 30 members" — Megasthenes' Pataliputra.

Exam Traps

  • Arthashastra recommends 1/6 (Bhaga) as standard land tax — NOT 1/4 or 1/10; 1/10 is a sales tax (per Megasthenes).
  • Sudarshana Lake was built by Chandragupta (Mauryan) and repaired by Ashoka (through Tushaspa) — the Rudradaman inscription records Rudradaman's repair later in 2nd c. CE.
  • Rajukas are Ashoka's addition, NOT from the original Arthashastra system.
  • Karshapana = silver punch-marked coins; NOT gold coins (gold coins come later, especially in Gupta era).
