Satavahanas — Deccan/Maharashtra polity & trade PREMAINSMH

Overview

The Satavahana dynasty (c. 230 BCE – 220 CE) was the first great historical dynasty of the Deccan and Maharashtra region, ruling for nearly 450 years from their heartland between the Krishna–Godavari–Sahyadri triangle. They are critical for MPSC: they are the first Maharashtra-connected dynasty with documented rulers, trade networks, Buddhist patronage (Amaravati, Nashik, Karla caves), and a distinctive administrative system. The MAINS/MH tag means Maharashtra-specific questions are common.

In-Depth

Origins and Foundation

The Satavahanas are identified with the Andhras of the Puranas. The dynasty is said to have been founded by Simuka (c. 230 BCE), who overthrew the last Kanva ruler (Susharman) and established control over the Deccan. Their original heartland is debated — either the Pratishtana (Paithan, Aurangabad district, Maharashtra) region or the Andhra region — but Paithan became their principal capital.

Administration

  • Mahayana and Amatya were high officials. Local administration used akaras (districts).
  • A notable feature: Matrilineal naming convention — Satavahana kings bear their mother's name in patronymic form (Gautami-putra = son of Gautami; Vashishti-putra = son of Vashishthi). This was a social/dynastic legitimation device, not necessarily full matrilineality.
  • Land grants to Brahmins and Buddhist monks (viharas) from inscriptions at Nashik, Nasik, and Kanheri.
  • Currency: lead, copper, and potin (lead-tin alloy) coins with ship motifs (especially on Yajna Sri Satakarni's coins), suggesting naval trade.

Economy and Trade — The Dakshinapatha Link

The Satavahanas controlled the Dakshinapatha — the great arterial trade route connecting the northwest (Broach/Bharuch) through the Deccan to the southeast ports (Masulipatnam). This was the spine of:

  • Roman trade: Satavahana ports (Sopara, Kalyan in Maharashtra; Bharuch in Gujarat) exported cotton textiles, spices, gems, pearls, iron to Rome in exchange for gold, wine, olive oil.
  • The Periplus of the Erythraean Sea (1st century CE Greek sailing manual) mentions Barygaza (Bharuch) and the Satavahana king Saraganos (Satakarni) by name — key external primary source.
  • Trade enabled Buddhist patronage and urban growth at Paithan, Nashik, Ter (Osmanabad, Maharashtra).

Buddhist Patronage — Caves and Stupas

The Satavahanas were the greatest patrons of Buddhist rock-cut architecture in the Western Deccan:

  • Karla Caves (Pune district): the chaitya (prayer hall) at Karla is the largest early chaitya in India — a single barrel-vaulted hall with a stupa at the far end, carved c. 1st century CE. Donors include merchant guilds (shrenis) from Nashik, Karhatak (Karha) etc.
  • Bhaja Caves (Pune district): early Buddhist chaitya and viharas; Ashokan-influence visible in chaitya-arch decoration.
  • Nashik Caves (Pandu Lena): 24 rock-cut caves; Gautamiputra Satakarni's mother Gautami Balashri donated a cave (vihara); famous Nashik Prashasti (eulogy) inscribed here.
  • Kanheri Caves (Mumbai): active from 1st century CE; 109 Buddhist caves on Salsette island (now Borivali NP) — post-Satavahana but initiated in this period.
  • Amaravati Stupa (Andhra): major Satavahana monument; elaborate marble medallion carvings; at its peak under Vashishtiputra Pulumayi.

Gautamiputra Satakarni — the Greatest Satavahana

The Nashik Prashasti (rock inscription at Nashik Cave 3), composed by his mother Gautami Balashri, describes him as:

  • Destroyer of the Shakas, Yavanas (Greeks), and Pahlavas.
  • Restorer of the four varnas.
  • Exclusive Brahmin (his mother proclaims him ekabamhana — "the only Brahmin").
  • He reversed the Shaka advance into Satavahana territory and recaptured Nashik from the Western Satrap Nahapana.
  • Nahapana's silver coins (found in thousands, demonetised/cancelled by Gautamiputra) are discovered at Jogalthambi (Nashik district, Maharashtra) — one of the largest ancient coin hoards in India.

Maharashtra Connection

Core Maharashtra topic. The Satavahanas are the first dynasty with a capital in Maharashtra (Paithan/Pratishtana, Aurangabad district). Their legacy includes:

  • Cave complexes at Karla (Pune), Bhaja (Pune), Nashik, Kanheri (Mumbai), Bedse (Pune).
  • Jogalthambi coin hoard (Nashik) — Nahapana's cancelled coins.
  • Ter (Osmanabad/Dharashiv) — important Satavahana trading town in Marathwada.
  • Trade networks established the economic foundations of Maharashtra's ancient cities.
  • Nashik Prashasti is the most detailed Satavahana inscription and a primary source for Maharashtra history.

Key Facts

  • Capital: Pratishtana (Paithan), Aurangabad, Maharashtra.
  • Dynasty duration: c. 230 BCE – 220 CE (~450 years).
  • Greatest king: Gautamiputra Satakarni — destroyed Nahapana's power.
  • Key inscription: Nashik Prashasti — composed by Gautami Balashri.
  • Jogalthambi coin hoard: Nashik district, Maharashtra.
  • External source: Periplus of the Erythraean Sea (1st century CE) mentions Satavahana king.
  • Largest chaitya in early Indian rock-cut architecture: Karla Caves, Pune district.
  • Lead and potin coins with ship motifs (Yajna Sri Satakarni) — evidence of maritime trade.
  • Matrilineal naming: Gautamiputra = son of Gautami.

Memory Aids

  • "Sata = seven + vahana = carrier" — dynasty's name.
  • "P-K-B-N-K" for Maharashtra Satavahana caves: Pandu Lena (Nashik), Karla, Bhaja, Nashik (same), Kanheri.
  • "Gautamiputra killed Nahapana; coins found at Jogalthambi" — pair these always.
  • Periplus = Greek sailing guide = external proof of Satavahana trade.
  • Matrilineal naming: "sons named after mothers" — Gautami → Gautamiputra.

Exam Traps

  • Satavahanas are NOT the same as the later Andhra dynasty — they are identified with Andhras in Puranas but ruled primarily from Maharashtra, not Andhra.
  • Gautamiputra defeated Nahapana (Western Shaka Satrap) — don't confuse with Rudradaman I (Shaka who later defeated a Satavahana).
  • The Nashik Prashasti is NOT authored by Gautamiputra — it was written by his mother Gautami Balashri after his death.
  • Karla Caves are the largest early chaitya — not Kanheri, not Nashik.
