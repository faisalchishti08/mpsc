Peshwa era — Bajirao I, expansion PREMAINSMH

Overview

The Peshwa era (1713–1818) was the period when the hereditary Brahmin Peshwas of the Bhat family became the de facto rulers of the Maratha confederacy, relegating the Chhatrapati to a ceremonial role. Bajirao I (1720–1740) is the greatest Peshwa — a military genius who expanded the Marathas from the Deccan to Delhi and beyond. MPSC (MH tag) asks heavily on this period: Bajirao's campaigns, the confederacy structure, and the British conflict.

In-Depth

Background — Rise of Hereditary Peshwa

Under Shivaji, the Peshwa was one of eight ministers. The transformation:

  • Balaji Vishwanath Bhat (1713–1720): First hereditary Peshwa; based at Pune (Shaniwarwada). He negotiated with the Sayyid Brothers in Delhi and secured Shahu's position; obtained the right to collect Chauth and Sardeshmukhi from the Deccan subas in 1719 — a major political victory.
  • After Balaji Vishwanath's death, the Peshwaship passed to his son — making it hereditary and effectively separating the Maratha state's real power from the Chhatrapati.

Bajirao I (1720–1740) — The Invincible Peshwa

Bajirao Ballal Vishwanath (Bajirao I) assumed the Peshwaship at age 19 (1720) under Chhatrapati Shahu. He is widely regarded as India's greatest cavalry commander and strategic genius of the 18th century:

Strategic vision: Bajirao famously said "Let us strike the withered trunk of the Mughal empire. The branches will fall themselves." He pursued a strategy of rapid cavalry raids (chhabila fauj) — covering hundreds of miles in days, avoiding pitched battles.

Character: A devout Brahmin and gifted administrator; also known for his romance with Mastani (daughter of Rajput chief Chhatar Sal and a Muslim woman) — a legendary affair that caused controversy in Pune's Brahmin establishment.

Death: Died at Raver (near Khandwa, now Madhya Pradesh) on 28 April 1740 — possibly from fever, aged 39.

After Bajirao I — Expansion and Decline

Balaji Bajirao (Nana Saheb, 1740–1761):

  • Expanded Maratha reach into Bengal (1742), Orissa; collected tribute across India.
  • Third Battle of Panipat (1761): Maratha forces under his son Viswasrao and cousin Sadashivrao Bhau were decisively defeated by Ahmad Shah Abdali. Both Viswasrao and Bhau died in battle. Nana Saheb died of shock upon receiving the news (23 June 1761).

Madhavrao I (1761–1772): "Madhavrao the Good"; revived Maratha power after the Panipat disaster; reasserted control over Mysore (Hyder Ali), Rajputs, Jats; generally considered the last great Peshwa.

Narayanrao (1772–1773): Murdered by his uncle Raghunathrao's guard — the infamous "Narayanrao murder."

Later Peshwas: Corruption, civil wars (Nana Fadnavis, Raghunathrao controversy) weakened the Peshwas. Baji Rao II (last Peshwa, 1796–1818): defeated at Battle of Koregaon (1 January 1818) and Battle of Palkhed; surrendered to the British; Pune came under British control 1818 — end of the Peshwa and Maratha confederacy.

Peshwa Capital — Shaniwarwada

Shaniwarwada (built 1732) in Pune was the Peshwa's palace and the nerve-centre of the Maratha confederacy:

  • Built under Bajirao I (completed 1732 by Balaji Vishwanath's successors).
  • Destroyed by fire in 1828 (after British takeover); only outer walls survive.
  • A major heritage site in Pune today.

Maharashtra Connection

Directly central — the Peshwa period is Maharashtra's imperial phase:

  • Pune (Poona) became the de facto capital of a pan-India empire during the Peshwa era.
  • Vasai (Bassein) conquest (1739) — Chimaji Appa's victory over Portuguese in coastal Maharashtra.
  • Shaniwarwada (Pune) — the Peshwa's seat of power.
  • Battle of Koregaon-Bhima (1 January 1818) — near Pune; controversial but historically significant; a small force of Mahar soldiers of the British EIC defeated a larger Peshwa force — commemorated as a Dalit assertion of dignity against Brahmin Peshwa rule; still politically significant in Maharashtra.

Exam Traps

  • Bajirao I ≠ Baji Rao II — Bajirao I (1720–1740) is the great general; Baji Rao II (1796–1818) is the last, defeated Peshwa.
  • Chimaji Appa captured Vasai, NOT Bajirao I himself — Bajirao was campaigning in north India.
  • Madhavrao I (1761–1772) is different from Madhavrao II (Sawai Madhavrao, 1774–1795) — two Madhavaraos; first is the great reviver.
  • Koregaon (1818) is in Pune district — not to be confused with "Battle of Kirkee (1817)" which is also near Pune.
